# Shrikhand
*Maharashtrian and Gujarati Strained Yogurt Dessert with Saffron and Cardamom*

---

## Headnote

Shrikhand begins the day before you eat it. Or at least the night before. Curd (thick, full-fat, homemade if possible) goes into a muslin cloth tied at four corners and hung somewhere it can drip freely. What falls away, drip by drip over five hours or more, is the whey. What remains in the cloth, dense and cream-white, is chakka: the strained curd that is shrikhand's foundation.

This transformation is simple but not trivial. The longer the curd hangs, the denser the chakka and the more intense the final shrikhand. Five hours produces a chakka that is thick and spreadable. Overnight produces one that is almost solid: a dry, tangy block of concentrated milk proteins that, once strained through a fine cloth with sugar, becomes silky and smooth as a rich mousse. Both are correct; overnight chakka produces the more characteristic Maharashtrian shrikhand, where the finished dessert is dry enough to hold a spoon upright.

Sugar dissolves into the strained chakka without any cooking. The natural moisture in the chakka is sufficient to dissolve it completely when strained together through a thin cloth or fine sieve. That straining is the second important step: it removes any remaining lumps and produces the impossibly smooth texture that distinguishes good shrikhand from merely adequate strained yogurt.

Saffron is roasted briefly, powdered, and added with cardamom and nutmeg. The three together give shrikhand its signature warmth: the saffron's honeyed floral character, the cardamom's clean resinous note, the nutmeg's faint earthiness. A fragrance that is immediately and unmistakably Maharashtrian.

Serve cold. Always cold.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

**To make the curd (if not using ready-made)**
- 3 litres whole milk
- 10 g curd (yogurt), as a starter

**For the shrikhand**
- Strained chakka from above (or 1.2–1.5 kg full-fat thick yogurt, strained overnight)
- 10 g sugar (plus more to taste; the quantity listed here is for the curd-making stage. For shrikhand, use approximately 200–300 g sugar, adjusted to the sourness of your chakka)
- 2 g green cardamom powder
- 2 g nutmeg powder
- 1 g saffron, roasted and powdered
- 20 g almonds or cashews, roughly chopped, for garnish

*Note: The recipe data lists 10 g sugar for 3 litres milk. This is for setting the curd. Once chakka is made, sweeten generously to taste (typically 1:3 ratio sugar to chakka by weight).*

---

## Method

**1. Make the curd (if starting from scratch).**
Bring 3 litres of whole milk to a full boil. Cool until the milk is warm to the touch (approximately 40–45°C). Add the curd (10 g) starter and stir gently. Cover and keep in a warm place undisturbed for 8 hours or overnight until set. The curd should be firmly set with no liquid pooling.

**2. Hang the curd to drain.**
Pour the set curd into a large piece of clean muslin cloth. Gather the four corners together, tie securely, and hang over a bowl or over the kitchen sink. Allow to drain for at least 5 hours. Overnight is ideal. Refrigerate the hanging curd if the room temperature is warm. What remains in the cloth (chakka) should be very thick, dense, and almost solid.

**3. Toast and powder the saffron.**
Place the saffron (1 g) in a small dry pan over very low heat for 30–60 seconds until just lightly toasted and fragrant. Remove immediately and crush between your fingers into a fine powder.

**4. Combine chakka with sugar.**
Remove the chakka from the cloth and place in a mixing bowl. Add the sugar (10 g). Mix and press together vigorously. To achieve truly smooth shrikhand, strain the chakka-sugar mixture through a thin cloth or very fine sieve, pressing it through with the back of a spoon. This removes any remaining granularity and produces the characteristic smooth, flowing texture.

**5. Add aromatics.**
Add the powdered saffron, cardamom powder (2 g), and nutmeg powder (2 g). Use a whisk, mixer, or thorough hand mixing to achieve a completely smooth, uniform consistency with an even pale-gold colour from the saffron. Taste and adjust sugar. Shrikhand should be notably sweet to balance the sourness of the chakka.

**6. Chill.**
Cover and refrigerate for at least 1–2 hours until thoroughly cold.

**7. Serve.**
Scoop into small bowls or clay pots. Garnish with chopped almonds or cashews (20 g). Serve immediately, chilled.

## Why This Works

Draining the curd removes the whey, which is mostly water, lactose, and water-soluble proteins. What remains (chakka) is concentrated casein proteins, fat, and some residual lactose. That concentration is what gives shrikhand its density and richness. Undrained yogurt simply lacks the structural protein to give the dessert its body.

The straining of chakka with sugar through a thin cloth is the smoothing step. Chakka, even after long hanging, has a slightly granular texture from the way casein proteins aggregate during draining. Pressing the mixture through a fine cloth or sieve breaks up these aggregates and produces the silky-smooth texture that is the hallmark of well-made shrikhand. Using a mixer or food processor can achieve a similar result but tends to incorporate air and lighten the colour.

Roasting the saffron before dissolving it improves colour and fragrance release. Dry heat makes saffron's primary pigment (crocin) more soluble and converts some of the precursor compounds into their most aromatic forms.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Full-fat curd** is essential. Low-fat yogurt produces a leaner, less satisfying chakka, and the finished shrikhand won't have the smooth, almost mousse-like quality that comes from the fat.

**Homemade curd** produces a fresher-tasting chakka than commercial yogurt. If using commercial Greek-style yogurt, which is already partially strained, the draining time can be reduced to 3–4 hours.

**Saffron** is the aromatic signature, present in small but decisive quantity. Its primary pigment crocin is fat-soluble, which is why it distributes so evenly through the fat-rich chakka. Roasting and powdering before adding ensures maximum colour and fragrance release.

**Sugar content:** Shrikhand is a notably sweet preparation by design. The sweetness balances the sourness of the strained curd. The ratio of sugar to chakka varies between households (typically 1:3 to 1:2) depending on the sourness of your curd. Start with less and add to taste.

## Substitutions and Variations

- **Amrakhand:** The most celebrated variation. Replace all aromatics with 3–4 tablespoons of Alphonso mango pulp (fresh or tinned Kesar or Alphonso). Fold into the chakka with sugar for a shrikhand that is entirely mango-flavoured, a vivid orange-yellow.
- **Without nutmeg:** Omit the nutmeg for a cleaner, more cardamom-forward shrikhand.
- **With rose water:** Add 1 teaspoon of rose water for a more floral, Middle Eastern-inflected variation.
- **With pistachios:** Replace the almond garnish with sliced pistachio for a green-flecked, slightly more assertive garnish.
- **Quick version:** Start with 1 kg of commercial Greek-style yogurt, strain for 3–4 hours, and proceed.

---

## Serving Suggestions

Shrikhand is served chilled as a dessert, most commonly as part of the Maharashtrian and Gujarati puran poli meal at Holi and other festivals. The combination of sweet, rich shrikhand and warm, ghee-drizzled stuffed flatbread is one of the great pairings in Indian festive cooking. It is also served in small clay pots (matkas) at celebrations, or alongside warm puri, or simply on its own. In Maharashtrian households it is the summer sweet par excellence: cool, restrained, entirely satisfying.

---

## Storage and Reheating

Shrikhand keeps refrigerated for up to 4–5 days. It may thicken further as it sits. Stir well before serving and add a small amount of whole milk if needed to loosen slightly. The saffron colour deepens over the first two days. Serve directly from the refrigerator. Do not reheat. Shrikhand is a cold dessert.
