# Dried Shrimp
*Xia Mi · Hay Bee · Dried Prawns*

## Overview

Dried shrimp are tiny, intensely flavored orange-pink shrimp that have been salted and sun-dried to a chewy, concentrated form. They are one of the essential umami-boosting ingredients in Chinese, Southeast Asian, and Brazilian cooking — small in size but enormous in flavor impact. Added to stir-fries, scrambled eggs, soups, and stuffings, they impart a savory, oceanic depth that anchors a dish's flavor profile. Unlike dried scallops, dried shrimp are affordable and used with abandon in everyday Chinese home cooking. The best quality are plump, pink-orange, and fragrant; poor quality are grey, shrunken, and overly fishy.

---

## Cultural History

Dried shrimp have been produced across coastal China, particularly in Guangdong, Fujian, and Zhejiang provinces, for centuries — a preservation technique that allowed the bounty of the sea to reach inland communities and to persist through seasons of poor catch. In Cantonese home cooking, xia mi are a near-universal pantry staple, added to turnip cake (lo bak go), taro cake, and virtually every stir-fried leafy green. Dried shrimp spread globally with Chinese and Vietnamese diaspora communities and became a cornerstone seasoning in Southeast Asian and Latin American cuisines as well. Malaysian hawker cooking, Vietnamese pho condiment tables, and Brazilian shrimp dishes all use dried shrimp as a flavor base.

## Traditional Medicine Perspectives

**Traditional Chinese Medicine:** Dried shrimp share the TCM properties of shrimp generally — warm and sweet-salty, associated with the Kidney and Spleen meridians. They are used to tonify Kidney Yang, strengthen the back, benefit Qi and Blood, and nourish the sea of marrow. They are prescribed for Kidney Yang deficiency, weakness, and impotence. Their warming, tonifying nature makes them appropriate for cold-type deficiency conditions.

---

## Modern Scientific Research

Dried shrimp are a concentrated source of protein, iodine, selenium, calcium, and zinc — all amplified by the drying process. Iodine content is particularly high, reflecting the shrimp's marine origin. Research on shellfish consumption shows associations with improved thyroid function (from iodine), immune support (from zinc and selenium), and cardiovascular benefits. The calcium content of dried shrimp is notably high — traditional communities used them as a calcium source. The salt content should be considered when seasoning dishes that include them.

## Culinary Uses

Add a small handful to stir-fried napa cabbage, leafy greens, or bean sprouts for savory depth. Use in turnip cake, taro cake, and other steamed savory cakes. Add to XO sauce and other compound condiments. Use in fried rice and egg dishes. Grind coarsely and use as a seasoning powder. Add to dumplings and baozi fillings. The soaking liquid is flavorful — add it to the dish.

---

## Preparation Methods

For most applications, soak in warm water for 10–20 minutes to soften and remove excess salt. Drain, reserving the liquid. Chop roughly for stir-fries and stuffings. Smaller, higher-quality dried shrimp can sometimes be used directly without soaking in dishes with sufficient liquid.
