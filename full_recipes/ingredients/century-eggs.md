# Century Eggs
*Pi Dan · Preserved Eggs · Thousand-Year Eggs*

## Overview

Century eggs are duck, chicken, or quail eggs preserved in a strongly alkaline medium — traditionally a mixture of clay, ash, salt, quicklime, and rice hulls — for weeks to months, resulting in a complete transformation of the egg's color, texture, and flavor. The white becomes a dark, translucent, firm jelly; the yolk turns dark green to black and develops a creamy, almost custardy texture with a complex, sulfurous, and deeply savory flavor unlike any other food. The name "thousand-year egg" is poetic hyperbole — the actual process takes weeks. They are one of the most uniquely and proudly Chinese preserved foods, an ingredient that generates intense reactions from first-timers but is beloved by those who grow up eating them.

---

## Cultural History

Century eggs have been produced in China for over five hundred years, with the earliest records from the Ming dynasty referencing a preparation from Hunan province. The alkaline preservation method was likely discovered accidentally — eggs falling into lime-rich soil or ash heaps would have undergone the transformation and been found edible and intriguingly different. The association with the phrase "thousand years" reflects both the dramatic appearance and Chinese culture's appreciation for food products that require patience and transformation. Pi dan is both a beloved everyday ingredient (in congee, with tofu) and a badge of cultural pride for Chinese food lovers worldwide.

## Traditional Medicine Perspectives

**Traditional Chinese Medicine:** Century eggs (Pi Dan) are classified as cool and salty in TCM, associated with the Stomach and Large Intestine meridians. They are used to clear heat, detoxify, cool the Blood, and calm the Liver. They are prescribed for heat conditions, inflammation, hypertension associated with Liver Yang rising, and constipation. The alkaline processing is understood in TCM to alter the egg's properties toward greater heat-clearing potency. They should be eaten in moderation due to high sodium content and lead concerns with traditional preparations.

---

## Modern Scientific Research

Modern century egg production uses sodium hydroxide rather than lime, eliminating the lead contamination associated with traditional preparations. The alkaline environment denatures proteins and converts the liquid egg into a gel through protein cross-linking. The dark color of the yolk comes from iron sulfide (from reaction between iron in the yolk and hydrogen sulfide released by the denatured proteins). Research on century eggs shows they retain significant protein and fat-soluble vitamins. The alkaline pH creates a unique flavor profile from amino acid and sugar reactions.

## Culinary Uses

Serve halved or quartered alongside silken tofu dressed with soy sauce, sesame oil, and ginger for the classic pi dan tofu. Add to congee — a small amount creates extraordinary depth of flavor. Serve with pickled ginger as a palate-awakening starter. Use in cold salads with cucumber, garlic chili sauce, and sesame oil. Serve halved as a garnish and condiment.

---

## Preparation Methods

Century eggs are fully preserved and require no cooking. Peel the outer clay or paste coating (commercial eggs come pre-cleaned). Remove the thin membrane before eating. The interior gel may feel firm or custardy depending on the egg type and production time. For the cleanest cut, use a string or thin wire rather than a knife, or rinse the knife between cuts.
