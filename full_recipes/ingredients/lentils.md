# Lentils
*Dal · Masoor · Lens culinaris*

## Overview

"Lentil" is not a single ingredient. It is a family of legumes, all members of the species Lens culinaris, that vary so significantly in color, size, texture, cooking time, and culinary role that treating them as interchangeable is one of the more common mistakes a cook can make. Red lentils, which are actually orange-pink and sold split, dissolve into a thick puree in 15 to 20 minutes without soaking. French Puy lentils, deep green-black and slate-colored, hold their shape through 30 to 40 minutes of simmering and are the lentil of composed salads and cold preparations. Black beluga lentils look like caviar, cook in about 25 minutes, and remain firm and glossy. Brown lentils, the most common in North America and Europe, cook in 20 to 30 minutes and fall somewhere between: they soften considerably but maintain some shape. Green lentils are similar to brown, slightly firmer.

In South Asia, the word "dal" describes both a specific ingredient (the split and often husked version of any legume) and the concept of a cooked lentil or legume dish. When an Indian recipe calls for masoor dal, it means split red lentils specifically. When a menu offers "dal," it means a cooked, seasoned legume preparation in any form. This dual meaning is worth holding in mind when cooking from South Asian sources.

The unifying characteristic of lentils across their varieties is their accessibility: they are inexpensive, shelf-stable, extraordinarily nutritious, and require no soaking (for the split varieties) to cook in under half an hour. This has made them a cornerstone of working-class and vegetarian cooking across multiple continents for millennia.

## Cultural History

Lentils are among the most ancient cultivated crops in human history. Archaeological sites in the Fertile Crescent, including the Franchthi Cave in Greece and sites in Syria and Jordan, have produced lentil remains dating to 11,000 BCE. Lentils appear in the Bible: Esau trades his birthright for a bowl of red lentil pottage. They were a staple of ancient Egyptian cooking, fed to pyramid builders, found in excavated tombs. Roman soldiers carried dried lentils as field rations. In their portability, shelf stability, and nutritional density, lentils were a kind of ancient energy bar.

In India, lentils arrived with or alongside early agricultural development, and dal in its various forms became the protein backbone of a vegetarian culture spanning a billion people across two millennia. Dal is not merely a dish in the Indian context; it is a meal structure. The classic formula of dal, rice or roti, and vegetable side dish provides a complete amino acid profile, iron, complex carbohydrates, and fiber in a single low-cost meal. For most of Indian history, and for much of rural India today, this triad is the daily meal.

In the Middle East, lentils never left the table. Mujaddara, the Syrian and Lebanese preparation of lentils and rice topped with caramelized onions, is a dish of profound age and simplicity. It appears in Arab cookbooks dating to the 13th century and is almost certainly much older. In Egypt, koshari, the national street food, layers lentils with pasta, rice, and a spiced tomato sauce in a combination that should not work and does spectacularly. In Turkey, red lentil soup seasoned with cumin and a butter-paprika drizzle is what is served to a sick person, to a cold person, and at the start of nearly every sit-down meal in Anatolia.

## Traditional Medicine Perspectives

**Ayurveda:** In Ayurvedic thought, different lentils have different properties, and they are not treated as a category. Red lentils (masoor) are considered slightly warming and moderately easy to digest. Split and husked moong dal (yellow moong) is considered the most sattvic and easily digestible of all legumes. Brown and green lentils, which retain their husk, are considered more Vata-aggravating and are not recommended for those with weak digestion. The standard Ayurvedic prescription for digestive support is to always cook lentils with digestive spices: cumin, ginger, turmeric, and asafoetida.

**Traditional Chinese Medicine:** Lentils are classified as sweet in taste and neutral in temperature, supporting Spleen and Stomach function. They are considered nourishing to Qi and Blood, and are recommended for fatigue, weakness, and poor appetite. Like Ayurveda, TCM emphasizes proper cooking and seasoning to support digestibility.

## Modern Scientific Research

Lentils sit at the top of the plant-based protein league table. A cooked cup provides roughly 18 grams of protein, 16 grams of fiber, and significant amounts of folate, iron, manganese, and B vitamins, at a cost of under a dollar in most markets. Their fiber is split between soluble fiber (which lowers LDL cholesterol and feeds gut microbiota) and insoluble fiber (which supports bowel regularity). Studies from the Canadian Lentil Clinical Trial program have produced consistent evidence that regular lentil consumption reduces glycemic response to subsequent meals, an effect called the "second meal effect," where resistant starches and fiber slow glucose absorption not just immediately after eating lentils but hours later.

The iron in lentils is non-heme iron, which is less bioavailable than heme iron from animal sources, but pairing lentils with a source of vitamin C (tomatoes, lemon juice, bell peppers) significantly increases absorption. This combination, acidulated dal with tomatoes, is embedded in South Asian cooking long before anyone understood the biochemistry.

## Culinary Uses

Split red lentils (masoor dal) are the fastest-cooking and most dissolving variety, ideal for soups and thickened dals where a smooth, porridge-like texture is desired. They require no soaking and are ready in 15 to 20 minutes on the stovetop. They cannot hold their shape and should not be used in salads or preparations where lentil integrity matters.

French Puy lentils and black beluga lentils are the salad lentils. They hold their shape, have a pleasant peppery flavor, and pair well with vinaigrette, roasted vegetables, and soft-boiled eggs. They should be cooked in salted water (or stock) and rinsed after cooking if serving cold. Brown and green lentils fall in the middle: they can make a decent soup or a thick, chunky dal, but they will partially break down.

## Preparation Methods

**Split red lentils (masoor/moong dal):** Rinse thoroughly until water runs clear. Add to 2.5 to 3 parts water. Bring to a boil, skim any foam, then simmer 15 to 20 minutes until completely soft. Season with salt at the end. Finish with a tadka (tempering) of whole spices fried in ghee or oil.

**Whole brown/green lentils:** Rinse. No soaking required. Simmer in well-salted water for 25 to 35 minutes until tender but holding shape. Drain immediately to prevent overcooking.

**French Puy/beluga lentils:** Rinse. Simmer in lightly salted water or stock for 25 to 35 minutes. These can be slightly undercooked (al dente) for salad use. Dress while still warm for best flavor absorption.

**Tadka (tempering) for Indian dal:** Heat ghee in a small pan until shimmering. Add whole cumin seeds and let sputter 30 seconds. Add dried chilies, a pinch of asafoetida, minced garlic, and let cook until garlic is golden. Pour the entire hot mixture over the cooked dal. Stir to combine.
