# Arrowhead Root
*Ci Gu · Chinese Arrowhead*

## Overview

Arrowhead root is a small, smooth-skinned aquatic tuber with arrow-shaped leaves, harvested in late autumn and winter from shallow ponds and paddy fields. The corm, which resembles a smooth water chestnut in shape, has a mildly starchy, slightly sweet flavor with a clean, earthy quality reminiscent of potato. It is a seasonal delicacy in Shanghainese and Cantonese cooking — available only in the colder months — and is one of the traditional New Year ingredients of the Yangtze Delta. Slow-braised with pork fat and soy sauce until caramelized, it achieves a depth of flavor that is extraordinary for such a humble root.

---

## Cultural History

Arrowhead root has been harvested and eaten in China for over two thousand years, growing wild in rice paddies and wetlands across the Yangtze Delta and Pearl River Delta. In Shanghai and the surrounding Jiangnan region, it is a deeply cherished seasonal ingredient associated with the lunar New Year and cold-weather cooking. The traditional Shanghainese preparation — ci gu braised slowly with pork belly fat, soy sauce, sugar, and Shaoxing wine until deeply glossy — is considered a defining regional dish. The seasonal scarcity of arrowhead root contributes to its cultural significance as a winter and New Year ingredient.

---

## Traditional Medicine Perspectives

**Traditional Chinese Medicine:** Arrowhead (Ci Gu) is classified as neutral to slightly cool and sweet in TCM, associated with the Spleen and Stomach meridians. It is used to strengthen the Spleen, promote digestion, resolve phlegm and nodules, detoxify, and promote urination. It is considered a nourishing, digestive food appropriate for most constitutions. The cool nature of the raw form can be tempered by braising with warming ingredients like pork and Shaoxing wine.

---

## Modern Scientific Research

Arrowhead root is a source of complex carbohydrates, potassium, phosphorus, and B vitamins. It contains flavonoids and polyphenols with antioxidant properties. Research on similar aquatic tubers shows potential benefits for blood sugar regulation from the fiber and complex starch content. As a seasonal root vegetable in traditional Chinese diet culture, it exemplifies the principle of eating in alignment with seasons and locality.

## Culinary Uses

Braise whole with pork belly, soy sauce, Shaoxing wine, and rock sugar for the classic Shanghainese preparation — long, slow cooking transforms them into deeply savory, glazed, intensely flavored bites. Stir-fry sliced with garlic and oyster sauce for a simpler preparation. Add to hot pots and stews. Boil until tender and serve simply dressed. The braised preparation is the finest use of this ingredient.

---

## Preparation Methods

Peel the smooth outer skin with a knife or peeler — the skin is thin and slides off easily. Cut off both ends. Leave whole for braising (they hold their shape well). Slice for stir-fries. Arrowhead root discolors quickly after peeling — keep in water until ready to cook. They are typically available at Chinese markets only in autumn and winter.
