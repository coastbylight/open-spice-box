# Fermented Tofu
*Doufu Ru · Sufu · Nan Ru*

## Overview

Fermented tofu is tofu that has been inoculated with molds, aged in brine, and transformed into a soft, intensely savory, pungent paste-like substance that bears the same relationship to plain tofu as aged cheese does to fresh milk. Often called "Chinese cheese" by those encountering it for the first time, it comes in two primary forms: white fermented tofu (bai furu), which is creamy and intensely salty-savory; and red fermented tofu (nan ru or hong furu), colored and flavored by red fermented rice (hongqu) in the brine, with a sweeter, more complex flavor. A single cube flavors an entire dish.

---

## Cultural History

Fermented tofu has been produced in China for over a thousand years, with the earliest records dating to the Tang dynasty. The fermentation technique likely developed as a preservation method for tofu, which has a very short shelf life. Over centuries, fermented tofu evolved from a practical preservation food to a celebrated condiment with regional variations across China. In Cantonese cooking, the combination of fermented white tofu with garlic in stir-fried water spinach (ong choy) is one of the most beloved street food and restaurant preparations. Red fermented tofu (nan ru) from Guangdong is one of the most prized regional varieties, used to marinate roasted meats and to braise pork belly.

## Traditional Medicine Perspectives

**Traditional Chinese Medicine:** Fermented tofu shares properties with plain tofu (cool and sweet, tonifying Yin and clearing heat) but the fermentation process adds warmth and reduces the cooling nature somewhat. It is associated with the Spleen, Stomach, and Lung meridians and is used to strengthen the Spleen, clear heat, and tonify Qi. The active molds and bacteria involved in fermentation are understood in TCM to increase digestibility and the bioavailability of nutrients.

---

## Modern Scientific Research

Fermented tofu contains isoflavones from soybeans in forms that are more bioavailable than in unfermented tofu — fermentation hydrolyzes isoflavone glycosides into aglycones (genistein, daidzein), which are absorbed more readily. Research on fermented soy isoflavones shows potential benefits for bone density, cardiovascular health, and hormone-related cancers. Fermented tofu contains B12 precursors and higher levels of bioavailable minerals than plain tofu. The beneficial molds used in fermentation (Mucor, Actinomucor) may produce enzymes with probiotic-like effects.

## Culinary Uses

Use white fermented tofu in the sauce for stir-fried water spinach — one cube, mashed and dissolved in the wok with garlic and a little water, creates the definitive Cantonese preparation. Use red fermented tofu as a marinade for roasted pork (char siu) and suckling pig. Braise pork belly with red fermented tofu and Shaoxing wine. Spread white fermented tofu on congee or steamed rice as a condiment. Use as a dipping sauce for hot pot.

---

## Preparation Methods

Remove cubes from the jar with a clean spoon. Mash with a fork or the back of a spoon before using in sauces — this helps integrate it evenly. For stir-fries, add to the hot wok and break up with a spatula before adding other ingredients. Start with one cube per dish and adjust to taste — fermented tofu is intensely salty and pungent.
