# Preserved Mustard Greens
*Mei Cai · Zha Cai · Xue Cai · Ya Cai · Suan Cai*

## Overview

Preserved mustard greens are not one thing but a family of essential Chinese preserved vegetables — each regional variety distinct in character, yet all sharing the deep, fermented complexity that comes from salting and transforming Brassica juncea. Mei cai (Guangdong): dried, sweet-salty preserved greens that enrich Hakka braised pork belly into one of China's great dishes. Zha cai (Sichuan): a spicy, crunchy preserved mustard stem used in noodle soups. Xue cai (Ningbo/Shanghai): salty, tangy preserved greens used in rice cakes and noodle dishes. Ya cai (Yibin, Sichuan): sweetly fermented preserved sprouts used in dan dan noodles. Suan cai (Northeast): lactic-fermented sour pickled cabbage used in Sichuan fish soup.

---

## Cultural History

The preservation of mustard greens through salting and fermentation has been practiced in China for over two thousand years, with each major agricultural region developing its own distinctive style. Mei cai (dried preserved mustard from Guangdong) is inseparable from Hakka identity — the braised pork dish mei cai kou rou is considered the heart of Hakka cuisine, a dish that sustained generations of Hakka migrants. Ya cai from Yibin, Sichuan became famous as the defining garnish of dan dan noodles and Yibin-style noodles. Suan cai (lactic-fermented sour mustard from Northeast China and Sichuan) provides the characteristic sour-spicy note to Sichuan suan cai fish — one of the most copied dishes in modern Chinese cooking.

## Traditional Medicine Perspectives

**Traditional Chinese Medicine:** Preserved mustard greens are classified as warm and acrid-salty in TCM, reflecting both the properties of the original mustard (warm and dispersing) and the fermentation process (which adds sourness and probiotic benefit). They are associated with the Lung, Liver, and Stomach meridians. Used to warm the Lung and dissolve phlegm, stimulate digestion, and move stagnant Qi. The fermentation is understood to add digestive-supporting and detoxifying properties beyond those of the fresh vegetable.

---

## Modern Scientific Research

Preserved mustard greens are lactic acid fermented foods containing beneficial Lactobacillus species, organic acids, and bioactive compounds. Research on Chinese pickled vegetables shows significant antioxidant activity and potential anti-tumor properties from isothiocyanate precursors. The fermentation process increases bioavailability of iron, calcium, and other minerals. Zha cai consumption has been studied for potential blood pressure-reducing effects. All preserved mustard greens are high in sodium — a consideration for those monitoring salt intake.

---

## Culinary Uses

Mei cai: soak, squeeze dry, and layer with pork belly for the definitive Hakka braise. Zha cai: rinse, slice thinly, and add to noodle soups and cold dishes. Xue cai: use directly (rinsed if very salty) in stir-fried rice cakes and noodle soups. Ya cai: add a spoonful as a garnish and flavoring for dan dan noodles and Yibin-style cold noodles. Suan cai: add to fish soups for the characteristic sour note.

---

## Preparation Methods

Rinse all preserved mustard greens under cold water before use to remove excess surface salt. Taste before cooking and adjust seasoning of the dish accordingly. Mei cai requires soaking 30 minutes then squeezing. Zha cai is typically sold in vacuum packs or cans — rinse and dry before slicing. Store opened packages refrigerated.
