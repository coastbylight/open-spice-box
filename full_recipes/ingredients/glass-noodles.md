# Glass Noodles
*Fen Si · Cellophane Noodles · Bean Thread Noodles*

## Overview

Glass noodles — also called cellophane noodles, bean thread noodles, or fen si — are thin, translucent noodles made from mung bean starch (or other starches, including sweet potato in the Korean dangmyeon). They are almost completely flavorless on their own, which is their greatest culinary strength: they absorb the flavors of the sauce or broth around them with extraordinary thoroughness, becoming a perfect vehicle for other flavors. When cooked, they are slippery, slightly gelatinous, and pleasantly chewy — a unique texture unlike wheat or rice noodles. They appear in hot pots, braised preparations, cold salads, and some of China's most famous dishes.

---

## Cultural History

Glass noodles made from mung bean starch have been produced in China for centuries, with the technique developed as a way to transform the starch of mung beans into a shelf-stable, versatile food. They became particularly central to Sichuan cooking — "ants climbing a tree" (ma yi shang shu), where minced pork clings to the noodles like ants on a branch, is one of Sichuan's most famous home-cooking dishes. Korean dangmyeon (made from sweet potato starch) became the foundation of japchae, Korea's beloved stir-fried noodle dish. In Thai cooking, woon sen (glass noodles) appear in salads and spring rolls. The virtually universal presence of glass noodles across East and Southeast Asian cuisines reflects their versatility and convenience.

## Traditional Medicine Perspectives

**Traditional Chinese Medicine:** Glass noodles (Fen Si) are classified as cool and sweet in TCM, associated with the Stomach and Large Intestine. Made from mung beans (which are strongly cooling in TCM), glass noodles are used to clear summer heat, quench thirst, and cool the Blood. They are considered appropriate for hot weather consumption and for people with heat conditions. The neutral, non-stimulating nature makes them appropriate for most constitutions.

---

## Modern Scientific Research

Glass noodles are composed almost entirely of starch and have a very low protein and fat content. Mung bean starch noodles have a lower glycemic index than wheat noodles due to the resistant starch content of the mung bean starch. Korean sweet potato starch noodles (dangmyeon) contain anthocyanins from the purple sweet potato variety. Research on mung bean starch shows potential blood glucose-moderating effects. Glass noodles are naturally gluten-free.

## Culinary Uses

Add dried glass noodles directly to hot pots and soups without pre-soaking — they hydrate quickly in the hot liquid. Soak briefly before stir-frying (they continue to absorb liquid rapidly, so undercook slightly). Use in japchae with sesame oil, soy sauce, and vegetables. Make ants climbing a tree by cooking soaked glass noodles with spicy minced pork and doubanjiang. Use soaked and rinsed in cold salads dressed with lime juice, fish sauce, and chili.

---

## Preparation Methods

Soak in cold water for 10–15 minutes until pliable. They will continue absorbing liquid during cooking — add them near the end of hot pot and soup preparations. For stir-fries: soak, drain, and cut with scissors into manageable lengths before cooking. Glass noodles can go from pleasantly chewy to mushy quickly — watch carefully.
