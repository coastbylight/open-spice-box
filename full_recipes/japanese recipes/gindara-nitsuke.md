# Gindara Nitsuke (銀ダラ煮付け)

*Black cod braised in sake, mirin, and soy sauce until glazed, the Japanese technique that makes any fish extraordinary*

---

## Headnote

Nitsuke is the Japanese technique of braising fish in a sweet-savory liquid of sake, mirin, soy sauce, and sugar. It is one of the foundational methods of washoku, the traditional Japanese cuisine recognized by UNESCO, and it represents a philosophy of cooking that prizes restraint over force. The fish is never seared, never subjected to high heat. Instead, it is gently simmered in an aromatic broth and basted constantly until the liquid reduces to a glossy glaze that coats each fillet.

Black cod, known in Japanese as gindara (silver cod), is perhaps the ideal fish for this treatment. Its flesh is extraordinarily rich, with a fat content that can exceed 15%, giving it a buttery, almost custard-like texture when cooked. The gentle heat of nitsuke preserves that delicate texture in a way that grilling or roasting cannot. The fish practically melts under your chopsticks, each flake saturated with the sweet-salty braising liquid.

The technique itself is deceptively simple. You bring the braising liquid to a boil, slip the fish in, cover with a drop lid (otoshibuta), and baste frequently for about ten minutes. The drop lid is a smaller lid that sits directly on the surface of the liquid, trapping steam against the fish while still allowing evaporation around the edges. This dual action cooks the fish from below and above simultaneously while concentrating the sauce. If you do not have a traditional wooden drop lid, a circle of aluminum foil with a few small holes poked through works perfectly.

The finished dish is remarkably elegant for something so quick and easy. The fish glistens with a dark amber glaze, a few slices of ginger rest alongside for contrast, and the sauce pools on the plate like a thin, savory syrup. Served with steamed rice and a simple vegetable, it is the kind of meal that makes you wonder why you would ever cook fish any other way.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the braising liquid
- 60 ml sake (1/4 cup)
- 60 ml mirin (1/4 cup)
- 60 ml soy sauce (1/4 cup)
- 30 g sugar (2 tablespoons)

### For the fish
- 400 g black cod (sablefish) fillets, skin on, cut into 2 portions
- 10 g fresh ginger, peeled and sliced into thin rounds

---

## Method

1. If the fish has any scales remaining, scrape them off with the back of a knife. Check for pin bones and remove them with tweezers. Pat the fillets dry with paper towels.

2. Combine the sake, mirin, soy sauce, and sugar in a shallow saucepan or skillet that is wide enough to hold the fish in a single layer. Bring the mixture to a boil over medium-high heat, stirring until the sugar dissolves. Let it boil for about 30 seconds to cook off some of the alcohol.

3. Reduce the heat to medium. Scatter the ginger slices in the liquid. Gently lower the fish fillets into the braising liquid, skin side up. Spoon the liquid over the top of the fillets several times.

4. Place a drop lid (otoshibuta) or a circle of aluminum foil with a few small holes directly on the surface of the liquid. Reduce the heat to medium-low so the liquid maintains a gentle simmer.

5. Braise for about 4 to 5 minutes, lifting the drop lid occasionally to spoon the liquid over the fish. The liquid should be bubbling gently but not vigorously.

6. Carefully flip the fillets so the skin side is down. Replace the drop lid and continue braising for another 4 to 5 minutes, basting every minute or so. The liquid will reduce and thicken into a glossy sauce. The fish is done when it flakes easily and the sauce coats the back of a spoon.

7. Remove the drop lid. Transfer the fish to serving plates, skin side up. Spoon the reduced sauce and ginger slices over and around the fish. Serve immediately.

## Why This Works

The braising liquid is built on the classic Japanese flavor base of sake, mirin, and soy sauce in equal parts, with sugar added for additional sweetness and body. Sake contributes alcohol that helps extract flavor compounds from the fish and carries aroma. Mirin, a sweet rice wine, adds a clean sweetness and a distinctive sheen to the finished sauce. Soy sauce provides salt, umami, and the dark color that defines the dish.

The drop lid is the key technique in nitsuke. By sitting directly on the surface of the liquid, it creates a closed environment where steam constantly bathes the top of the fish, cooking it evenly from all sides. Simultaneously, the gap between the drop lid and the pan walls allows moisture to escape, concentrating the braising liquid into a thick, syrupy glaze. Without the drop lid, the top of the fish dries out while the sauce remains too thin.

Black cod's high fat content means it stays moist even as the braising liquid reduces around it. Leaner fish can become dry with this technique if overcooked, but black cod is forgiving. The fat also carries the flavors of the soy and mirin deep into the flesh, making each bite intensely savory.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Black cod (sablefish):** One of the richest sources of omega-3 fatty acids among commercially available fish, with approximately 1,500 mg of combined EPA and DHA per 100 g serving. It also provides excellent amounts of vitamin D, selenium, and vitamin B12. Black cod is a deep-water species that generally contains lower mercury levels than many predatory fish, making it a recommended choice for regular consumption.

**Sake:** Brewed from rice, sake contains amino acids that contribute to umami and help tenderize protein. The alcohol evaporates during cooking, leaving behind complex flavor compounds that enhance the overall taste of the braising liquid.

**Mirin:** True mirin (hon-mirin) is a naturally sweet rice wine with about 14% alcohol and significant sugar content from the rice fermentation process. It adds a distinctive luster to sauces that sugar alone cannot replicate. The amino acids in mirin also participate in Maillard reactions during cooking, contributing to the golden color of the finished glaze.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Fish alternatives:** Any rich, fatty fish works well with nitsuke. Salmon, sea bream (tai), and flounder (karei) are traditional choices. Leaner fish like sea bass or snapper can be used but require more careful timing to avoid overcooking.

**Without sake:** Dry white wine can substitute in a pinch, though the flavor will differ. For an alcohol-free version, use dashi stock in place of sake and increase the sugar slightly.

**Without mirin:** A combination of 1 tablespoon sugar and 1 tablespoon sake can approximate mirin's sweetness, though the characteristic sheen will be less pronounced.

**Ginger variations:** Some preparations add a few slices of burdock root or a piece of kombu to the braising liquid for additional umami depth.

---

## Serving Suggestions

Gindara nitsuke is traditionally served as the main protein in a Japanese ichiju-sansai (one soup, three sides) meal. Pair it with a bowl of steamed white rice, [miso-shiru](/recipes/miso-shiru), and a vegetable side like [kinpira-gobo](/recipes/kinpira-gobo) or steamed greens with a drizzle of soy sauce. The rich, sweet-savory fish benefits from the contrast of something clean and slightly bitter, like blanched spinach dressed in dashi and soy sauce (ohitashi). A small dish of pickled vegetables provides the acidic counterpoint that rounds out the meal.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store in an airtight container with the braising liquid for up to 2 days. The fish will continue to absorb the sauce as it rests, becoming more deeply flavored.

**Reheating:** Reheat gently in a saucepan over low heat, spooning the sauce over the fish as it warms. Avoid high heat, which will cause the delicate flesh to break apart. The fish is also pleasant served at room temperature, a common practice in Japanese bento boxes.

**Freezing:** Not recommended. The delicate texture of black cod deteriorates significantly when frozen and thawed after braising.
