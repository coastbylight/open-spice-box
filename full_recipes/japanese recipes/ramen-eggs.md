# Ramen Eggs (味付け玉子)

*Soft-boiled eggs with jammy yolks marinated overnight in a soy-mirin-sake sauce, the essential ramen topping*

---

## Headnote

Ajitsuke tamago, the marinated soft-boiled egg that sits halved on top of a bowl of ramen, is one of those things that looks deceptively simple. It is an egg. It has been boiled and soaked in soy sauce. And yet the difference between a properly made ramen egg and a regular soft-boiled egg is the difference between a good bowl of ramen and a great one. The yolk should be jammy, somewhere between liquid and set, with a deep golden color that looks almost like custard. The white should be stained a translucent amber-brown from the marinade, savory all the way through.

The technique is precise but not difficult. Seven minutes in boiling water, then straight into ice water to stop the cooking. The timing produces a yolk that is just set around the edges but still creamy and slightly flowing in the center. The marinade is four ingredients: soy sauce, mirin, sake, and sugar, simmered briefly to cook off the alcohol and dissolve the sugar, then cooled completely before the peeled eggs go in. The eggs sit in the marinade overnight, and by morning they have absorbed the salty-sweet liquid through the white into the yolk.

The trick that makes this work at home is using a plastic bag instead of a container. A bag requires far less marinade to fully submerge the eggs, and you can press out the air to keep them surrounded. Four eggs, a quarter cup each of soy sauce, mirin, and sake, and a teaspoon of sugar. That is the entire recipe. The overnight wait is the hardest part.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the marinade
- 60 ml soy sauce (1/4 cup)
- 60 ml mirin (1/4 cup)
- 60 ml sake (1/4 cup, or water if avoiding alcohol)
- 1 tsp sugar

### For the eggs
- 4 large eggs, refrigerated

---

## Method

1. Make the marinade. Combine the soy sauce, mirin, sake, and sugar in a small saucepan. Whisk to dissolve the sugar. Bring to a boil, reduce heat, and simmer for 1 minute. Turn off the heat and set aside to cool completely. The marinade must be cool before the eggs go in, or the residual heat will overcook the yolks.

2. Boil the eggs. Add 4 cups of water to a saucepan deep enough to cover the eggs by about an inch. Bring to a full rolling boil. Gently lower the eggs straight from the refrigerator into the boiling water one at a time using a ladle or slotted spoon. Start a 7-minute timer when the first egg goes in. Keep the water at a gentle simmer. During the first 3 minutes, gently rotate the eggs with chopsticks to help center the yolks.

3. Ice bath. After exactly 7 minutes, immediately remove the eggs and submerge them in a bowl of ice water. Let them sit for 15 minutes until completely cooled. This stops the cooking and makes peeling easier.

4. Peel the eggs. Gently tap the wide bottom end of each egg to crack the shell. Peel from bottom to top, dipping the egg in the ice water to help the shell release. Work carefully. The yolk is still soft.

5. Marinate overnight. Place the peeled eggs in a plastic bag. Pour in the cooled marinade. Press out as much air as possible and seal the bag with a clip or rubber band so the eggs are fully submerged. Place the bag in a bowl (in case of leaks) and refrigerate for at least 8 hours or overnight.

6. Serve. Remove the eggs from the marinade. To cut cleanly, use a piece of unflavored dental floss, fishing line, or a thin wire rather than a knife, which tends to drag the yolk. Serve halved, warm or cold, on top of ramen, in bento boxes, or as a snack.

## Why This Works

The 7-minute boil from cold (refrigerator-temperature eggs into already boiling water) is the timing that produces the ideal ramen egg yolk: set around the edges, jammy and flowing in the center, deep golden rather than pale yellow. Six minutes gives a runnier yolk. Eight to nine minutes gives a firmer one. The immediate ice bath is what locks in the texture by stopping the carryover cooking that would continue to set the yolk.

Starting from boiling water rather than cold water gives consistent results regardless of the pot size or stove power. Every egg gets the same treatment. Rotating the eggs during the first 3 minutes of cooking helps the yolk settle in the center of the white rather than drifting to one side.

The marinade ratio of equal parts soy sauce, mirin, and sake produces a balanced salty-sweet flavor that penetrates the egg white over 8 to 12 hours. The mirin contributes sweetness and a glossy sheen. The sake adds depth and helps carry the soy flavor into the egg. The sugar is a small addition that rounds out any sharpness. Marinating longer than 24 hours makes the eggs too salty.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Eggs:** Each provides about 6 grams of complete protein, plus choline, vitamin D, and B12. The soft-boiled yolk retains more heat-sensitive nutrients than a fully hard-boiled yolk.

**Soy sauce:** Naturally fermented soy sauce provides glutamate (umami), amino acids, and antioxidants from the fermentation process. It penetrates the porous egg white during marination, seasoning the egg from the outside in.

**Mirin:** A sweet Japanese rice wine used exclusively for cooking. The sugars in mirin caramelize slightly during the brief simmer and contribute the characteristic glossy sheen on the marinated egg white.

---

## Substitutions and Variations

**Cook time:** 6 to 6.5 minutes for runnier yolks. 8 to 9 minutes for firmer yolks. 7 minutes is the sweet spot for jammy.

**Without sake:** Use water. The eggs will be slightly less complex in flavor but still very good.

**Without mirin:** Use 1 tablespoon of sugar dissolved in 1/4 cup of water. Not identical but workable.

**Spicy version:** Add a teaspoon of chili flakes or a splash of chili oil to the marinade for a spicy ramen egg.

---

## Serving Suggestions

The obvious home for these is on top of a bowl of ramen, alongside [Chashu Pork](/recipes/chashu-pork) and nori. They are also excellent in bento boxes, sliced over rice bowls, or eaten on their own as a snack with a sprinkle of furikake and shichimi togarashi.

---

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Remove from the marinade before storing. Soft-boiled ramen eggs keep for 3 to 4 days refrigerated. Do not marinate longer than 24 hours or they become too salty.

**Serving temperature:** Serve cold, at room temperature, or warm (soak the eggs in warm water for a few minutes before halving).

**Freezing:** Not recommended. The egg white becomes rubbery.

**Marinade:** Do not reuse the marinade with new eggs for food safety. Repurpose it in stir-fries or simmered dishes.
