# Asian Coleslaw with Miso-Ginger Dressing

*Shredded green and red cabbage, carrots, radishes, and scallions tossed in a creamy miso-ginger dressing with rice vinegar and lemon*

---

## Headnote

There is a version of coleslaw that does not involve mayonnaise pooling at the bottom of a deli container, and this is it. The dressing is built on white miso, which brings a salty, fermented earthiness that replaces the richness of mayo without the heaviness. Rice vinegar and lemon juice provide the acid. Grated ginger adds a sharpness that wakes up the entire bowl. Oil emulsified into the mixture in a slow stream produces a dressing that is creamy and clingy without being thick.

The slaw itself is straightforward: equal parts green and red cabbage for color and crunch, shredded carrots for sweetness, sliced radishes for peppery bite, and julienned scallions for sharpness. It comes together in about fifteen minutes and holds up well in the fridge, which makes it the kind of side dish you can make Sunday afternoon and eat all week. The dressing makes about twice what you need for the slaw, which is intentional. Keep the extra in the fridge for rice noodle salads, as a dip for raw vegetables, or spooned over grilled fish.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the miso-ginger dressing
- 60 ml rice vinegar (unseasoned)
- 3 tbsp white miso (shiro miso)
- 1 tbsp mayonnaise
- 1 tbsp fresh lemon juice
- 1 tsp fresh ginger, finely grated
- 1 pinch sugar
- 180 ml grapeseed or vegetable oil
- Salt and freshly ground black pepper to taste

### For the coleslaw
- 1/2 small green cabbage, finely shredded (about 4 cups)
- 1/2 small red cabbage, finely shredded (about 4 cups)
- 4 medium carrots, shredded
- 4 radishes, shredded or thinly sliced
- 3 large scallions, white and light green parts only, julienned

---

## Method

1. Make the dressing. Combine the rice vinegar, miso, mayonnaise, lemon juice, ginger, and sugar in a food processor or blender. Process until completely smooth. With the machine running, add the oil in a slow, steady stream until the dressing is thick and emulsified. Season with salt and pepper. This makes about 1 1/2 cups of dressing.

2. Dress the slaw. Pour about 1/2 cup of the dressing into a large bowl. Refrigerate the rest for another use. Add the green and red cabbage, carrots, radishes, and scallions. Toss thoroughly to coat every strand. Taste and add more dressing if you prefer a wetter slaw.

3. Serve. Serve slightly chilled or at room temperature. The slaw holds its crunch for several hours after dressing.

## Why This Works

White miso is a fermented paste that provides glutamate (umami), salt, and a creamy body that replaces the structural role of mayonnaise in traditional coleslaw. The fermentation produces complex flavor compounds that give the dressing a depth that vinaigrettes lack. The small amount of mayonnaise adds just enough fat to stabilize the emulsion without making the dressing heavy.

Emulsifying the oil into the miso-vinegar base in a slow stream creates a stable, creamy dressing that clings to the shredded vegetables rather than sliding off. The technique is identical to making a vinaigrette or aioli, and the result has a similar consistency.

The combination of green and red cabbage provides both visual contrast and a difference in texture. Green cabbage is crunchier and more neutral. Red cabbage is slightly tougher and has a faintly peppery flavor that stands up to the dressing. Together they produce a slaw that looks vivid and eats well.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**White miso:** A fermented paste of soybeans and rice koji, aged for a shorter period than darker misos. It contains probiotics (Lactobacillus and other beneficial bacteria), B vitamins, and minerals from the fermentation process. In Japanese dietary tradition, miso is consumed daily as part of a health-supporting diet, associated in epidemiological research with lower rates of certain cancers and cardiovascular disease.

**Cabbage:** Both green and red varieties are cruciferous vegetables rich in vitamin C, vitamin K, and fiber. Red cabbage contains anthocyanins, the same antioxidant pigments found in blueberries, which give it its purple color.

**Ginger:** Fresh grated ginger provides gingerols with documented anti-inflammatory and digestive properties. In the dressing, it adds a brightness that prevents the miso from tasting heavy.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Napa cabbage:** Produces a softer, more delicate slaw. Reduce the dressing amount slightly as napa cabbage is more absorbent.

**Sesame seeds:** A tablespoon of toasted sesame seeds scattered over the top adds crunch and nuttiness.

**Edamame or snap peas:** Blanched edamame or thinly sliced sugar snap peas add protein and a pop of green.

**Without mayonnaise:** Replace the tablespoon of mayo with an extra tablespoon of miso. The dressing will be slightly thinner but still good.

**As a rice noodle salad:** Toss the extra dressing with cooked and cooled rice vermicelli, shredded vegetables, and fresh herbs for a cold noodle salad.

## Serving Suggestions

This coleslaw sits happily next to almost anything grilled: chicken, pork, fish, or tofu. It is excellent alongside [Japanese Beef Tongue](/recipes/japanese-beef-tongue), pulled pork, or fish tacos. It also works as part of a ramen spread, served alongside [Ramen Eggs](/recipes/ramen-eggs) and [Chashu Pork](/recipes/chashu-pork).

---

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** The dressed slaw keeps for up to 2 days, though it softens as it sits. The undressed vegetables and dressing stored separately will keep for up to 5 days. The dressing alone keeps for up to 1 week.

**Freezing:** Not recommended. Cabbage becomes waterlogged when frozen.
