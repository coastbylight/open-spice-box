# Chashu Pork (チャーシュー)

*Pork belly simmered low and slow in soy sauce, sake, ginger, and garlic until meltingly tender, the essential ramen topping*

---

## Headnote

Chashu is the Japanese adaptation of Chinese char siu, but where char siu is roasted or grilled with a sweet glaze, Japanese chashu is braised. The pork belly sits in a bath of soy sauce, sake, ginger, and garlic at a low simmer for about an hour, and during that time the connective tissue in the belly breaks down into gelatin, the fat renders until it is translucent and silky, and the meat absorbs the soy-sake liquid until it is deeply savory all the way through. When you slice it thin and lay it on top of a bowl of ramen, the heat of the broth finishes the work, softening each slice until it practically dissolves on your tongue.

The recipe is almost absurdly simple for how good the result is. Five ingredients plus water. No marinating, no searing, no oven. You put everything in a pot, bring it to a boil, skim the foam, drop to a simmer, and walk away for an hour. The hardest part is not eating it all before it makes it onto the ramen.

The braising liquid that remains after cooking is liquid gold. It is essentially a concentrated shoyu tare, the seasoning base for soy sauce ramen. Save every drop. Use it to marinate ramen eggs, to season the broth in a bowl of shoyu ramen, or to simmer bamboo shoots into menma. A batch of chashu is really three recipes in one: the meat, the eggs, and the broth.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

- 680 g pork belly (about 1.5 lb), skin removed if present
- 4 cloves garlic, peeled and halved
- 30 g fresh ginger, sliced into coins (about the size of a ping-pong ball)
- 1/2 tbsp salt
- 60 ml sake (about 1/4 cup)
- 150 ml soy sauce (about 2/3 cup)
- Water, enough to just cover the pork

---

## Method

1. Prepare the pork. Cut the pork belly across the grain into 2 to 3 blocks, each about 6 inches long. If you prefer round slices for ramen, tie each block into a tight cylinder with kitchen twine. This is optional but makes for neater slices.

2. Combine everything. Place the pork in a large pot or Dutch oven in a single layer. Add the garlic, ginger, salt, sake, and soy sauce. Add enough water to just cover the pork.

3. Braise. Bring the liquid to a boil over high heat. Skim any foam that rises to the surface in the first few minutes. Reduce the heat to medium-low, cover with a drop lid (otoshibuta) or a circle of parchment paper pressed directly on the surface of the liquid, and simmer gently for 40 minutes to 1 hour. The pork is done when a chopstick or fork slides into the meat with no resistance. If your simmer is very gentle, it may take closer to an hour.

4. Rest in the liquid. Turn off the heat and let the pork rest in its braising liquid for 15 minutes. This allows the meat to reabsorb some of the liquid it released during cooking, making it juicier.

5. Remove and slice. Lift the pork out of the liquid. If tied, remove the twine. Slice thin, about 1/4 inch thick, against the grain. The slices should hold together but feel tender enough to pull apart with chopsticks.

6. Save the braising liquid. Let the liquid cool to room temperature, then refrigerate. The fat will solidify on top and can be removed or left in. Use the liquid to marinate ramen eggs, season ramen broth, or simmer bamboo shoots.

## Why This Works

Pork belly is roughly equal parts meat, fat, and connective tissue, which is exactly why it is the ideal cut for braising. The connective tissue (collagen) breaks down into gelatin at temperatures above 160°F (70°C), which takes about 40 to 60 minutes at a gentle simmer. This gelatin gives the cooked meat its characteristic silky, almost melting texture and enriches the braising liquid with body.

The soy sauce and sake penetrate the meat during the long simmer, seasoning it all the way through rather than just on the surface. The garlic and ginger infuse the braising liquid with aromatic depth that transfers to the pork. The salt and soy sauce together provide enough sodium to properly season the meat, which is dense and needs more seasoning than you might expect.

The drop lid (a lid that sits directly on the surface of the liquid, smaller than the pot) serves a specific purpose: it keeps the pork submerged and ensures even cooking while allowing steam to escape so the liquid reduces slightly. A circle of parchment paper does the same job.

Resting the pork in its liquid after cooking is the step that prevents the chashu from drying out. Meat contracts during cooking and squeezes out moisture. Resting in liquid allows the relaxing fibers to reabsorb some of that liquid. Fifteen minutes is enough.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Pork belly:** A rich cut high in both protein and fat, with a characteristic layered structure of meat, fat, and connective tissue. The long braising converts the tough collagen into gelatin, which is easily digestible and has been studied for potential benefits to joint and gut health. The rendered fat contributes to the rich mouthfeel that defines good ramen.

**Sake:** Japanese rice wine used in cooking to tenderize meat, remove gamey flavors, and add a subtle sweetness and depth. The alcohol evaporates during the simmer, leaving behind flavor compounds that enhance the soy sauce.

**Ginger:** The gingerols in fresh ginger serve a dual purpose here: they add aromatic warmth to the braising liquid and help neutralize any pork odor (a standard technique in Japanese and Chinese meat cookery).

## Substitutions and Variations

**Pork shoulder (Boston butt):** Leaner than belly with more meat and less fat. It works well but produces a firmer, less silky result. Increase cooking time to 1.5 hours.

**Torched or seared chashu:** For a charred finish, sear the cooked slices in a very hot dry pan or with a kitchen torch before serving. This adds a smoky, caramelized edge that contrasts the soft interior.

**Miso chashu:** Add 2 tablespoons of white miso to the braising liquid for a different, sweeter flavor profile. Stir it in after the initial boil.

**Double batch:** This recipe doubles easily. The pork stores and freezes well, so making extra is always worthwhile.

---

## Serving Suggestions

The primary home for chashu is on top of a bowl of ramen, alongside [Ramen Eggs](/recipes/ramen-eggs), nori, scallions, and menma. It is also excellent sliced over steamed rice as a quick chashu-don, tucked into bao buns, or served cold and thinly sliced as part of a Japanese-style appetizer spread. The braising liquid can be used directly as the tare (seasoning base) for shoyu ramen.

---

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store the pork in its braising liquid for up to 1 week. The liquid keeps the meat moist and continues to season it gently.

**Reheating:** Warm slices in the braising liquid over low heat, or sear briefly in a hot dry pan. For ramen, the hot broth will warm the slices through.

**Freezing:** Freezes well for up to 3 months, either in the braising liquid or sliced and vacuum-sealed. Thaw overnight in the refrigerator.
