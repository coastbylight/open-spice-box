# Maeuntang (매운탕)

*Korean spicy fish stew with flaky white fish, radish, and chrysanthemum greens in a gochugaru-doenjang broth*

---

## Headnote

Maeuntang is one of those dishes that Koreans turn to without much deliberation. When there is fresh fish, you make maeuntang. The name simply means "spicy soup," and it lives up to the description with a broth that is red, fragrant, and deeply savory from the combination of gochugaru, doenjang, and fish sauce working in concert.

The beauty of maeuntang is that it works with virtually any flaky white fish. Whiting, cod, snapper, and sea bass all perform well here. Whole fish cut into cross-section pieces is traditional, and the bones contribute gelatin and flavor to the broth as they simmer. If whole fish intimidates you, thick fillets will also work, though the broth will not have quite the same body.

The process is straightforward. You build an anchovy and radish broth first, simmering it for about 20 minutes until it turns cloudy and full-bodied. Then the fish goes in along with a seasoning paste built from gochugaru, doenjang, gochujang, garlic, fish sauce, and soy sauce. Another 20 minutes of gentle simmering and the fish is flaky and tender, the radish is translucent, and the broth has taken on that characteristic fiery orange-red color.

The finishing touches matter. Sliced scallion, fresh chili, and a handful of chrysanthemum greens go in right at the end, wilting in the residual heat. Chrysanthemum greens (ssukgat) have a slightly bitter, herbal quality that cuts through the richness of the broth. Serve with steamed rice and [tongbaechu-kimchi](/recipes/tongbaechu-kimchi), setting out individual bowls for discarding bones.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the anchovy-radish broth
- 8 cups (1.9 L) water
- 7 to 8 large dried anchovies, heads and guts removed
- 450 g Korean radish (mu), peeled and sliced into thin half-moons

### For the fish
- 1.1 kg (about 2.5 lb) whole white fish (whiting, cod, snapper, or sea bass), cleaned and cut into 5 cm (2-inch) pieces

### For the seasoning paste
- 30 g gochugaru (about 1/2 cup, Korean chili flakes)
- 30 g minced garlic (about 10 cloves)
- 36 g doenjang (2 tablespoons)
- 17 g gochujang (1 tablespoon)
- 15 ml soy sauce (1 tablespoon)
- 30 ml fish sauce (2 tablespoons)
- 1/2 teaspoon ground black pepper
- 45 ml mirim or soju (3 tablespoons)

### For the finish
- 1 large scallion or 4 to 5 small scallions, cut into 5 cm pieces
- 1 green or red chili pepper, sliced diagonally
- 1 handful chrysanthemum greens (ssukgat), about 60 g

---

## Method

1. Clean the anchovies by removing the heads and dark intestinal strips. This prevents bitterness in the broth. Set aside.

2. Place the water, anchovies, and sliced radish in a large pot. Bring to a boil over high heat, then reduce to a steady simmer. Cook for 20 minutes. The broth will turn cloudy and take on a light savory flavor from the anchovies. Remove and discard the anchovies.

3. While the broth simmers, prepare the seasoning paste. Combine the gochugaru, garlic, doenjang, gochujang, soy sauce, fish sauce, black pepper, and mirim in a small bowl. Stir until it forms a uniform paste.

4. Add the seasoning paste to the simmering broth and stir to dissolve. The broth will turn a deep reddish orange.

5. Gently lower the fish pieces into the broth. Try not to stack them. Bring back to a gentle boil, then reduce to medium heat. Simmer for 15 to 20 minutes, spooning broth over the fish occasionally. Avoid stirring aggressively, as the fish will break apart. The fish is done when it flakes easily and the flesh is opaque throughout.

6. Taste the broth and adjust the seasoning. Add more fish sauce for salinity or more gochugaru for heat.

7. Add the scallion pieces and sliced chili pepper. Cook for 2 minutes, then scatter the chrysanthemum greens over the top. Turn off the heat and let the greens wilt in the residual heat for about 1 minute.

8. Serve immediately in the pot or ladle into individual bowls. Set out steamed rice and small dishes for discarding fish bones.

## Why This Works

The two-stage approach is central to maeuntang. Building the anchovy-radish broth first creates a savory foundation rich in glutamates from the dried anchovies and natural sugars from the radish. When the seasoning paste is introduced to this already-flavorful liquid, the doenjang and gochugaru have something to build on rather than competing with raw water.

Simmering the fish gently rather than boiling vigorously keeps the pieces intact while still allowing the collagen from the bones and skin to enrich the broth. The combination of doenjang (fermented soybean paste) and fish sauce creates overlapping layers of umami, while the gochugaru provides a fruity, lingering heat that does not overpower the delicate flavor of the fish.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Dried anchovies:** Korean soup anchovies (guk-myeolchi) are larger than the tiny ones used for banchan. They are rich in calcium, especially when the bones are consumed, and provide a concentrated source of omega-3 fatty acids and inosinate, a nucleotide that amplifies umami perception.

**Korean radish (mu):** Denser and sweeter than daikon, Korean radish contains digestive enzymes (diastase) and vitamin C. When simmered in broth, it releases natural sugars that round out the flavor.

**Chrysanthemum greens (ssukgat):** These slightly bitter greens are rich in beta-carotene, vitamin C, and folate. Their aromatic quality, similar to a mix of celery and chrysanthemum flowers, adds a layer of herbaceous freshness to rich or spicy dishes.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Fish options:** Any firm, flaky white fish works. Cod and snapper are the most widely available substitutes. Frozen fish works in a pinch, though fresh will give better texture. Some cooks add shrimp or clams along with the fish for a mixed seafood version.

**Without chrysanthemum greens:** Crown daisy greens can be difficult to find outside Korean markets. Watercress or baby spinach provides a similar wilting quality, though the flavor will be different. You can also simply omit them and increase the scallion.

**Spice level:** Reduce the gochugaru to 3 tablespoons for a milder version. For more heat, add an extra sliced chili pepper or a spoonful of gochugaru at the end.

**Tofu addition:** Silken or soft tofu, cut into cubes and added in the last 5 minutes of cooking, is a common addition that stretches the dish and adds protein.

---

## Serving Suggestions

Maeuntang is a communal dish, traditionally served in the center of the table with the pot still bubbling. Steamed short-grain rice is essential for absorbing the broth. [Tongbaechu-kimchi](/recipes/tongbaechu-kimchi) is the classic accompaniment, along with simple banchan like [kongnamul-muchim](/recipes/kongnamul-muchim) and [sigeumchi-namul](/recipes/sigeumchi-namul). Provide small empty bowls at each place setting for discarding fish bones tidily.

---

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store in a sealed container for up to 2 days. The broth remains flavorful, though the fish texture softens with storage. Chrysanthemum greens will darken and lose their freshness overnight.

**Freezer:** Not recommended. The fish becomes mushy after freezing and thawing, and the delicate vegetables lose their texture entirely.

**Reheating:** Warm gently in a pot over medium heat until the broth simmers. Add fresh chrysanthemum greens and scallion when reheating to restore the bright finish. Do not boil vigorously, as the fish will break apart.
