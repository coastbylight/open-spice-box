# Dwaeji Bulgogi (돼지불고기)

*Spicy pork bulgogi marinated in gochujang, Korean pear, and garlic, grilled or pan-fried and wrapped in lettuce with ssamjang*

---

## Headnote

Dwaeji bulgogi is the spicy pork counterpart to the more widely known beef [bulgogi](/recipes/bulgogi). Where beef bulgogi relies on soy sauce and sugar for its sweet-savory character, dwaeji bulgogi gets its identity from gochujang, the fermented red pepper paste that gives the meat a deep red color and a complex, lingering heat. The two dishes share a name and a technique, but they occupy different corners of the Korean table.

The key to a good dwaeji bulgogi marinade is Korean pear. When crushed or pureed, it releases enzymes that gently tenderize the pork while adding a subtle, clean sweetness that balances the salty fermented funk of the gochujang. Pureed onion works alongside the pear, contributing moisture and its own enzymatic tenderizing effect. Garlic, ginger, soy sauce, rice syrup, sesame oil, and a generous amount of gochugaru fill out the marinade.

Pork belly is the traditional cut, sliced thin so it marinates quickly and cooks fast over high heat. Thirty minutes is enough time for the flavors to penetrate, though an overnight marinade in the refrigerator deepens everything. The cooking is simple: grill over high flame until the edges char and the gochujang glaze caramelizes, or pan-fry in a hot skillet if grilling is not an option. Either way, the pork should have some blackened spots and a sticky, lacquered surface.

The proper way to eat dwaeji bulgogi is wrapped in a lettuce leaf with a smear of ssamjang, a slice of raw garlic, and maybe a piece of green chili. You fold the lettuce around the filling, press it together, and eat it in one or two bites. It is messy, communal, and exactly the kind of eating that makes Korean barbecue so satisfying.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the marinade
- 120 g Korean pear (about 1/2 cup), peeled, cored, and crushed or pureed
- 40 g onion (about 1/4 cup), pureed or grated fine
- 4 cloves garlic, minced
- 1/2 teaspoon minced ginger
- 1 scallion, minced
- 15 ml soy sauce (1 tablespoon)
- 12 g sugar (1 tablespoon)
- 22 ml rice syrup (1.5 tablespoons)
- 10 ml toasted sesame oil (2 teaspoons)
- 1/4 teaspoon ground black pepper
- 40 to 50 g gochujang (2 to 3 tablespoons)
- 5 g gochugaru (1 tablespoon)

### For the pork
- 450 g pork belly (about 1 lb), sliced thin (3 mm)

### For serving
- Lettuce leaves (green leaf, red leaf, or perilla leaves)
- Raw garlic cloves, sliced
- Green chili peppers, sliced
- Ssamjang

---

## Method

1. Prepare the marinade. If using a blender, combine the Korean pear and onion and pulse until pureed. Otherwise, grate them finely on a box grater. Transfer to a mixing bowl and add the garlic, ginger, scallion, soy sauce, sugar, rice syrup, sesame oil, black pepper, gochujang, and gochugaru. Stir until thoroughly combined.

2. Add the thin-sliced pork belly to the marinade and toss to coat every piece evenly. Cover and refrigerate for at least 30 minutes. For a deeper flavor, marinate for up to 8 hours or overnight.

3. Remove the pork from the refrigerator 10 minutes before cooking to take the chill off.

4. To grill: Heat a grill to high. Lay the marinated pork slices directly on the grates (or use a grill basket for thin pieces). Grill for 2 to 3 minutes per side until charred at the edges and cooked through. The gochujang marinade will caramelize and blacken in spots, which is desirable.

5. To pan-fry: Heat a large skillet or cast iron pan over high heat. Add the pork in a single layer, working in batches to avoid crowding. Cook for 3 to 4 minutes per side until the edges are crispy and caramelized. Do not stir constantly. Let the pork sit in contact with the hot pan to develop color.

6. Transfer the cooked pork to a serving plate. Arrange the lettuce leaves, sliced garlic, chili peppers, and ssamjang around the plate.

7. To eat, place a piece or two of pork on a lettuce leaf, add a dab of ssamjang and a slice of garlic, fold the leaf around the filling, and eat in one or two bites.

## Why This Works

Korean pear contains proteolytic enzymes that break down proteins on the surface of the meat, tenderizing it without turning it mushy during the short marinating time. The high sugar content of the pear also promotes caramelization during grilling, contributing to the characteristic charred edges.

Gochujang is the defining ingredient. Unlike gochugaru, which provides a relatively straightforward chili heat, gochujang is a fermented paste with layers of flavor: sweet from the glutinous rice, salty from the brine, umami from the fermented soybeans, and a rounded heat from the chili powder. When it hits a hot grill, the sugars in the paste caramelize rapidly, creating a sticky, lacquered glaze that clings to the pork.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Pork belly:** A fatty cut that stays moist during high-heat cooking. The fat renders during grilling, basting the meat from within. Pork belly provides protein, B vitamins, and a high caloric density. The rendered fat carries the flavors of the gochujang marinade, which is why this cut is preferred over leaner options.

**Korean pear (bae):** Larger and rounder than Western pears, with a crisp, grainy texture and high water content. Korean pears contain dietary fiber, vitamin C, and potassium. The enzyme content makes them a traditional meat tenderizer in Korean cooking, used in marinades for centuries.

**Gochujang:** Traditionally fermented for months or years in earthenware pots (onggi) exposed to sunlight. The fermentation process produces beneficial lactic acid bacteria and breaks down starches into sugars, creating the paste's characteristic sweet-spicy flavor. Commercial versions use a shorter fermentation period but retain the basic flavor profile.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Pork cuts:** Pork shoulder, sliced thin, is a leaner alternative that still has enough intramuscular fat to stay juicy. Pork loin works but dries out more easily, so be careful not to overcook.

**Without Korean pear:** Substitute with an equal amount of ripe Bosc or Asian pear. In a pinch, a tablespoon of pineapple juice provides similar tenderizing enzymes, though the flavor will be slightly different. Grated apple is another option.

**Chicken version:** Boneless, skinless chicken thighs sliced thin take the gochujang marinade beautifully. Reduce marinating time to 20 minutes and grill or pan-fry over medium-high heat.

**Milder version:** Reduce the gochujang to 1 tablespoon and omit the gochugaru. The dish will still have color and flavor from the gochujang but significantly less heat.

---

## Serving Suggestions

Dwaeji bulgogi is best eaten as part of a Korean barbecue spread. Steamed rice, lettuce and perilla leaves for wrapping, ssamjang, sliced raw garlic, and green chili peppers are the essentials. [Kongnamul-muchim](/recipes/kongnamul-muchim) and [oi-muchim](/recipes/oi-muchim) provide cool, crunchy contrast to the rich, spicy pork. [Tongbaechu-kimchi](/recipes/tongbaechu-kimchi) is always appropriate. A bowl of [doenjang-jjigae](/recipes/doenjang-jjigae) alongside rounds out the meal.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store cooked pork in a sealed container for up to 3 days. The gochujang marinade keeps the meat well-seasoned and flavorful even cold. Uncooked marinated pork keeps for up to 2 days in the refrigerator.

**Freezer:** Freeze the raw marinated pork in a sealed bag for up to 1 month. Thaw in the refrigerator overnight before grilling. Cooked pork can be frozen for up to 1 month, though the texture is best when freshly cooked.

**Reheating:** Reheat in a hot skillet over high heat to restore the caramelized exterior. A couple of minutes per side is enough. Microwaving works but will not revive the charred edges. Prepare fresh lettuce, garlic, and ssamjang when serving leftovers.
