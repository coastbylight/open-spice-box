# Tangsuyuk (탕수육)

*Double-fried pork in a solidified potato starch batter with a bright sweet and sour sauce of pineapple, vegetables, and vinegar*

---

## Headnote

Tangsuyuk is the Korean-Chinese take on sweet and sour pork, and it differs from both its Chinese ancestor and Western Chinese takeout versions in one critical way: the batter. Korean tangsuyuk uses a technique where potato starch is soaked in water for an hour and a half until the starch settles to the bottom and solidifies into a dense, smooth mass. The water is drained off and the solid starch is used to coat the pork, creating a batter that fries into something closer to glass than breadcrumb. It is shatteringly crispy, almost transparent, and maintains its crunch far longer than any flour-based coating.

The dish occupies a central place in Han-Jung cuisine, the unique category of Korean-Chinese food that developed in Korea over the last century. Along with jjajangmyeon and jjamppong, tangsuyuk forms the essential trio of dishes that every Korean-Chinese restaurant must serve. It is celebration food, ordered for birthdays and gatherings, and it sparks one of Korean food culture's most passionate debates: jjikmeok versus bumeok. Should you dip each piece of pork into the sauce individually (jjikmeok) or pour the sauce over the entire plate at once (bumeok)? Both camps have fierce advocates, and restaurants serve the components separately so diners can choose their own path.

The sauce is bright and unapologetically sweet-sour. Water, sugar, and vinegar provide the base, with soy sauce adding a savory anchor. A starch slurry thickens it to a glossy, pourable consistency, and pieces of onion, carrot, bell pepper, and pineapple are cooked briefly in the sauce so they retain some crunch. The contrast between the shatteringly crispy pork and the tangy, fruity sauce is what makes tangsuyuk so addictive.

The starch-settling technique requires patience but no skill. You simply stir potato starch into water, walk away for ninety minutes, and return to find a block of dense starch at the bottom of the bowl. This step cannot be rushed, but it is entirely passive. Once the starch is ready, the actual cooking moves quickly: marinate, coat, double-fry, make the sauce, and serve.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the starch batter
- 140 g potato starch (1 cup)
- 720 ml cold water (3 cups)

### For the pork
- 200 g pork loin (about 7 oz), cut into bite-size strips or pieces
- Pinch of salt
- Pinch of black pepper
- 5 g fresh ginger, grated (1 teaspoon)
- 1/2 egg white
- Vegetable oil for deep frying

### For the sweet and sour sauce
- 720 ml water (3 cups)
- 50 g sugar (1/4 cup)
- 60 ml rice vinegar (4 tablespoons)
- 15 ml soy sauce (1 tablespoon)
- 15 g potato starch (about 1 tablespoon), mixed with 30 ml water for slurry
- 1/2 medium onion, cut into bite-size pieces
- 1/2 medium carrot, thinly sliced or cut into small pieces
- 1/2 green or red bell pepper, cut into bite-size pieces
- 80 g pineapple chunks (fresh or canned, about 1/2 cup)

---

## Method

1. Start the starch batter first. In a large mixing bowl, combine the potato starch and 3 cups of cold water. Stir well until the starch is fully dissolved. Set the bowl aside undisturbed for 1 hour and 30 minutes. During this time, the starch will settle to the bottom and solidify into a dense, smooth layer.

2. While the starch settles, season the pork strips with salt, pepper, and grated ginger. Toss to coat evenly and let sit at room temperature.

3. After the settling time, carefully pour off all the clear water on top without disturbing the solid starch at the bottom. The starch should be a dense, smooth, almost clay-like mass. Add the half egg white to the solid starch and mix with your hands until you have a thick, sticky batter.

4. Add the seasoned pork to the starch batter and toss to coat each piece thoroughly. The coating should be heavy and opaque, completely covering the pork.

5. Heat oil for deep frying in a deep pot or wok to 165 degrees C (330 degrees F). Carefully add the coated pork pieces one at a time, separating any that stick together. Fry in batches for 3 to 4 minutes until the coating is set and light golden. The batter will puff slightly and become rigid. Remove with a slotted spoon and drain on a wire rack. Let rest for 5 minutes.

6. Increase the oil temperature to 190 degrees C (375 degrees F). Return all the pork pieces to the oil and fry a second time for 2 to 3 minutes until the coating is deeply golden and very crispy. The batter should be hard and glass-like when tapped. Remove and drain on a wire rack.

7. While the pork rests after the second fry, make the sauce. Bring 3 cups of water to a boil in a saucepan. Add the sugar, rice vinegar, and soy sauce. Stir until the sugar dissolves.

8. Add the onion, carrot, bell pepper, and pineapple to the sauce. Cook for 2 to 3 minutes until the vegetables are just softened but still have some crunch.

9. Give the starch slurry a stir and pour it into the sauce, stirring constantly. Cook for another 30 seconds to 1 minute until the sauce thickens to a glossy, pourable consistency. It should coat the back of a spoon but still flow freely.

10. Serve immediately. Either pour the sauce over the fried pork on a platter (bumeok style) or serve the sauce in a separate bowl for dipping (jjikmeok style).

## Why This Works

The settled potato starch technique is what makes tangsuyuk's batter unique in the world of fried foods. When potato starch is suspended in water and allowed to settle, the starch granules compact into a dense layer with very little air or water trapped between them. This produces a batter that, when fried, creates an extremely thin, hard, glass-like shell. The density of the coating means it absorbs very little oil, resulting in a crispiness that is clean and sharp rather than greasy.

The double frying is essential for the same reason it is in kkanpunggi and many other Korean fried dishes. The first fry cooks the pork through and sets the starch coating. The resting period allows internal moisture to migrate to the surface. The second fry at a higher temperature then drives out that moisture and hardens the coating further. The result is a crust that can survive contact with sauce for several minutes before beginning to soften, which is why the jjikmeok versus bumeok debate exists in the first place.

The sauce is intentionally thin and bright. Unlike some sweet and sour sauces that are thick and candy-like, the Korean version uses a large proportion of water relative to sugar and vinegar, creating a lighter sauce that coats the pork without overwhelming the crispy batter. The starch slurry gives it just enough body to cling rather than run off.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Pork loin:** A lean cut with approximately 25 g of protein and 3 g of fat per 100 g serving. It is a good source of thiamine (vitamin B1), which plays a key role in energy metabolism, as well as niacin, vitamin B6, and phosphorus. The deep-frying process adds fat, but the dense potato starch coating absorbs less oil than most flour-based batters.

**Potato starch:** A pure starch extracted from potatoes that is naturally gluten-free. Its large granule size and unique molecular structure produce a distinctively crispy, light texture when fried. It is virtually flavorless, allowing the seasoned pork and sweet-sour sauce to remain the focus.

**Pineapple:** Contains bromelain, a mixture of enzymes that has been studied for anti-inflammatory properties and protein digestion. Fresh pineapple provides vitamin C and manganese. The acidity of pineapple also contributes to the sweet-sour balance of the sauce.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Protein alternatives:** Beef sirloin or chicken breast can replace the pork loin. Shrimp is another popular option. The batter and sauce work with any protein, though cooking times may need slight adjustment.

**Without potato starch:** Cornstarch can substitute for the batter, though it will not achieve the same glass-like crispiness. Mix the cornstarch directly with the egg white and pork without the settling step.

**Fruit variations:** Canned mandarin oranges, grapes, or lychees are sometimes added to or substituted for the pineapple. These are more common in modern Korean-Chinese restaurants.

**Vegetable variations:** Cucumber, wood ear mushroom, or water chestnuts can be added to the sauce for additional texture.

---

## Serving Suggestions

Tangsuyuk is traditionally served as part of a Korean-Chinese feast alongside [jjajangmyeon](/recipes/jjajangmyeon) and [jjamppong](/recipes/jjamppong). This trio is the standard order at any Han-Jung restaurant. At home, serve with steamed rice or on its own as a shared appetizer or drinking snack. The traditional accompaniment is a small dish of pickled yellow radish (danmuji) and sliced raw onion, which provide palate-cleansing crunch and sharpness between bites of sweet, crispy pork.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** The fried pork can be stored separately from the sauce for up to 2 days. The sauce keeps for 3 to 4 days. Keep them in separate containers to preserve any remaining crispiness.

**Reheating:** Re-fry the pork in oil at 190 degrees C (375 degrees F) for 1 to 2 minutes to restore crunch. Reheat the sauce in a saucepan and combine just before serving. Oven reheating at 200 degrees C (400 degrees F) for 8 to 10 minutes is a reasonable alternative if deep frying is not practical. Microwaving the pork will make the coating soft and chewy.

**Freezing:** The fried pork (without sauce) can be frozen for up to 1 month. Freeze on a sheet pan first, then transfer to a freezer bag. Fry directly from frozen to reheat, adding 1 to 2 extra minutes of cooking time. The sauce can be frozen separately for up to 2 months.
