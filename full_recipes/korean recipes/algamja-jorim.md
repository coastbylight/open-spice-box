# Algamja-jorim (알감자조림)

*Baby potatoes boiled, pan-fried until wrinkled, and glazed in soy sauce and rice syrup*

---

## Headnote

Algamja-jorim is a banchan that proves how much flavor you can coax from the simplest ingredients. Baby potatoes are boiled until tender, pan-fried until their skins wrinkle and blister, and then tossed in a glaze of soy sauce, rice syrup, and garlic. The finished potatoes are dark and shiny, simultaneously tender inside and slightly chewy on the surface, with a sweet-savory coating that makes them nearly impossible to stop eating.

The name itself tells you something about the dish's character. "Algamja" translates roughly to "egg potato," a reference to the small size of the potatoes used. Traditionally, these are the tiny potatoes that Korean farmers would find at the edges of the harvest, too small to sell but perfect for braising whole. They resemble quail eggs in size, and that diminutive scale is part of the appeal. Each potato is a single bite, fully glazed on all sides, a self-contained package of flavor.

The technique has three distinct stages, and each one matters. Boiling cooks the potatoes through and softens the starch. Pan-frying in oil creates the wrinkled skin that gives the dish its distinctive texture and provides surface area for the glaze to grip. And the final toss in soy sauce and rice syrup with garlic creates the dark, lacquered coating that defines algamja-jorim.

This is comfort food in the truest Korean sense. It appears at everyday family meals, in school lunchboxes, and on holiday tables. Children love it because the potatoes are naturally sweet. Adults love it because the soy sauce and sesame bring depth that a simple description does not prepare you for. It is one of those banchan that disappears first from the table, prompting someone to make a double batch next time.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the potatoes
- 450 g baby potatoes (about 1 lb), scrubbed clean, skin on
- 30 ml vegetable oil (2 tablespoons)

### For the glaze
- 45 ml soy sauce (3 tablespoons)
- 45 ml rice syrup (3 tablespoons)
- 1 clove garlic, minced fine
- 5 ml toasted sesame oil (1 teaspoon)
- 15 g toasted sesame seeds (about 1 tablespoon)

---

## Method

1. Place the baby potatoes in a medium saucepan and cover with cold water by about 2 cm (1 inch). Bring to a boil over high heat, then reduce to a steady simmer. Cook for about 15 minutes until the potatoes are tender when pierced with a knife but not falling apart. Drain thoroughly and let them sit in the colander for a minute to allow surface moisture to evaporate.

2. Heat the vegetable oil in a large non-stick skillet or cast iron pan over medium-high heat. Add the boiled potatoes in a single layer. Cook for 3 to 5 minutes, shaking the pan occasionally, until the potato skins are wrinkled, slightly blistered, and golden in spots. Some of the skins may split, which is fine.

3. Reduce the heat to medium. Add the soy sauce, rice syrup, and minced garlic directly to the pan with the potatoes. Stir and toss constantly for 4 to 5 minutes as the liquid bubbles and reduces. The glaze will thicken and darken, coating each potato in a shiny, sticky layer. Continue until the sauce has nearly fully reduced and the potatoes are uniformly glossy and dark brown.

4. Remove from heat. Drizzle with the sesame oil and sprinkle with sesame seeds. Toss gently to distribute. Transfer to a serving dish and let cool slightly before serving.

---

## Why This Works

Boiling the potatoes before frying accomplishes two things that cannot be achieved by frying alone. It cooks the starch completely through, ensuring a fluffy interior, and it hydrates the outer layer of the potato so that when it hits the hot oil, the moisture rapidly evaporates and creates the characteristic wrinkled, blistered skin. That textured surface is essential because it dramatically increases the surface area that the glaze can adhere to.

The combination of soy sauce and rice syrup in the glaze creates a balance that neither ingredient could achieve alone. Soy sauce provides salt and glutamic acid, the primary driver of umami flavor. Rice syrup provides viscosity and a clean sweetness that tempers the saltiness without making the dish taste like dessert. As the glaze reduces in the pan, the sugars in the rice syrup undergo light caramelization, adding another layer of flavor complexity.

Adding the garlic raw to the glaze rather than frying it first is intentional. The brief contact with the hot sauce mellows the garlic just enough to remove its harsh bite while preserving its sharp, pungent aroma. Fully cooked garlic would disappear into the background, but here it stays present and assertive.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Baby potatoes:** Potatoes are an excellent source of potassium, vitamin C, and vitamin B6. Cooking them with the skin on preserves more of the fiber and nutrients concentrated just beneath the surface. Baby potatoes have a higher skin-to-flesh ratio than larger varieties, which means more fiber per serving. The resistant starch that forms as cooked potatoes cool has been studied for potential benefits to gut health.

**Rice syrup (jocheong):** Made from cooked rice processed with malt enzyme, rice syrup provides a gentler sweetness than refined sugar and does not crystallize when cooled. Its high viscosity is what gives jorim dishes their characteristic glossy, lacquered appearance.

**Sesame seeds:** Rich in calcium, iron, magnesium, and zinc. Sesame seeds also contain lignans, particularly sesamin and sesamolin, which have been studied for their antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Toasting the seeds before use intensifies their nutty flavor and aroma.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Potato alternatives:** If baby potatoes are unavailable, cut medium-sized potatoes into 2 to 3 cm (1 inch) cubes. The cooking time will be similar. Waxy varieties like Yukon Gold or red potatoes hold their shape better than starchy russets.

**Sweetener swap:** Corn syrup, honey, or maple syrup can replace rice syrup. Honey will add its own flavor notes, while corn syrup is more neutral. Use the same quantity as a starting point and adjust to taste.

**Spicy version:** Add 1 teaspoon of gochugaru or a few slices of fresh chili to the glaze for gentle heat. A tablespoon of gochujang mixed into the sauce creates a richer, spicier variation.

**Corn syrup variation:** Some Korean recipes use a combination of rice syrup and corn syrup (mulgot) for a lighter-colored, slightly different flavor profile.

## Serving Suggestions

Algamja-jorim is a versatile banchan that complements almost any Korean meal. Its mild, sweet-savory flavor makes it a natural partner for spicier dishes like [sundubu-jjigae](/recipes/sundubu-jjigae) or [kimchi-jjigae](/recipes/kimchi-jjigae). It also pairs well with grilled meats like [bulgogi](/recipes/bulgogi) or [galbi](/recipes/galbi), where the potatoes serve as a starchy side in place of rice. Pack it in lunchboxes, where it tastes just as good at room temperature as it does warm.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store in an airtight container for up to 5 days. The glaze may thicken and become slightly tacky as it cools, which is normal. The flavor remains consistent throughout the storage period.

**Reheating:** Reheat gently in a skillet over medium-low heat with a teaspoon of water to loosen the glaze. Microwave reheating works but may cause the potato skins to toughen slightly. Best eaten within the first three days for optimal texture.

**Freezing:** Can be frozen for up to 1 month, though the potato texture will soften after thawing. Thaw in the refrigerator overnight and reheat in a skillet.
