# Jeyuk-Deopbap (제육덮밥)

*Spicy gochujang pork and vegetables stir-fried and served over steamed rice, a Korean one-bowl meal*

---

## Headnote

Jeyuk-deopbap is the kind of meal you throw together on a weeknight when you want something fast, satisfying, and bold enough to shake off a dull day. It is a rice bowl topped with stir-fried pork in a gochujang-based sauce, piled high with cabbage, carrot, and scallions. The name breaks down simply: jeyuk means pork, and deopbap means rice topped with something.

This dish lives in the same family as jeyuk-bokkeum (spicy stir-fried pork), the difference being that deopbap is specifically served over rice as a one-bowl meal rather than as a separate banchan. The distinction matters more in naming than in cooking, because the pork preparation is essentially the same.

The sauce does most of the work. Gochujang provides fermented depth and moderate heat. A spoonful of gochugaru adds the characteristic Korean chili flavor, while soy sauce and a touch of sugar balance everything. Potato starch in the sauce creates a light glaze that clings to the pork and vegetables rather than pooling at the bottom of the bowl. The cooking is fast and aggressive, just a few minutes in a screaming hot pan to get some caramelization on the pork before the vegetables go in.

There is room for improvisation with the vegetables. Cabbage and carrot are standard, but zucchini, mushrooms, and bean sprouts all work. The one thing that should not change is the generous pile of scallions, which soften just enough to become sweet and aromatic without losing their bite. A fried egg on top is optional but strongly encouraged.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the pork
- 450 g pork shoulder (about 1 lb), sliced thin against the grain (3 mm thick)
- 30 ml vegetable oil (2 tablespoons)

### For the sauce
- 30 ml soy sauce (2 tablespoons)
- 120 ml water (1/2 cup)
- 5 g gochugaru (1 tablespoon)
- 65 g gochujang (1/4 cup)
- 5 g potato starch or cornstarch (1 teaspoon)
- 1/4 teaspoon ground black pepper
- 12 g sugar (1 tablespoon)

### For the vegetables
- 150 g cabbage (about 2 cups), cut into bite-size pieces
- 1 medium carrot, julienned or cut into thin matchsticks
- 6 scallions, cut into 5 cm (2-inch) pieces
- 1 green chili pepper, sliced diagonally
- 1 medium onion, sliced into thin half-moons
- 4 cloves garlic, minced
- 1/2 teaspoon minced ginger

### For the finish
- 10 ml toasted sesame oil (2 teaspoons)
- Toasted sesame seeds for garnish
- Steamed short-grain rice for serving
- Fried eggs (optional, 1 per serving)

---

## Method

1. Prepare the sauce by whisking together the soy sauce, water, gochugaru, gochujang, potato starch, black pepper, and sugar in a small bowl until smooth. Set aside.

2. Heat the vegetable oil in a large skillet or wok over high heat until the oil shimmers. Add the pork slices in a single layer, working in batches if necessary to avoid crowding. Cook without stirring for 2 minutes to get color on one side, then flip and cook for another minute.

3. Add the onion, garlic, and ginger to the pan. Stir-fry for 1 minute until fragrant.

4. Add the cabbage and carrot. Stir-fry for 2 minutes until the cabbage begins to wilt and the carrot softens slightly.

5. Pour the sauce over everything and toss to coat evenly. Cook for 2 to 3 minutes, stirring frequently, until the sauce thickens and glazes the pork and vegetables. The sauce should be glossy and clinging rather than watery.

6. Add the scallion pieces and sliced green chili. Toss for 30 seconds, just enough to soften them slightly while keeping some bite.

7. Remove from heat and drizzle with sesame oil. Toss once more.

8. Divide steamed rice among bowls. Pile the pork and vegetables on top, spooning any remaining sauce from the pan over the rice. Garnish with sesame seeds and top with a fried egg if desired.

---

## Why This Works

High heat and a short cooking time are critical here. The thin-sliced pork needs contact with a hot pan to develop caramelized edges, which adds a layer of flavor that no amount of sauce can replicate. Crowding the pan steams the meat instead of searing it, which is why working in batches matters.

Potato starch in the sauce creates a light, glossy coating that clings to the ingredients without making the dish feel heavy or gloppy. As the starch heats in the liquid, it gelatinizes and thickens just enough to turn the sauce into a glaze. The gochujang provides both heat and fermented complexity, which deepens when it hits the hot pan and caramelizes slightly.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Pork shoulder:** A well-marbled cut that stays moist during high-heat cooking. Pork is one of the best dietary sources of thiamine (vitamin B1), which plays a key role in energy metabolism. It also provides zinc, selenium, and complete protein.

**Gochujang:** This fermented red pepper paste is made from chili powder, glutinous rice, fermented soybeans, and salt. The fermentation process produces beneficial compounds and develops the complex, slightly sweet flavor that distinguishes it from a simple chili sauce. It typically ranges from mild to moderately spicy.

**Potato starch:** Used here as a thickener rather than a coating. It produces a clearer, glossier sauce than wheat flour and has a more neutral flavor. Cornstarch works as a direct substitute.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Pork cuts:** Pork belly or thinly sliced pork loin can substitute for shoulder. Belly will be richer and fattier, while loin will be leaner and slightly less forgiving if overcooked. Partially freezing the meat for 30 minutes makes thin slicing much easier.

**Chicken or beef:** Thinly sliced chicken thigh or beef sirloin both work with the same sauce. Adjust cooking times accordingly.

**Vegetables:** Zucchini, sliced mushrooms, bean sprouts, and perilla leaves are all common additions or substitutions. Add softer vegetables later in the cooking to prevent them from turning mushy.

**Spice level:** For a milder dish, reduce the gochujang to 2 tablespoons and omit the gochugaru. For more heat, add an extra tablespoon of gochugaru.

---

## Serving Suggestions

Jeyuk-deopbap is a one-bowl meal by design, but a few banchan on the side elevate it. [Kongnamul-muchim](/recipes/kongnamul-muchim) adds a clean, crunchy contrast. [Oi-muchim](/recipes/oi-muchim) provides cool relief from the heat. A simple bowl of [miyeok-guk](/recipes/miyeok-guk) alongside turns it into a proper Korean meal. The fried egg on top is not just garnish. Break the yolk and let it run into the rice and sauce.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store the pork and vegetables in a sealed container, separate from the rice, for up to 3 days. The flavors concentrate as it sits.

**Freezer:** The cooked pork and sauce freeze well for up to 1 month. The vegetables soften after thawing but remain acceptable. Freeze the rice separately.

**Reheating:** Warm the pork and vegetables in a skillet over medium heat, adding a splash of water to loosen the sauce. Reheat rice in the microwave with a damp paper towel over the bowl, or steam it briefly. Assemble fresh when ready to serve.
