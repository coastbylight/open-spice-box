# Yukhoe (육회)

*Korean beef tartare with soy, sesame, garlic, and pine nuts served on a bed of Asian pear matchsticks with an egg yolk*

---

## Headnote

Yukhoe is Korea's answer to steak tartare, though it predates any European influence. The dish appears in Korean culinary records going back centuries, and it remains a celebrated preparation at traditional markets and high-end restaurants alike. Gwangjang Market in Seoul is particularly famous for it, where vendors slice and dress the beef to order and serve it with a raw egg yolk perched on top.

The preparation demands the freshest possible beef. There is no heat to hide behind here, no sauce to mask subpar ingredients. Tenderloin is the standard cut, chosen for its tenderness, clean flavor, and lack of connective tissue. The beef is partially frozen to firm it up, then julienned into thin matchstick strips that are easy to eat and absorb the seasoning evenly.

The dressing is elegant in its simplicity: soy sauce, honey, sesame oil, toasted sesame seeds, garlic, scallion, and black pepper. That is all. Each ingredient plays a clear role without competing for attention. The soy provides salt and umami, the honey rounds out the edges, and the sesame oil ties everything together with its rich, nutty warmth.

The pear bed is not merely decorative. Korean pear matchsticks, soaked briefly in sugar water to prevent browning, provide a crisp, sweet base that contrasts with the soft, savory beef. The raw egg yolk on top is meant to be broken and mixed into the beef before eating, adding richness and binding the seasoning to the meat. Scattered pine nuts contribute a buttery crunch.

Yukhoe should be assembled at the last moment and eaten immediately. It does not wait. Keep everything cold throughout the process, from the cutting board to the serving plate. This is a dish of precision and care, a reminder that restraint in the kitchen can produce something extraordinary.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the beef
- 230 g beef tenderloin (about 8 oz), in one piece

### For the seasoning
- 30 ml soy sauce (2 tablespoons)
- 20 g honey (1 tablespoon)
- 15 ml toasted sesame oil (1 tablespoon)
- 15 g toasted sesame seeds (2 tablespoons)
- 2 cloves garlic, minced very fine
- 1 scallion, minced
- 1/4 teaspoon ground black pepper

### For the pear bed
- 1 Korean pear (or Asian pear), peeled, cored, and julienned into matchsticks
- 240 ml cold water mixed with 1 tablespoon sugar (for soaking)

### For the garnish
- 15 g pine nuts (about 1 tablespoon)
- 2 egg yolks (the freshest available)

---

## Method

1. Wrap the beef tenderloin tightly in plastic wrap and place it in the freezer for 1 to 2 hours. The goal is to firm the meat so it holds its shape during slicing, not to freeze it solid. The beef should be stiff but still give slightly when pressed.

2. While the beef freezes, prepare the Korean pear. Julienne the pear into thin matchsticks, about 5 cm (2 inches) long and 3 mm wide. Immediately place the pear matchsticks in the sugar water to prevent browning. Soak for 10 to 15 minutes, then drain well and pat dry gently. Set aside in the refrigerator.

3. Prepare the seasoning. Combine the soy sauce, honey, sesame oil, sesame seeds, garlic, scallion, and black pepper in a small bowl. Stir until the honey is dissolved. Set aside.

4. Remove the beef from the freezer. Using a very sharp knife, slice the tenderloin against the grain into thin sheets about 3 mm thick, then cut those sheets into fine julienne strips about 5 cm long. Work quickly to keep the beef cold. If the meat softens too much, return it to the freezer for 10 minutes.

5. Place the julienned beef in a chilled mixing bowl. Pour the seasoning over the beef and toss gently with chopsticks or a fork, coating the strips evenly. Handle the beef lightly to avoid compressing it.

6. Arrange the drained pear matchsticks on a chilled serving plate, creating a bed in the center.

7. Mound the seasoned beef on top of the pear bed. Shape it into a neat dome.

8. Make a small well in the center of the beef mound. Carefully place a raw egg yolk in the well. Scatter pine nuts around the plate and over the beef.

9. Serve immediately. Instruct diners to break the egg yolk and mix it into the beef and pear before eating.

## Why This Works

Partially freezing the tenderloin is essential for achieving the thin, clean julienne cuts that define yukhoe. Room-temperature beef is too soft and slippery to cut precisely, and the fibers compress under the knife. A semi-frozen state firms the proteins just enough for the blade to pass through cleanly, producing neat matchsticks that hold their shape and absorb the dressing evenly.

The seasoning relies on the synergy between soy sauce (glutamic acid) and sesame seeds (containing their own amino acids) to create a layered umami effect. Honey serves as more than a sweetener: its viscosity helps the dressing cling to the beef rather than pooling at the bottom of the plate. The raw egg yolk, when mixed in, acts as an emulsifier, binding the oil-based sesame dressing to the water-based soy sauce and creating a smooth, cohesive coating.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Beef tenderloin:** The leanest and most tender cut, making it ideal for raw consumption. It provides high-quality complete protein, heme iron (more bioavailable than plant-based iron), zinc, and vitamin B12. When eating beef raw, sourcing from a reputable butcher who can confirm the meat is fresh and intended for raw consumption is essential. Pregnant individuals, young children, elderly individuals, and those with compromised immune systems should avoid raw meat.

**Korean pear (bae):** Contains high levels of dietary fiber and vitamin C. The crisp texture provides a necessary contrast to the soft beef. Korean pears also contain enzymes that can aid in digestion of the raw protein, which is one reason the pairing is traditional.

**Pine nuts (jat):** A traditional Korean garnish prized for their rich, buttery flavor. They are a good source of magnesium, vitamin E, and vitamin K. Pine nuts also contain pinolenic acid, a polyunsaturated fatty acid that has been studied for its potential role in appetite regulation and cholesterol management.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Beef cuts:** Eye of round or top sirloin can substitute for tenderloin at a lower cost. These cuts are leaner and slightly tougher, so extra care in slicing thin is important. Avoid cuts with significant marbling or connective tissue.

**Without Korean pear:** A ripe but firm Bosc pear or Fuji apple can stand in. The texture will differ slightly, but the sweet, crisp contrast to the beef will remain.

**Egg-free:** The egg yolk can be omitted. Some contemporary presentations replace it with a quail egg yolk, which is smaller and milder.

**Yukhoe bibimbap:** A popular variation mixes the seasoned raw beef and pear into a bowl of warm rice with the egg yolk, creating a composed rice bowl. The warmth of the rice gently warms the surface of the beef without cooking it through.

## Serving Suggestions

Yukhoe is typically served as an appetizer or special-occasion dish. At Gwangjang Market in Seoul, it is often eaten as a standalone snack. At home, it pairs well before a Korean barbecue meal or as part of a special dinner spread. [Japchae](/recipes/japchae) and [gyeran-jjim](/recipes/gyeran-jjim) are complementary dishes that do not compete with the delicate flavors of the raw beef. A small cup of [saenggang-cha](/recipes/saenggang-cha) alongside provides a warming contrast.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Yukhoe should not be stored. Prepare it immediately before serving and consume it within 30 minutes of assembly. The seasoning begins to draw moisture from the beef, and the texture deteriorates rapidly.

**Freezer:** Not applicable. This dish is meant to be eaten fresh.

**Leftovers:** If any seasoned beef remains uneaten, it can be quickly stir-fried in a hot pan within a few hours. The soy-sesame seasoning works well as a base for cooked beef over rice. Do not attempt to serve leftover yukhoe as raw beef the following day.
