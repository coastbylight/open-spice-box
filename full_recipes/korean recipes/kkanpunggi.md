# Kkanpunggi (깐풍기)

*Double-fried crispy chicken tossed in a spicy garlic-leek oil with sweet and sour sauce, a Korean-Chinese classic*

---

## Headnote

Kkanpunggi belongs to the family of Korean-Chinese dishes known as Han-Jung cuisine, a category of food that exists only in Korea, created by Chinese immigrants who adapted their regional specialties for Korean palates. The name breaks down into three parts: "kkan" meaning dry, "pung" meaning stir-fry, and "gi" meaning chicken. Together they describe the technique perfectly: chicken that is fried until dry and crispy, then tossed quickly in a sauce so that it remains crunchy rather than becoming soft and saucy.

The dish starts with chicken breast, which might seem like an odd choice for fried chicken until you understand the role of the batter. The chicken is marinated briefly in ginger, soy sauce, and pepper, then coated first in egg white and then in potato starch. This creates an extraordinarily crispy shell during the double-fry process, a thin, glass-like crust that shatters when you bite through it. The chicken breast inside stays juicy because the batter acts as insulation, and the double-frying ensures the crust is stable enough to survive being tossed in sauce without going soggy.

The sauce has two components that come together at the end. First, there is the garlic-leek oil: sliced garlic and chopped leek fried slowly in oil with gochugaru until the oil turns red and the garlic is fragrant and golden. Second, there is the sweet and sour sauce: a mixture of soy sauce, water, rice syrup, vinegar, and a touch of starch for body. The crispy chicken, stir-fried vegetables, and both sauce elements are combined in a hot wok for just seconds, tossed rapidly so every piece gets coated without losing its crunch.

The result is a dish of contrasts. The chicken is simultaneously crispy and saucy, sweet and sour, spicy and garlicky. Every Korean-Chinese restaurant has its own version, and kkanpunggi is one of the most popular items on any Han-Jung menu. Making it at home requires a bit of organization, but the technique is straightforward once you understand the sequence.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the chicken marinade
- 225 g boneless, skinless chicken breast (about 1/2 lb), cut into bite-size pieces
- 5 g fresh ginger, grated (1 teaspoon)
- 10 ml soy sauce (2 teaspoons)
- Pinch of black pepper

### For the batter
- 1 egg white
- 60 g potato starch (about 1/2 cup), plus more for dusting
- Vegetable oil for deep frying

### For the garlic-leek oil
- 60 ml vegetable oil (1/4 cup)
- 6 cloves garlic, thinly sliced
- 30 g leek, white and light green parts, finely chopped (about 2 tablespoons)
- 10 g gochugaru (about 1 tablespoon)

### For the sweet and sour sauce
- 30 ml soy sauce (2 tablespoons)
- 60 ml water (1/4 cup)
- 30 ml rice syrup (2 tablespoons)
- 15 ml rice vinegar (1 tablespoon)
- 10 g potato starch (about 1 tablespoon), mixed with 15 ml water

### For the stir-fry
- 1/2 medium onion, cut into bite-size pieces
- 1/2 green bell pepper, cut into bite-size pieces
- 1/2 red bell pepper, cut into bite-size pieces
- 3 to 4 dried red chilies (optional, for garnish and heat)

---

## Method

1. Combine the chicken pieces with the grated ginger, soy sauce, and black pepper in a bowl. Toss to coat and let marinate for 15 minutes at room temperature.

2. Prepare the garlic-leek oil. Heat 1/4 cup vegetable oil in a small saucepan or skillet over low heat. Add the sliced garlic and chopped leek. Cook slowly, stirring occasionally, for 3 to 4 minutes until the garlic is light golden and fragrant. Add the gochugaru and stir for another 30 seconds until the oil turns red. Remove from heat immediately and set aside. Be careful not to burn the garlic.

3. Prepare the sweet and sour sauce. In a small bowl, combine the soy sauce, water, rice syrup, and rice vinegar. Stir the potato starch slurry and have it ready nearby. Set aside.

4. Coat the marinated chicken. Add the egg white to the chicken and mix well. Add the potato starch and toss until each piece is evenly coated with a thick layer of starch. The coating should be heavy and visible.

5. Heat oil for deep frying in a deep pot or wok to 175 degrees C (350 degrees F). Fry the chicken pieces in batches without crowding for 3 to 4 minutes until the coating is set and pale golden. Remove with a slotted spoon and drain on a wire rack. Let the chicken rest for 5 minutes.

6. Increase the oil temperature to 190 degrees C (375 degrees F). Return all the chicken to the oil and fry a second time for 2 to 3 minutes until the crust is deeply golden and very crispy. Remove and drain on a wire rack.

7. Heat a wok or large skillet over high heat. Add a tablespoon of oil and stir-fry the onion and bell peppers for 1 to 2 minutes until slightly charred at the edges but still crisp. Add the dried red chilies if using.

8. Pour the sweet and sour sauce mixture into the wok and bring to a quick boil. Give the starch slurry a stir and add it to the wok. Stir until the sauce thickens slightly, about 30 seconds.

9. Add the garlic-leek oil (oil and solids together) to the wok. Immediately add the double-fried chicken. Toss everything together rapidly for 15 to 20 seconds, just long enough to coat the chicken without making it soggy. Transfer to a serving plate immediately.

## Why This Works

Double frying is the technique that makes kkanpunggi possible. The first fry at a lower temperature cooks the chicken through and sets the potato starch coating. The resting period between fries allows moisture from the chicken to migrate outward into the crust. The second fry at a higher temperature then drives that moisture out and creates a rigid, glass-like shell that is far more resistant to sauce than a single-fried coating would be.

Potato starch, rather than cornstarch or flour, is the correct choice for this batter. When fried, potato starch creates a distinctively crispy, almost transparent crust that stays crunchy much longer than wheat-based batters. It also absorbs less oil, resulting in a lighter, cleaner crunch. The egg white helps the starch adhere to the chicken and adds a subtle airiness to the coating.

The garlic-leek oil is prepared separately and gently to preserve the flavor of both the garlic and the gochugaru. Cooking them slowly in oil allows their aromatic compounds to infuse the fat without burning, creating a flavored oil that delivers heat, fragrance, and color in a single addition. Adding this oil at the very end, along with the minimal sauce contact time, ensures the chicken stays crispy even after being dressed.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Chicken breast:** A lean protein source with approximately 31 g of protein per 100 g serving and very little fat. It is also a good source of niacin, vitamin B6, and selenium. The deep-frying process adds fat and calories, but the potato starch coating absorbs less oil than flour-based batters.

**Potato starch:** Extracted from potatoes and composed almost entirely of carbohydrate, potato starch produces a distinctively crispy, light coating when fried. It is naturally gluten-free, making this dish accessible to those avoiding wheat.

**Gochugaru:** Korean red pepper flakes contribute fruity heat and vibrant red color. They contain capsaicin, which has been studied for anti-inflammatory and metabolic effects, and they are also a source of vitamin A.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Protein alternatives:** Chicken thigh can replace breast for a juicier result, though the flavor will be richer. Shrimp or firm tofu can also be battered and fried using the same technique.

**Without potato starch:** Cornstarch is the closest substitute and will produce a similar crunch, though slightly less crispy. All-purpose flour will work but creates a heavier, less delicate coating.

**Spice level:** Omit the gochugaru and dried chilies for a non-spicy version. The garlic-leek oil will still provide flavor and fragrance. For more heat, increase the gochugaru to 2 tablespoons.

**Sauce variation:** Some versions add a tablespoon of ketchup to the sweet and sour sauce for a more pronounced tanginess and deeper red color.

---

## Serving Suggestions

Kkanpunggi is traditionally served as a main dish alongside steamed white rice. In Korean-Chinese restaurants, it often appears as part of a larger spread that includes [jjajangmyeon](/recipes/jjajangmyeon) and [tangsuyuk](/recipes/tangsuyuk), the holy trinity of Han-Jung cuisine. At home, serve it with rice and a simple side of pickled radish (danmuji). It also works as an appetizer or drinking snack, especially with beer, where the crispy, spicy chicken is an ideal match.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store in an airtight container for up to 2 days. The chicken will lose its crispiness as it sits, which is unavoidable.

**Reheating:** The best method is to re-fry the chicken briefly in hot oil at 190 degrees C (375 degrees F) for 1 to 2 minutes to restore crunch, then toss with freshly heated sauce. Alternatively, reheat in an oven at 200 degrees C (400 degrees F) for 8 to 10 minutes. Microwaving will make the coating soft.

**Freezing:** The fried chicken can be frozen before saucing for up to 1 month. Freeze on a sheet pan, then transfer to a bag. Reheat by frying from frozen, then sauce as directed.
