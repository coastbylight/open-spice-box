# Tteokgalbi (떡갈비)

*Minced short rib meat seasoned with soy, garlic, and honey, shaped into patties and grilled, originally a Korean royal court dish*

---

## Headnote

Tteokgalbi has one of the more charming origin stories in Korean cuisine. The name means "rice cake ribs," not because it contains rice cake, but because the minced and shaped meat has the smooth, dense texture of tteok. Legend has it that the dish was created for Korean royalty, specifically for a king who loved the flavor of galbi but could not eat with his hands at the formal court table. The royal kitchen staff solved the problem by removing the meat from the bones, mincing it finely, seasoning it, and shaping it back onto the rib bones so the king could eat it with dignity.

Whether or not the story is entirely true, tteokgalbi has become one of the most celebrated dishes in Korean cuisine. The city of Damyang, in South Jeolla Province, is especially famous for it. Restaurants there have served tteokgalbi for generations, each with their own closely guarded seasoning ratios.

The process requires some patience. Short ribs are soaked to draw out excess blood, then frozen for an hour to firm up the meat, making it easier to separate from the bone and mince finely. The minced meat is mixed with soy sauce, garlic, onion, scallion, mirim, rice syrup, honey, sesame oil, and black pepper, then shaped into thick patties. You can also mold the seasoned meat back onto the rib bones for a traditional presentation.

Grilling is the preferred cooking method, and the honey and rice syrup in the seasoning create a beautiful caramelized glaze. Broiling and pan-frying both work as well. The finished tteokgalbi should be deeply browned on the outside, juicy within, and sweet-savory in a way that makes it hard to stop eating.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the beef
- 450 g (about 1 lb) beef short ribs (bone-in, flanken-cut or English-cut)

### For the seasoning
- 30 ml soy sauce (2 tablespoons)
- 15 ml mirim (1 tablespoon)
- 5 cloves garlic, minced fine
- 40 g onion (about 1/4 cup), minced very fine or grated
- 1 scallion, minced
- 15 ml rice syrup (1 tablespoon)
- 20 g honey (1 tablespoon)
- 10 ml toasted sesame oil (2 teaspoons)
- 1/2 teaspoon ground black pepper

---

## Method

1. Soak the short ribs in cold water for 20 to 30 minutes to draw out excess blood. Change the water once if it becomes very red. Drain and pat dry.

2. Place the ribs on a baking sheet and freeze for 1 hour. This firms the meat and fat, making it much easier to separate from the bone and mince cleanly.

3. Remove the ribs from the freezer. Using a sharp knife, separate the meat from the bones, cutting away any large pieces of connective tissue but keeping the fat. Reserve the bones if you want to shape the meat back onto them for a traditional presentation.

4. Mince the meat finely with a sharp knife, chopping it repeatedly until it reaches a coarse ground consistency. Alternatively, pass it through a meat grinder using a coarse die. The texture should be uniform but not as smooth as a paste. Some small pieces of fat are desirable for moisture.

5. Place the minced meat in a mixing bowl. Add the soy sauce, mirim, garlic, onion, scallion, rice syrup, honey, sesame oil, and black pepper. Mix thoroughly with your hands, kneading the meat and seasonings together for 2 to 3 minutes until the mixture feels cohesive and slightly sticky.

6. Shape the seasoned meat into oval patties about 2 cm (3/4 inch) thick and roughly the size of your palm. If using the reserved bones, press the meat firmly around the bone in an even layer. You should get 4 to 6 patties depending on size.

7. Heat a grill, grill pan, or cast iron skillet over medium-high heat. Brush the cooking surface lightly with oil.

8. Grill the patties for 6 to 8 minutes per side, turning once, until they develop a deep caramelized crust and the internal temperature reaches 71C (160F). The honey and rice syrup will create a glossy, lacquered surface. If the exterior is browning too quickly, reduce the heat to medium.

9. Let the patties rest for 2 minutes before serving. This allows the juices to redistribute.

## Why This Works

Mincing the meat and then kneading it with the seasonings creates a cohesive texture similar to a sausage patty. The mechanical action extracts myosin from the muscle fibers, which acts as a natural binder and keeps the patties from falling apart on the grill. The minced onion contributes moisture and enzymes that help tenderize the meat.

The combination of honey and rice syrup serves different purposes. Honey caramelizes at a lower temperature and contributes a floral sweetness, while rice syrup provides a clean sweetness and creates the characteristic glaze. Together they promote the Maillard reaction on the surface, building the deeply browned, slightly sticky crust that defines well-made tteokgalbi. Freezing the meat before mincing firms up the fat, which ensures it gets cut into small pieces rather than smearing, resulting in a better texture in the finished patty.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Beef short ribs:** One of the most flavorful cuts of beef due to the high fat content and connective tissue. Short ribs are rich in heme iron, which the body absorbs more efficiently than the non-heme iron found in plant foods. They also provide significant amounts of zinc and vitamin B12, essential for red blood cell formation.

**Honey:** Raw honey contains trace amounts of enzymes, minerals, and antioxidants. In this recipe, it functions primarily as a sweetener and browning agent. The fructose in honey caramelizes at a lower temperature than the sucrose in granulated sugar, which helps build color on the grill.

**Sesame oil:** Toasted sesame oil is rich in lignans (sesamin and sesamolin), compounds that have been studied for their antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. It also provides vitamin E and healthy unsaturated fats. A small amount goes a long way, as its flavor is concentrated.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Without short ribs:** Ground beef (80/20 blend) can be used directly, skipping the soaking, freezing, and mincing steps. The flavor will be less complex than hand-minced short rib, but the dish will still be satisfying. Some cooks mix ground pork with the beef for added richness.

**Cooking method:** Broiling works well. Place the patties on a foil-lined baking sheet about 10 cm from the broiler element and cook for 5 to 6 minutes per side. Pan-frying in a cast iron skillet over medium-high heat is another excellent option, and the fond left in the pan can be deglazed with a splash of mirim for a quick sauce.

**Cheese-stuffed variation:** A modern Korean street food version fills the center of each patty with mozzarella cheese. Shape the meat around a cube of cheese, sealing the edges, and grill as directed.

**Pork version:** Minced pork shoulder or a combination of pork and beef makes a richer, fattier patty. Adjust the soy sauce slightly downward if using pork, as it carries seasoning differently than beef.

## Serving Suggestions

Tteokgalbi is traditionally served with steamed rice and a spread of banchan. Lettuce leaves for wrapping (ssam) with [ssamjang](/recipes/doenjang-jjigae) and sliced raw garlic are classic accompaniments. Pickled radish (danmuji) provides a crunchy, sweet contrast. [Kongnamul-muchim](/recipes/kongnamul-muchim) and [sigeumchi-namul](/recipes/sigeumchi-namul) round out the table. In Damyang, tteokgalbi is often served as part of a larger set meal with multiple side dishes.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store cooked patties in a sealed container for up to 3 days. The seasoning keeps the meat well-flavored even when cold, and tteokgalbi makes excellent next-day leftovers.

**Freezer:** Freeze uncooked shaped patties on a baking sheet until solid, then transfer to a freezer bag. They keep for up to 2 months. Cook from frozen, adding 2 to 3 minutes per side. Cooked patties can also be frozen for up to 1 month.

**Reheating:** Reheat under the broiler or in a hot skillet to restore the caramelized exterior. A few minutes per side is enough. Microwaving works but will soften the crust.
