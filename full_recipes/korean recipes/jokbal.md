# Jokbal (족발)

*Pig's trotters braised in soy sauce, doenjang, star anise, and cinnamon with jujubes and apple, sliced and served with shrimp dipping sauce*

---

## Headnote

Jokbal is one of the great anju dishes of Korean cuisine, the kind of food that is designed to be eaten alongside soju or beer late into the evening. Pig's trotters are soaked, blanched, and then braised for hours in a deeply aromatic liquid of soy sauce, doenjang, mirin, star anise, cinnamon, jujubes, apple, and a handful of other seasonings until the meat is tender enough to pull apart and the skin has turned glossy and gelatinous. The trotters are cooled, sliced thin, and served on a platter with a pungent dipping sauce made from saeujeot (fermented shrimp), garlic, and gochugaru.

The dish has been a fixture of Korean nightlife culture for decades. Walk through any Korean neighborhood with late-night restaurants and you will see platters of jokbal on tables surrounded by groups sharing drinks and conversation. The combination of rich, collagen-heavy meat with the salty, funky shrimp sauce and the bite of raw garlic is designed to complement alcohol, and it does this exceptionally well. But jokbal is equally at home at family celebrations and holiday gatherings, where the large platter encourages communal eating.

The braising liquid is where the magic happens. The combination of doenjang and soy sauce creates a savory base with both surface saltiness and deep fermented complexity. Star anise and cinnamon sticks add warm, sweet spice notes that cut through the richness of the pork. Jujubes and apple contribute natural sweetness and fruity acidity. A small amount of brewed coffee, tucked into a spice pouch, adds bitterness and depth that helps darken the skin to a rich mahogany color. Each of these ingredients plays a role, and the long braising time allows them to meld into something greater than any single component.

The trotters need an overnight soak to draw out blood and impurities, followed by a thorough blanching. This double cleaning step is essential for producing a clean-tasting broth and tender, odor-free meat. Patience is the primary skill required here. The actual hands-on work is minimal, but the soaking, blanching, and braising add up to a full day of calendar time. The reward is a platter of thinly sliced pork that is tender, richly flavored, and deeply satisfying.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For soaking and blanching
- 1.4 to 1.8 kg pig's trotter (3 to 4 lb), split lengthwise by the butcher if possible

### For the braising liquid
- 1.9 L water (8 cups)
- 480 ml low-sodium chicken broth (2 cups)
- 30 g doenjang (2 tablespoons)
- 60 ml mirin (1/4 cup)
- 80 ml soy sauce (1/3 cup)
- 55 g brown sugar (1/4 cup, packed)
- 15 g salt (1 tablespoon)
- 60 ml rice syrup (1/4 cup)
- 1 medium apple, quartered (skin on)
- 8 dried jujubes (daechu)
- 8 cloves garlic, smashed
- 30 g fresh ginger, sliced (about a 5 cm piece)
- 1 medium onion, quartered
- 3 to 4 dried red chilies
- 2 stalks green onion, cut into large pieces
- 2 whole star anise
- 1 cinnamon stick
- 1 teaspoon black peppercorns

### For the spice pouch
- 30 ml brewed coffee (2 tablespoons), or 1 teaspoon instant coffee dissolved in 2 tablespoons hot water

### For the shrimp dipping sauce
- 30 g saeujeot (fermented salted shrimp, about 2 tablespoons)
- 2 cloves garlic, minced fine
- 5 g gochugaru (1 teaspoon)
- 5 ml rice vinegar (1 teaspoon, optional)

## Method

1. Place the pig's trotter in a large pot or basin and cover with cold water. Soak in the refrigerator for at least 12 hours, or up to 24 hours, changing the water two or three times. This draws out blood and impurities, resulting in a cleaner-tasting final product.

2. Drain the trotter and place it in a large stockpot. Cover with fresh cold water. Bring to a boil over high heat and let it boil vigorously for 20 to 25 minutes. A significant amount of gray foam and impurities will rise to the surface. Drain the trotter, rinse it thoroughly under running water, and scrub away any remaining scum or debris. Clean the pot as well.

3. Return the cleaned trotter to the clean pot. Add all of the braising liquid ingredients: the water, chicken broth, doenjang, mirin, soy sauce, brown sugar, salt, rice syrup, apple, jujubes, garlic, ginger, onion, dried chilies, green onion, star anise, cinnamon stick, and peppercorns.

4. Place the coffee in a small piece of cheesecloth or a disposable spice bag, tie it closed, and add it to the pot. This allows the coffee to infuse the liquid without leaving grounds in the braise.

5. Bring the liquid to a boil over high heat. Reduce the heat to low, maintaining a gentle simmer. Cover and braise for 2 hours, turning the trotter over once halfway through. The meat should be very tender and pulling away from the bone when done. A chopstick or skewer should slide through the thickest part with no resistance.

6. Remove the pot from the heat and let the trotter cool in the braising liquid for at least 30 minutes. This allows the meat to reabsorb some of the liquid and firms up the gelatinous skin. Remove the trotter from the liquid and place it on a cutting board.

7. While the trotter cools enough to handle, prepare the dipping sauce. Combine the saeujeot, minced garlic, gochugaru, and rice vinegar (if using) in a small bowl. Stir to combine. The sauce should be chunky and pungent.

8. Using a sharp knife, separate the meat from the bones. Slice the meat and skin across the grain into thin pieces, about 6 mm (1/4 inch) thick. Arrange the slices on a serving platter, showing the layers of meat, fat, and gelatinous skin.

9. Serve the sliced jokbal at room temperature or slightly warm, with the shrimp dipping sauce alongside. Provide lettuce leaves or perilla leaves for wrapping, if desired.

## Why This Works

The overnight soak and vigorous blanch are not optional steps. Pig's trotters contain a significant amount of blood, particularly in the joints and marrow. Soaking draws this out gradually, while the blanching forces remaining impurities to coagulate and rise to the surface. Skipping either step results in a murky broth and a pork-y, off-putting aroma in the finished dish.

The braising liquid is built on a foundation of two fermented products working in concert. Soy sauce provides clean saltiness and glutamic acid, the primary umami compound. Doenjang adds a deeper, more complex fermented flavor that includes earthy, slightly funky notes. Together, they season the meat more thoroughly than either could alone.

The warm spices, star anise and cinnamon, serve a specific purpose beyond flavor. They contain compounds that help mask the strong aroma of braised pork skin and fat, which some find off-putting. The apple and jujubes contribute natural acids and sugars that tenderize the meat and balance the saltiness of the soy and doenjang. The coffee, perhaps the most unexpected ingredient, adds bitter depth and helps develop the rich, dark mahogany color of the finished skin.

Cooling the trotter in the braising liquid is critical. As the liquid cools, the gelatin extracted from the skin and connective tissue begins to set, trapping flavor within the meat. Removing the trotter while still hot would result in drier, less flavorful slices.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Pig's trotter:** Extremely rich in collagen, the connective tissue protein that converts to gelatin during long cooking. Collagen has been widely studied for its potential benefits to skin elasticity, joint health, and gut lining integrity, though clinical evidence for supplementation remains mixed. Trotters also provide significant iron, zinc, and B vitamins. The skin and fat layers are high in saturated fat.

**Saeujeot (fermented salted shrimp):** A traditional Korean condiment produced by salting and fermenting small shrimp. It is intensely salty and funky, with a deep umami flavor that comes from the breakdown of proteins during fermentation. It contains beneficial bacteria and enzymes from the fermentation process.

**Jujubes (daechu):** Dried red dates used extensively in Korean cooking and traditional medicine. They are a good source of vitamin C, potassium, and various antioxidant compounds. In Korean tradition, jujubes are considered warming and nourishing, often used in restorative soups and teas.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Cut alternatives:** If whole trotters are unavailable or intimidating, pork hocks or bone-in pork shoulder can be braised using the same liquid and technique. The flavor profile will be similar, though the distinctive gelatinous texture of trotter skin will be absent.

**Without saeujeot:** The dipping sauce can be made with a mixture of soy sauce, minced garlic, gochugaru, and a touch of sesame oil. The flavor will be less complex, but the dish will still be very enjoyable.

**Without coffee:** Omit the coffee pouch entirely. The finished skin will be lighter in color, but the flavor difference is subtle.

**Wrapping style:** Jokbal is traditionally eaten wrapped in lettuce or perilla leaves with a dab of ssamjang, a slice of raw garlic, and a piece of pickled radish. This wrapping transforms each bite into a complete package of flavors and textures.

---

## Serving Suggestions

Jokbal is quintessential anju, designed to accompany drinking. Serve it with soju or beer as part of a Korean drinking session. The traditional accompaniments are the shrimp dipping sauce, lettuce or perilla leaves for wrapping, sliced raw garlic, sliced green chilies, and [kkakdugi](/recipes/kkakdugi) (cubed radish kimchi). For a more substantial meal, add steamed rice and a bowl of [doenjang-jjigae](/recipes/doenjang-jjigae). Jokbal also appears at holiday tables and celebrations, where the large platter serves as a centerpiece for communal eating.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store the sliced or unsliced meat in an airtight container for up to 4 days. The braising liquid will set into a firm jelly as it cools. Save the jelly separately; it can be used as a base for soups or sauces.

**Reheating:** Jokbal is traditionally served at room temperature or slightly warm. If you prefer it warm, steam the sliced meat gently for 5 to 10 minutes, or reheat in a covered skillet with a splash of the braising liquid over low heat. Avoid high heat, which can toughen the skin.

**Freezing:** The braised trotter can be frozen whole or sliced for up to 2 months. Wrap tightly in plastic wrap and then foil. Thaw in the refrigerator overnight before slicing and serving. The texture holds up reasonably well after freezing.
