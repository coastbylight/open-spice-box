# Bugeopo Gochujang-Muchim (북어포 고추장무침)

*Dried shredded pollock tossed in a sweet-spicy gochujang sauce with rice syrup, sesame oil, and garlic*

---

## Headnote

Bugeopo gochujang-muchim is the kind of banchan that Korean households always have waiting in the refrigerator. There is no cooking involved. You soak dried shredded pollock, squeeze it dry, toss it in a sweet and spicy sauce, and the dish is finished. The whole process takes about twenty minutes, and the result improves over time as the sauce softens the fish and the flavors meld into one another.

Dried pollock, or bugeo, holds a special place in Korean food culture. The fish is caught and then dried outdoors through cycles of freezing and thawing during the winter months, a process that transforms its texture into something light, fibrous, and almost cotton-like when shredded. This form, bugeopo, is sold in bags at Korean grocery stores and has become one of the country's most common pantry staples. Koreans use it in soups, stir-fries, and side dishes, but this no-cook preparation may be the simplest and most satisfying of all.

The sauce follows a familiar Korean pattern: gochujang for fermented chili depth, rice syrup for glossy sweetness, sesame oil for nutty fragrance, vegetable oil for body, and garlic for sharpness. The balance between sweet and spicy is the key. Rice syrup provides a clean, rounded sweetness that tempers the heat of the gochujang without making the dish taste sugary. The two oils work together to create a smooth coating that clings to each shred of fish rather than pooling at the bottom of the bowl.

This is one of the first banchan that Korean home cooks learn to make, and for good reason. It requires no knife skills, no stove, and no precise timing. If you can mix a sauce and toss ingredients in a bowl, you can make bugeopo gochujang-muchim. It stores beautifully, packs well in lunchboxes, and pairs with virtually any Korean meal.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the pollock
- 100 g dried shredded pollock (bugeopo)

### For the sauce
- 130 g gochujang (about 1/2 cup)
- 160 ml rice syrup (about 1/2 cup)
- 30 ml toasted sesame oil (2 tablespoons)
- 30 ml vegetable oil (2 tablespoons)
- 2 cloves garlic, minced fine
- 15 g toasted sesame seeds (about 1 tablespoon)

---

## Method

1. Place the dried shredded pollock in a large bowl and cover with cold water. Soak for about 15 minutes until the strands are softened and pliable. Drain thoroughly in a colander, then squeeze out as much water as possible using your hands. The pollock should be damp but not dripping.

2. Tear or cut any long strands into bite-size pieces, roughly 5 to 7 cm (2 to 3 inches). Check through the strands for any small bones and remove them. Dried pollock is generally bone-free, but an occasional small pin bone can remain.

3. In a separate large mixing bowl, combine the gochujang, rice syrup, sesame oil, vegetable oil, and minced garlic. Stir vigorously until the sauce is smooth, glossy, and well combined. The oils should be fully incorporated with no visible separation.

4. Add the squeezed pollock to the sauce. Toss with your hands or tongs, working the sauce into the fish strands until every piece is evenly coated. The pollock should glisten with a thin, uniform layer of the red sauce.

5. Sprinkle the sesame seeds over the dressed pollock and toss once more to distribute evenly.

6. Transfer to a clean jar or airtight container. The bugeopo muchim is ready to eat immediately but will taste even better after resting in the refrigerator for a few hours.

---

## Why This Works

The combination of vegetable oil and rice syrup creates a stable, glossy emulsion that clings to the fibrous strands of dried pollock rather than sliding off. Rice syrup is viscous enough to act as an adhesive, binding the gochujang to each shred of fish while providing a clean sweetness that balances the fermented saltiness of the paste.

Soaking the dried pollock before dressing it serves two purposes. It softens the strands so they absorb the sauce more readily, and it removes excess salt that accumulates during the drying process. Squeezing out the water after soaking is essential because residual moisture would dilute the sauce and prevent it from adhering properly.

The absence of cooking is not a compromise. The dried pollock has already been fully cooked during the drying process, and the gochujang is a fermented product that needs no heat to develop flavor. The sauce actually penetrates the fish more evenly when applied cold, creating a better coating than would result from a stir-fry.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Dried shredded pollock (bugeopo):** Pollock is a lean white fish that becomes extremely protein-dense when dried, with roughly 70 to 80% protein by weight. It is low in fat and provides significant amounts of niacin, vitamin B12, and selenium. In Korean tradition, dried pollock is considered a restorative food, often served in soups after a night of heavy drinking for its purported ability to soothe the stomach and support liver recovery.

**Gochujang:** This fermented chili paste combines the heat of gochugaru with the umami of fermented soybeans and the sweetness of glutinous rice. The fermentation process produces lactic acid bacteria and complex flavor compounds. It is high in sodium, so no additional salt is needed in this recipe.

**Rice syrup (jocheong):** A traditional Korean sweetener produced by breaking down the starches in cooked rice using malt enzyme. It has a mild, clean sweetness and a thick, syrupy consistency that makes it ideal for coating and glazing. It provides a smoother sweetness than granulated sugar and does not crystallize.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Dried pollock alternatives:** Dried shredded squid (ojingeochae) or dried filefish (jwipo) can be prepared with the same sauce for a different texture. Dried squid will be chewier, while filefish will be more delicate.

**Spice level:** For a milder version, reduce the gochujang to 1/4 cup and increase the rice syrup slightly to maintain the sauce consistency. For more heat, add 1 teaspoon of gochugaru to the sauce.

**Without rice syrup:** Corn syrup or honey can substitute. Honey will add its own floral notes, while corn syrup provides neutral sweetness. The consistency will be similar in both cases.

**Added crunch:** Some versions include roasted peanuts or almonds tossed in at the end for textural contrast.

## Serving Suggestions

Bugeopo gochujang-muchim is a reliable banchan that fits into any Korean meal spread. Serve it alongside steamed rice, [doenjang-jjigae](/recipes/doenjang-jjigae), and a few other side dishes for a simple home-cooked dinner. It pairs especially well with mild dishes like [gyeran-jjim](/recipes/gyeran-jjim) or plain steamed vegetables, where its bold flavor provides contrast. It also works as an anju (drinking snack) with beer or soju, and it packs well in lunchboxes since it does not need reheating.

---

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store in an airtight container for up to 1 month. The flavor deepens over the first few days as the sauce fully penetrates the dried pollock. The texture softens gradually but remains pleasantly chewy for the full storage period.

**Freezer:** Not necessary given the long refrigerator shelf life. If frozen, thaw in the refrigerator before serving.

**Reheating:** No reheating is needed. Bugeopo muchim is served at room temperature or straight from the refrigerator. If the sauce has dried out after extended storage, toss with a small amount of sesame oil and rice syrup to refresh.
