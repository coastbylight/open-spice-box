# Daegu-jeon (대구전)

*Korean pan-fried cod fillets coated in flour and egg batter with scallion and chili, served with a soy-vinegar dipping sauce*

---

## Headnote

Jeon is the Korean word for a whole category of pan-fried dishes where ingredients are coated in flour, dipped in beaten egg, and cooked in a thin layer of oil until golden on both sides. The technique is one of the foundations of Korean home cooking, applied to everything from zucchini to mung beans to fish. Daegu-jeon uses cod, and it is one of the most elegant versions, the kind that appears at holiday tables during Lunar New Year and Chuseok alongside towers of other carefully prepared banchan.

The method is straightforward. Pieces of cod are tossed in flour, then dipped in beaten egg that has been studded with chopped scallion and red chili pepper. They go into a pan with a thin layer of oil and cook until the egg sets into a golden crust, about two minutes per side. The flour layer between the fish and the egg is what holds everything together. Without it, the egg slides off. With it, you get a thin, crispy shell around tender, flaky fish.

The result is delicate rather than heavy. This is not deep-fried fish. The coating is barely there, more like a second skin than a batter. The cod stays moist inside, and the scallion and chili in the egg add color and a faint sharpness without overwhelming the mild fish. A dipping sauce of soy sauce and vinegar is the only accompaniment it needs. It is the kind of dish that looks fussy but is actually one of the fastest things you can put on a table once you understand the three-step coating process.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the fish
- 400 g cod fillets, cut into bite-sized pieces (about 2 inches)
- 120 g all-purpose flour (about 1 cup)
- 2 large eggs
- 1 pinch salt
- 1 pinch ground black pepper
- 1 green onion (scallion), finely chopped
- 1 red chili pepper, finely chopped (optional, for color and mild heat)
- Vegetable oil for pan-frying

### For the dipping sauce
- 2 tbsp soy sauce
- 1 to 2 tbsp rice vinegar or white vinegar

### For serving
- Lettuce leaves (for lining the plate)

---

## Method

1. Season and flour the cod. Pat the cod pieces dry with a paper towel and season lightly with salt and pepper. Place the flour in a wide, shallow bowl. Add the cod pieces and toss quickly with your hands until each piece is lightly and evenly coated. Shake off any excess flour.

2. Prepare the egg wash. Beat the eggs in a shallow bowl with a pinch of salt. Stir in the chopped scallion and red chili pepper.

3. Pan-fry. Heat a large non-stick skillet over medium-high heat. Add about 1 tablespoon of oil and swirl to coat. One piece at a time, dip each flour-coated cod piece into the egg mixture, making sure it is well coated on all sides, and place it gently into the pan. Reduce the heat to medium. Cook until the bottom is golden, about 2 minutes, then turn carefully with a spatula. Cook the other side until golden, about 2 more minutes. The fish should be cooked through and the egg coating crisp and set. Add more oil as needed between batches.

4. Make the dipping sauce. Stir together the soy sauce and vinegar in a small dish.

5. Serve. Line a plate with lettuce leaves and arrange the cooked fish on top. Serve with the dipping sauce on the side.

## Why This Works

The three-step coating process (flour, then egg, then pan) is the core technique behind all jeon. The flour creates a dry, starchy layer that the egg clings to. Without it, the wet egg would bead up on the surface of the fish and slide off in the pan. The flour also absorbs surface moisture from the cod, which prevents steaming and promotes browning.

The egg layer cooks into a thin, golden shell that seals in the moisture of the fish. Cod is a lean, mild white fish that dries out easily under high heat, but inside its egg coating it essentially steams gently while the exterior crisps. The result is moist, flaky fish with a crispy surface.

Medium heat rather than high heat is important. High heat browns the egg too quickly, leaving the fish undercooked inside. Medium heat gives the egg time to set slowly while the cod cooks through evenly. The thin coating means the timing is forgiving. Two minutes per side is enough for most pieces.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Cod:** A lean white fish with about 20 grams of protein and less than 1 gram of fat per 100-gram serving. It is an excellent source of vitamin B12, phosphorus, and selenium. Cod is also one of the best dietary sources of iodine, a mineral essential for thyroid function. In Korean food culture, fish jeon dishes appear at celebrations partly because fish symbolizes abundance and prosperity.

**Eggs:** The egg coating adds protein and creates a barrier that keeps the fish moist during cooking. Each egg provides about 6 grams of complete protein and is a source of choline, vitamin D, and B12.

**Vinegar:** The acidity in the dipping sauce cuts through the richness of the fried coating and brightens the mild flavor of the cod. Vinegar also stimulates digestive enzymes, which is why acidic dipping sauces appear alongside fried foods in many Asian cuisines.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Fish:** Any firm, mild white fish works. Halibut, haddock, and pollock are all traditional alternatives. Avoid fish that flakes too easily, as it will break apart during coating and frying.

**Gluten-free:** Replace the all-purpose flour with rice flour. The coating will be slightly crispier and lighter.

**Without chili:** Omit the red chili pepper entirely for a milder version. The scallion alone provides enough color and flavor in the egg wash.

**Zucchini jeon:** The same flour-egg-pan technique applied to thin slices of zucchini is another classic Korean banchan. Slice zucchini into 1/4-inch rounds and follow the same steps.

**Dipping sauce variation:** Add a pinch of gochugaru (Korean red pepper flakes) to the dipping sauce for heat, or a drop of sesame oil for nuttiness.

## Serving Suggestions

Daegu-jeon is banchan, meant to be one of several small dishes on a Korean table. Serve it alongside steamed rice, kimchi, and two or three other side dishes for a traditional Korean meal. It pairs well with soups like doenjang-jjigae (fermented soybean paste stew) or a simple seaweed soup. For a holiday spread, serve alongside japchae (glass noodles), galbi-jjim (braised short ribs), and tteokguk (rice cake soup).

---

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store in an airtight container for up to 2 days. The coating softens as it sits but the flavor remains good.

**Reheating:** Reheat in a dry skillet over medium heat for 1 to 2 minutes per side to re-crisp the coating. The oven works too: 350°F (180°C) for 5 minutes on a wire rack. Avoid the microwave, which makes the coating soggy.

**Freezing:** Freeze cooked jeon in a single layer on a baking sheet, then transfer to a freezer bag. Reheat from frozen in the oven at 375°F (190°C) for 8 to 10 minutes.
