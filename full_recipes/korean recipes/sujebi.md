# Sujebi (수제비)

*Hand-torn noodle soup in an anchovy-kelp broth with potato and zucchini, Korean rainy-day comfort food*

---

## Headnote

There is a saying in Korea that when rain falls, you eat sujebi. The connection is partly practical and partly nostalgic. In agricultural Korea, rainy days meant time indoors, and the pantry staples required for sujebi, flour, dried anchovies, kelp, a few vegetables, were always on hand. The sound of rain on the roof somehow makes a bowl of hand-torn noodle soup taste better than it has any right to.

Sujebi is humble cooking. The dough is nothing more than flour, water, salt, and a little oil, kneaded until smooth and then rested in the refrigerator. The stock is a clean anchovy-kelp broth, simmered for about 40 minutes until it develops a gentle savory depth. There is no need for precision when tearing the dough. The irregular shapes are the whole point. Thin, flat pieces with ragged edges catch the broth in their folds and cook unevenly, so you get a range of textures from silky to slightly chewy in every bowl.

The vegetables are simple. Cubed potato adds starch and body, onion sweetens the broth, and a few cloves of garlic keep things grounded. A splash of fish sauce and guk-ganjang (Korean soup soy sauce) seasons the broth without darkening it. When the torn noodle pieces float to the surface, they are done.

Finish with sliced scallion and a drizzle of sesame oil. That is the entire recipe. No elaborate technique, no long ingredient list, just a bowl of noodle soup that tastes like someone cared enough to make it by hand.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the dough
- 240 g all-purpose flour (about 2 cups)
- 180 ml cold water (about 3/4 cup)
- 1/2 teaspoon fine salt
- 15 ml vegetable or neutral oil (1 tablespoon)

### For the broth
- 2.4 L water (about 10 cups)
- 12 large dried anchovies, heads and guts removed
- 1 piece dried kelp (dashima), about 10 to 13 cm (4 to 5 inches)

### For the soup
- 2 medium potatoes (about 340 g), peeled and cut into 2 cm cubes
- 60 g onion (about 1/2 cup), diced
- 3 cloves garlic, minced
- 15 ml fish sauce (1 tablespoon)
- 15 ml guk-ganjang (Korean soup soy sauce, 1 tablespoon)

### For the finish
- 2 scallions, thinly sliced
- 10 ml toasted sesame oil (2 teaspoons)

---

## Method

1. Make the dough. Place the flour in a mixing bowl and add the salt. Pour in the water and oil. Mix with a fork or chopsticks until shaggy clumps form, then turn out onto a lightly floured surface and knead for 10 to 15 minutes until the dough is smooth and elastic. It should spring back gently when pressed. Wrap tightly in plastic wrap and refrigerate for at least 30 minutes and up to overnight.

2. Prepare the broth. Place the water, cleaned anchovies, and kelp in a large pot. Bring to a boil over high heat. Once boiling, reduce to a steady simmer and cook for 15 minutes. Remove the kelp (leaving it longer can make the broth bitter). Continue simmering the anchovies for another 20 to 25 minutes, about 40 minutes total. Strain the broth, discarding the anchovies, and return the clear broth to the pot.

3. Add the potatoes and onion to the broth. Bring to a boil, then reduce to a simmer and cook for 10 minutes until the potatoes are just starting to become tender.

4. Add the garlic, fish sauce, and guk-ganjang to the broth. Stir to combine.

5. Tear the dough. Take the rested dough from the refrigerator. Oil your hands lightly. Pull off small pieces of dough and stretch each piece thin between your fingers, aiming for irregular flat shapes about 3 to 5 cm across and as thin as you can manage without tearing holes. Drop each piece directly into the simmering broth. Work quickly, pulling and dropping, until all the dough is used.

6. Simmer the noodles for 5 to 7 minutes after the last piece goes in. The sujebi is done when the pieces float to the surface and look translucent rather than raw-white. Stir gently a few times to prevent sticking.

7. Taste the broth and adjust the seasoning with more fish sauce or guk-ganjang as needed.

8. Ladle into bowls. Top with sliced scallion and a drizzle of sesame oil. Serve immediately while the noodles are still tender.

## Why This Works

The anchovy-kelp broth provides a foundation of umami through two complementary mechanisms: inosinate from the anchovies and glutamate from the kelp. When these nucleotides meet, they create a synergistic effect that makes the broth taste far richer than either ingredient alone.

Resting the dough in the refrigerator relaxes the gluten network, making it easier to stretch thin without snapping back. The oil in the dough keeps it pliable and prevents the torn pieces from becoming tough. The irregular shapes cook at different rates, creating a pleasant textural contrast in the finished bowl. Thinner edges become silky while thicker centers remain slightly chewy.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Dried anchovies:** Large Korean soup anchovies (guk-myeolchi) are the backbone of many Korean broths. They provide calcium, iron, and omega-3 fatty acids. Removing the heads and intestinal strips before simmering eliminates bitterness.

**Kelp (dashima):** A natural source of glutamic acid, which is why it makes broths taste savory without adding salt. Kelp is also one of the richest dietary sources of iodine, essential for thyroid hormone production.

**Guk-ganjang:** Korean soup soy sauce is lighter in color and saltier than regular soy sauce (ganjang). It seasons broths without darkening them. It is typically made as a byproduct of doenjang production and has a more complex, fermented flavor than standard soy sauce.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Vegetarian version:** Replace the anchovy-kelp broth with a kelp and dried shiitake mushroom broth. Use soy sauce instead of fish sauce. The flavor profile will be different but still satisfying.

**Zucchini:** Half a medium zucchini, sliced into half-moons and added with the potatoes, is a common addition. It softens into the broth and adds a gentle sweetness.

**Egg:** Beating an egg and drizzling it into the simmering soup in the last minute of cooking creates silky egg ribbons throughout the broth.

**Dough variations:** Some cooks add a tablespoon of soybean powder (konggaru) to the flour for a nuttier flavor. Others use a mix of wheat and buckwheat flour.

## Serving Suggestions

Sujebi is a complete meal in itself but pairs well with simple banchan. [Kkakdugi](/recipes/kkakdugi) adds crunch and spice. [Kongnamul-muchim](/recipes/kongnamul-muchim) is a light, refreshing contrast. A small dish of [oi-muchim](/recipes/oi-muchim) on a warm day rounds things out. Keep the sides simple. This is humble food and does not need much around it.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store in a sealed container for up to 1 day. The noodles will absorb broth and swell as they sit, so the soup thickens considerably. Add water or broth when reheating to restore the consistency.

**Freezer:** Not recommended. The noodles become gummy and lose their texture after freezing.

**Reheating:** Warm in a pot over medium heat, adding water or broth as needed to thin the soup back out. The noodles will have absorbed much of the liquid overnight, so expect to add at least a cup. Sujebi is best eaten the day it is made.

**Dough storage:** Unused dough can be refrigerated for up to 2 days, wrapped tightly. Bring to room temperature for 10 minutes before tearing.
