# Ojingeochae Muchim (오징어채무침)

*Sweet and spicy dried shredded squid tossed in gochujang, garlic, sesame oil, and rice syrup, a no-cook banchan that keeps for weeks*

---

## Headnote

Ojingeochae muchim is the kind of banchan that sits quietly in the refrigerator, ready whenever you need it. There is no cooking involved, no heat required. You toss dried shredded squid in a sweet and spicy sauce, and it is done. The whole process takes about ten minutes, and the result keeps for weeks, the flavor actually improving as the sauce soaks deeper into the squid over time.

Dried shredded squid (ojingeochae) is a pantry staple in Korean households. The squid is dried and then pulled into thin, fibrous strips that have a pleasantly chewy texture. Eaten plain, it is a popular snack, especially with beer or soju. But dressed in this gochujang sauce, it becomes something more substantial, a banchan that holds its own alongside rice and other side dishes.

The sauce is straightforward: gochujang for fermented heat, rice syrup for a clean sweetness and glossy coating, garlic for punch, vegetable oil to help everything emulsify, and sesame oil for fragrance. The trick is to mix the sauce ingredients first until smooth and shiny, then toss the squid in the sauce rather than the other way around. This ensures even coating without clumping.

This is a banchan that Korean mothers pack in lunchboxes, set out at dinner tables, and keep stocked in the refrigerator as a reliable standby. It is also one of the easiest Korean dishes to make, requiring no special technique and no cooking equipment beyond a bowl and a pair of kitchen scissors. If you have access to a Korean grocery store and can find dried shredded squid, you can have this ready in the time it takes to set the table.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the squid
- 450 g dried shredded squid (ojingeochae, about 1 lb)

### For the sauce
- 130 g gochujang (about 1/2 cup)
- 4 cloves garlic, minced fine
- 80 ml vegetable or neutral oil (about 1/3 cup)
- 80 ml rice syrup (about 1/3 cup)
- 30 ml toasted sesame oil (2 tablespoons)
- 15 g toasted sesame seeds (about 2 tablespoons)

---

## Method

1. If the dried squid strips are longer than bite-size, cut them into 5 to 7 cm (2 to 3 inch) lengths using kitchen scissors. This makes them easier to eat and helps the sauce coat them more evenly.

2. Prepare the sauce. In a large mixing bowl, combine the gochujang, garlic, vegetable oil, rice syrup, and sesame oil. Stir vigorously until the sauce is smooth, glossy, and uniform in color. The oil and rice syrup should be fully incorporated with no visible separation.

3. Add the dried squid to the sauce. Toss with your hands or tongs, working the sauce into the squid strips until every piece is evenly coated. The squid should glisten with a thin layer of the red sauce. If the squid seems dry, add a touch more rice syrup or oil.

4. Sprinkle the sesame seeds over the dressed squid and toss once more to distribute.

5. Transfer to a clean jar or airtight container. The ojingeochae muchim is ready to eat immediately but will taste even better after a few hours in the refrigerator, once the sauce has had time to soften the squid slightly and the flavors meld.

---

## Why This Works

The combination of vegetable oil and rice syrup in the sauce creates a stable emulsion that clings to the dried squid rather than pooling at the bottom of the container. Rice syrup is viscous enough to coat the fibrous squid strips and provides a clean sweetness that balances the fermented saltiness of the gochujang without the graininess of granulated sugar.

The dried squid itself absorbs the sauce gradually over time, which is why this banchan improves with age. As the moisture from the sauce softens the outer fibers of the squid, the texture shifts from firmly chewy to pleasantly tender while remaining toothsome. The garlic mellows as it sits, and the sesame oil fragrance infuses throughout.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Dried shredded squid:** An excellent source of lean protein, with roughly 80% protein by dry weight. Dried squid also contains significant amounts of taurine, an amino acid that occurs naturally in animal tissues and has been studied for its role in cardiovascular function, bile salt formation, and antioxidant activity. It is also rich in vitamin B12, phosphorus, and selenium.

**Gochujang:** This fermented chili paste provides capsaicin from the chili powder, along with glutamic acid from the fermented soybean component, creating a complex flavor that is simultaneously spicy, sweet, salty, and savory. The fermentation also produces beneficial lactic acid bacteria.

**Rice syrup (jocheong):** A traditional Korean sweetener made by converting the starches in rice into sugars using malt enzyme. It has a milder sweetness than corn syrup and a clean flavor that does not compete with other ingredients. Its high viscosity makes it an ideal coating agent.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Dried squid alternatives:** Dried filefish (jwipo) can be prepared with the same sauce for a different texture. Some Korean markets also sell dried cuttlefish strips, which work identically.

**Spice level:** For a milder version, reduce the gochujang to 1/4 cup and increase the rice syrup slightly. For more heat, add 1 to 2 teaspoons of gochugaru to the sauce.

**Mayonnaise variation:** A popular modern twist adds 2 tablespoons of mayonnaise to the sauce, creating a creamier, slightly tangy version. This is especially popular as a snack with beer.

**Without rice syrup:** Corn syrup or honey can substitute, though the flavor will differ slightly. Honey adds its own floral notes, while corn syrup provides neutral sweetness. The consistency will be similar.

---

## Serving Suggestions

Ojingeochae muchim is a versatile banchan that appears at nearly any Korean meal. It pairs especially well with steamed rice and [doenjang-jjigae](/recipes/doenjang-jjigae) or [kimchi-jjigae](/recipes/kimchi-jjigae) as part of a simple home-cooked spread. It is equally at home as a snack with beer or soju, served on its own or with other anju (drinking snacks). Pack it in lunchboxes as a protein-rich side dish that does not need reheating.

---

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store in an airtight container for up to 3 to 4 weeks. The flavor continues to develop over the first few days as the sauce penetrates the squid. The texture softens gradually but remains pleasantly chewy for the full storage period.

**Freezer:** Not necessary given the long refrigerator shelf life, but the dressed squid can be frozen for up to 2 months if needed. Thaw in the refrigerator before serving.

**Reheating:** No reheating is needed. Ojingeochae muchim is served at room temperature or straight from the refrigerator. If the sauce has thickened after extended storage, toss with a small amount of sesame oil to refresh.
