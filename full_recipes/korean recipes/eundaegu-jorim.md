# Eundaegu-jorim (은대구조림)

*Black cod braised Korean-style with radish, gochugaru, and doenjang in a spicy-savory sauce*

---

## Headnote

Eundaegu-jorim is one of those dishes that Korean families bring out when the occasion calls for something special. Black cod, called eundaegu in Korean (literally "silver cod"), is a prized fish in Korean cuisine for its rich, buttery flesh that holds together during braising while absorbing the bold flavors of the sauce. Laid over a bed of Korean radish and surrounded by onion, scallion, and fresh chilies, the fish simmers in a spicy-savory liquid until the radish is translucent and the sauce has reduced to a concentrated glaze.

The dish is built in layers. Korean radish goes in first, providing a foundation that protects the fish from direct heat and contributes its own clean, slightly sweet flavor to the broth. The radish cooks for fifteen minutes in seasoned water until it begins to soften and turn translucent. Then the onion, chilies, and daepa (large Korean green onion) are arranged around the radish, the fish steaks are placed on top, and the sauce is poured over everything. Another fifteen to twenty minutes of covered braising finishes the dish.

The sauce is a defining feature. It combines the fermented depth of soy sauce with the fruity heat of gochugaru, the savory backbone of doenjang (fermented soybean paste), and the pungent kick of raw garlic. Sugar balances the salt and heat, and the braising liquid from the radish ties everything together. The result is a sauce that is simultaneously spicy, salty, sweet, and deeply umami, coating the fish and vegetables in a thin, flavorful glaze.

This is home cooking elevated to celebration status. In Korea, eundaegu-jorim appears at birthday dinners, holiday gatherings, and important family meals. The fish itself is not inexpensive, and the presentation, with the whole steaks visible through the sauce and the colorful chilies scattered across the top, signals that care and intention went into the preparation.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the base
- 900 g black cod (sablefish) steaks or fillets, about 2 lb, cut into 4 pieces
- 1 medium Korean radish (mu), about 300 g, peeled and sliced into 6 mm (1/4 inch) half-moons
- 1/2 medium onion, sliced into thick rings
- 2 to 3 stalks daepa (large Korean green onion) or 4 regular scallions, cut into 5 cm (2 inch) lengths
- 1 green chili, sliced diagonally
- 1 red chili, sliced diagonally
- 480 ml water (2 cups)

### For the sauce
- 60 ml soy sauce (4 tablespoons)
- 15 g garlic, minced (about 1 tablespoon)
- 15 g gochugaru (2 tablespoons)
- 30 g sugar (2 tablespoons)
- 5 g fresh ginger, minced (1 teaspoon)
- 10 g doenjang (2 teaspoons)

---

## Method

1. Prepare the sauce by combining the soy sauce, minced garlic, gochugaru, sugar, ginger, and doenjang in a small bowl. Stir until the doenjang is fully dissolved and the mixture is uniform. Set aside.

2. Arrange the radish slices in an even layer in the bottom of a large, shallow pot or braiser. Pour the water over the radish. Bring to a boil over medium-high heat, then reduce to medium. Cover and cook for 15 minutes until the radish is beginning to turn translucent and is tender when pierced with a knife.

3. Arrange the onion rings, daepa pieces, and half the sliced chilies over and around the radish. Place the black cod pieces on top of the vegetables, skin side up. Pour the prepared sauce evenly over the fish and vegetables.

4. Bring the liquid back to a gentle boil, then reduce the heat to medium-low. Cover and braise for 15 to 20 minutes. Every 5 minutes, carefully spoon the braising liquid over the top of the fish to baste it. The fish is done when it flakes easily and the radish is fully translucent.

5. Scatter the remaining sliced chilies over the top for color. Serve the dish directly from the pot or carefully transfer to a shallow serving bowl, making sure to spoon plenty of the braising liquid over everything.

## Why This Works

Cooking the radish first, before adding the fish, serves a dual purpose. The radish releases its own liquid as it cooks, adding a subtle sweetness to the braising water that complements the spicy sauce. It also creates a protective bed that keeps the delicate fish elevated from the bottom of the pot, preventing it from sticking or breaking apart from direct contact with the heat source.

The sauce benefits from the combination of two fermented products. Soy sauce provides salt and a straightforward umami, while doenjang contributes a deeper, funkier fermented flavor that adds complexity. The doenjang also thickens the sauce slightly, helping it cling to the fish rather than remaining as a thin broth.

Black cod's exceptionally high fat content, which can reach 15% or more, makes it ideal for braising. The fat keeps the flesh moist and silky even as the braising liquid reduces around it, and it carries the flavors of the gochugaru and garlic deep into each piece of fish. Leaner fish can dry out with this treatment, but black cod remains lush and buttery from start to finish.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Black cod (eundaegu):** One of the richest commercially available sources of omega-3 fatty acids, providing approximately 1,500 mg of combined EPA and DHA per 100 g. It is also an excellent source of selenium, vitamin D, and vitamin B12. Its deep-water habitat generally results in lower mercury accumulation compared to many other predatory fish species.

**Korean radish (mu):** Larger and denser than Japanese daikon, Korean radish has a slightly sweeter flavor and firmer texture that holds up well during braising. It is a good source of vitamin C and contains compounds called glucosinolates, which have been studied for their potential anti-inflammatory properties.

**Doenjang:** Korean fermented soybean paste is produced through a natural fermentation process using meju (dried soybean blocks). It contains beneficial bacteria from the fermentation process, along with isoflavones, saponins, and melanoidins. It is high in sodium, which is why no additional salt is needed in this recipe.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Fish alternatives:** Salmon, sea bass, or snapper can replace the black cod. Reduce the braising time by a few minutes for leaner fish to prevent drying. Cod (true Atlantic or Pacific cod) works but has a much leaner texture than black cod.

**Without daepa:** Regular scallions are the most common substitute. Leeks, cut into similar lengths, also work well and provide a milder sweetness.

**Spice level:** Reduce the gochugaru to 1 tablespoon for a milder version. For more heat, add a whole dried red chili or increase the fresh chilies. A tablespoon of gochujang can be stirred into the sauce for additional depth.

**Without doenjang:** Increase the soy sauce by 1 tablespoon and add a pinch of sugar to compensate for the lost fermented sweetness. The dish will be less complex but still flavorful.

---

## Serving Suggestions

Eundaegu-jorim is substantial enough to serve as the centerpiece of a Korean meal. Pair it with steamed white rice, which is essential for soaking up the spicy braising liquid. A mild banchan spread works best alongside this boldly flavored dish: [gyeran-jjim](/recipes/gyeran-jjim), [sigeumchi-namul](/recipes/sigeumchi-namul), and [kongnamul-muchim](/recipes/kongnamul-muchim) provide gentle contrast without competing. A bowl of light soup like [kongnamul-guk](/recipes/kongnamul-guk) rounds out the table.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store in an airtight container with the braising liquid and vegetables for up to 2 days. The flavors develop further overnight, making the leftovers arguably better than the fresh dish.

**Reheating:** Reheat gently in a covered pot or skillet over medium-low heat, spooning the sauce over the fish as it warms. Add a splash of water if the sauce has reduced too much. Avoid high heat, which can cause the fish to fall apart.

**Freezing:** Not recommended. The delicate texture of black cod deteriorates after freezing and thawing, and the radish becomes waterlogged.
