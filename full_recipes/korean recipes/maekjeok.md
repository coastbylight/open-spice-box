# Maekjeok (맥적)

*Doenjang-marinated pork skewers grilled until caramelized, one of the oldest Korean barbecue dishes and the ancestor of bulgogi*

---

## Headnote

Maekjeok is a dish that reaches back to the Goguryeo kingdom (37 BC to 668 AD), making it one of the oldest documented barbecue preparations in Korean culinary history. The name comes from the Maek tribe, the ethnic group associated with the founding of Goguryeo in the northern reaches of the Korean peninsula and parts of Manchuria. "Jeok" means skewered and grilled meat, a cooking method that has persisted in Korean cuisine for millennia.

What makes maekjeok historically significant is what it does not contain. There are no chili peppers, no gochugaru, no gochujang. Those ingredients arrived in Korea centuries later, likely in the 16th or 17th century via trade with the Portuguese and Japanese. Maekjeok predates all of that. Its flavor comes entirely from doenjang (fermented soybean paste), garlic, ginger, sesame oil, and rice syrup, ingredients that were staples of ancient Korean cooking.

The preparation is remarkably simple. Thin slices of pork shoulder are marinated in a paste of doenjang, rice syrup, mirim, sesame oil, minced onion, garlic, and ginger. Thirty minutes is enough for the flavors to penetrate, though longer is fine. The marinated pork is threaded onto skewers and grilled until the doenjang caramelizes into a deeply savory, slightly sweet crust.

Many food historians consider maekjeok the direct ancestor of modern [bulgogi](/recipes/bulgogi). The technique of marinating thin-sliced meat and grilling it over fire is the same. Only the seasoning base shifted over the centuries as new ingredients became available. Serving it over garlic chives seasoned with sesame oil and salt is traditional and keeps the dish grounded in its ancient roots.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the marinade
- 36 g doenjang (2 tablespoons)
- 30 ml rice syrup (2 tablespoons)
- 15 ml mirim (1 tablespoon)
- 10 ml toasted sesame oil (2 teaspoons)
- 60 g onion (about 3/4 cup), minced fine
- 4 cloves garlic, minced
- 5 g fresh ginger (about 1 teaspoon), minced
- 1/2 teaspoon ground black pepper

### For the pork
- 450 g pork shoulder (about 1 lb), sliced thin (3 to 5 mm thick) against the grain
- Bamboo or metal skewers (if using bamboo, soak in water for 30 minutes)

### For the garlic chive bed
- 120 g garlic chives (buchu), about 2 large handfuls
- 5 ml toasted sesame oil (1 teaspoon)
- Pinch of fine salt

---

## Method

1. Combine the doenjang, rice syrup, mirim, sesame oil, onion, garlic, ginger, and black pepper in a mixing bowl. Stir until the doenjang is fully incorporated and the marinade is uniform.

2. Add the pork slices to the marinade and toss to coat every piece thoroughly. Cover and refrigerate for at least 30 minutes and up to 4 hours. The doenjang will season the meat and begin to tenderize it.

3. While the pork marinates, prepare the garlic chive bed. Wash the chives and cut them into 5 cm (2-inch) lengths. Toss with sesame oil and a pinch of salt. Arrange on a serving platter and set aside.

4. Thread the marinated pork onto skewers, weaving the slices so they lie flat and will make good contact with the grill grates.

5. Heat a grill, grill pan, or cast iron skillet over high heat. If using a grill pan, brush lightly with oil to prevent sticking.

6. Grill the skewers for 3 to 4 minutes per side, until the doenjang marinade caramelizes and the pork develops deep golden-brown edges. The sugars in the rice syrup will create a glossy crust. Watch closely, as the marinade can burn quickly if the heat is too high.

7. Remove the skewers from the grill and place them directly on the bed of seasoned garlic chives. The heat from the pork will gently wilt the chives underneath.

8. Serve immediately with steamed rice and additional doenjang on the side for dipping if desired.

## Why This Works

Doenjang is a powerhouse of umami. As a fermented soybean paste, it contains high concentrations of free glutamic acid, which gives the pork a deep, savory flavor that penetrates the meat during marinating. When exposed to high heat on the grill, the amino acids and sugars in the doenjang undergo the Maillard reaction, creating the characteristic caramelized crust.

Rice syrup serves a dual purpose. It sweetens the marinade gently, balancing the saltiness of the doenjang, and its sugar content promotes browning on the grill. The mirim adds a subtle sweetness and helps dissolve the doenjang into a more uniform paste. Minced onion contributes moisture and natural enzymes that help tenderize the pork during the short marinating time.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Doenjang:** One of the foundational fermented foods of Korean cuisine, traditionally made by fermenting blocks of soybeans (meju) with salt for months or years. It is rich in protein, isoflavones, and beneficial bacteria from the fermentation process. Studies have investigated doenjang for potential anticancer and antioxidant properties, though these are preliminary findings.

**Garlic chives (buchu):** These flat-bladed chives have a stronger garlic flavor than common chives. They are a source of vitamin K, important for blood clotting, and contain allicin, the same sulfur compound found in garlic that has been studied for cardiovascular benefits.

**Pork shoulder:** The intramuscular fat in shoulder keeps the meat juicy during the intense heat of grilling. This cut is traditional for Korean grilled pork dishes because it provides the right balance of meat and fat.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Without a grill:** A cast iron skillet or broiler produces excellent results. For the skillet, cook the skewered pork over high heat, pressing down gently to ensure contact with the pan surface. For the broiler, place on a foil-lined sheet pan about 10 cm from the element, flipping once.

**Beef version:** Thinly sliced beef sirloin or ribeye can replace the pork. The marinating time can be reduced to 15 minutes for tender cuts.

**Without skewers:** The pork can be grilled as individual slices directly on the grates of a well-oiled grill, or stir-fried in a hot pan. Skewers make handling easier but are not essential.

**Modern twist:** Some contemporary Korean cooks add a tablespoon of gochujang to the marinade for heat, though this departs from the historical preparation.

---

## Serving Suggestions

Maekjeok is traditionally served over garlic chives, but it also pairs well with a bowl of steamed rice and simple banchan. [Baek-kimchi](/recipes/baek-kimchi) is an appropriate pairing, given that it is also a pre-chili preparation. [Sigeumchi-namul](/recipes/sigeumchi-namul) and [kongnamul-muchim](/recipes/kongnamul-muchim) round out the table with clean vegetable flavors. Lettuce wraps (ssam) with a dab of doenjang make an excellent way to eat the grilled pork.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store cooked maekjeok in a sealed container for up to 3 days. The doenjang marinade keeps the pork well-seasoned even when cold.

**Freezer:** Freeze the raw marinated pork (before grilling) for up to 1 month. Thaw in the refrigerator overnight and grill from cold. Cooked maekjeok can also be frozen for up to 1 month, though the texture is best when freshly grilled.

**Reheating:** Reheat under the broiler for 2 to 3 minutes per side to restore the caramelized crust. A microwave will work in a pinch but will not revive the charred edges. Prepare the garlic chive bed fresh when serving.
