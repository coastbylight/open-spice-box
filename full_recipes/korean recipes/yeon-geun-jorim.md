# Yeon-geun-jorim (연근조림)

*Lotus roots braised in soy sauce and rice syrup until glossy, chewy, and caramelized*

---

## Headnote

Yeon-geun-jorim is one of the most visually striking banchan on any Korean table. The lotus root, sliced crosswise to reveal its natural lace-like pattern of holes, turns dark and glossy as it braises in soy sauce and rice syrup. Each slice develops a satisfying chewiness that sits somewhere between crisp and tender, with a sweet-savory glaze that deepens in flavor the longer it sits.

Lotus root has been cultivated in Korea for centuries, growing in the muddy bottoms of ponds and marshes. The root's ability to produce a beautiful flower from murky water has long made it a symbol of purity in East Asian culture. In the kitchen, the symbolism gives way to practicality. Lotus root is starchy, mild in flavor, and takes on seasonings beautifully. Its firm texture holds up through long braising without turning mushy, making it ideal for jorim, the Korean technique of slowly simmering ingredients in a seasoned liquid until the sauce reduces to a concentrated glaze.

The method is unhurried but hands-off. After a quick boil with vinegar to keep the slices from discoloring, the lotus root goes into a pot with soy sauce, garlic, and water. It braises covered on low heat for forty minutes, then rice syrup joins for another twenty minutes of covered cooking. The final step is the most important: uncovering the pot and letting the liquid reduce on medium-high heat until it becomes thick and lacquer-like, coating each slice in a dark, shiny glaze. This last ten minutes of uncovered cooking is what transforms the dish from simply braised to truly caramelized.

The result is a banchan that tastes far more complex than its short ingredient list suggests. The soy sauce provides salt and umami, the rice syrup adds a rounded sweetness and that characteristic Korean gloss, and the sesame oil and seeds at the finish bring warmth and fragrance. It is a dish of patience rewarded.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the lotus root
- 450 g lotus root (about 1 lb), peeled and sliced 6 mm (1/4 inch) thick
- 15 ml rice vinegar (1 tablespoon, for boiling water)

### For the braising liquid
- 530 ml water (about 2 1/4 cups)
- 60 ml soy sauce (1/4 cup)
- 3 cloves garlic, minced

### For the glaze and finish
- 180 ml rice syrup (3/4 cup)
- 15 ml toasted sesame oil (1 tablespoon)
- 15 g toasted sesame seeds (about 1 tablespoon)

---

## Method

1. Bring a medium pot of water to a boil and add the rice vinegar. Add the lotus root slices and boil for 5 minutes. This brief blanch removes excess starch and the vinegar helps the lotus root maintain its pale color. Drain and rinse under cold water.

2. Return the lotus root slices to the pot. Add 2 tablespoons of vegetable or neutral oil and stir-fry over medium-high heat for about 3 minutes until the slices are lightly coated and beginning to turn slightly translucent at the edges.

3. Add the water, soy sauce, and minced garlic. Bring to a boil, then reduce the heat to low. Cover and simmer for 40 minutes, stirring occasionally. The lotus root should be tender but still have a pleasant bite.

4. Add the rice syrup and stir to combine. Cover and continue cooking on low heat for 20 minutes. The liquid will begin to thicken noticeably.

5. Remove the lid and increase the heat to medium-high. Cook uncovered for about 10 minutes, stirring frequently, until the liquid has reduced to a thick, glossy glaze that coats each lotus root slice. Watch carefully during this stage, as the sauce can burn quickly once it concentrates.

6. Remove from heat. Drizzle with the sesame oil and sprinkle with sesame seeds. Toss gently to coat. Let cool to room temperature before serving.

## Why This Works

The long, slow braise allows the soy sauce to penetrate deep into the lotus root's starchy flesh. Lotus root has a porous structure thanks to its air channels, and these channels act as tiny reservoirs that trap and hold the braising liquid as it reduces. This means the flavor is not just on the surface but throughout each slice.

Adding the rice syrup in a separate stage, after the initial braising, is deliberate. If added at the beginning, the sugar in the syrup would interfere with the soy sauce's ability to penetrate the root and could cause the slices to toughen prematurely. By introducing sweetness only after the lotus root has already absorbed the savory base, you get proper seasoning throughout with a sweet glaze on top.

The final uncovered reduction is what separates good yeon-geun-jorim from great. As the water evaporates, the concentration of soy sauce and rice syrup increases dramatically, creating a thick, lacquer-like coating. The high heat also triggers mild caramelization of the sugars in the rice syrup, adding depth and complexity to the glaze.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Lotus root:** A good source of dietary fiber, vitamin C, and potassium. The root contains unique polyphenols that have been studied for antioxidant activity. In traditional Korean and Chinese medicine, lotus root is considered cooling and is recommended for respiratory health and digestive support. Its high starch content makes it more filling than most vegetables.

**Rice syrup (jocheong):** This traditional sweetener is made by saccharifying cooked rice with malt enzyme. It provides complex carbohydrates rather than simple sugars, which gives it a milder glycemic impact than refined sugar. Its viscosity is what creates the glossy coating characteristic of Korean jorim dishes.

**Soy sauce:** Naturally fermented soy sauce contains free amino acids, particularly glutamic acid, which provides the umami backbone of this dish. It also contains melanoidins, the dark compounds formed during fermentation, which contribute both color and antioxidant properties.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Without lotus root:** Burdock root (ueong) is the most common alternative and is prepared using the same technique. It has an earthier flavor and a firmer texture. Sweet potato or kabocha squash can also be braised this way but will require shorter cooking times.

**Sweetener swap:** Corn syrup or honey can replace rice syrup, though the flavor will shift slightly. Honey adds floral notes, while corn syrup is more neutral. The consistency and gloss will be similar.

**Added spice:** Some versions include a small amount of gochugaru or a few slices of dried red chili in the braising liquid for gentle heat.

**Soy sauce variation:** A mix of regular soy sauce and dark soy sauce (1:1 ratio) will produce a deeper color and slightly more complex flavor.

## Serving Suggestions

Yeon-geun-jorim is a banchan that complements nearly any Korean main dish. Its sweet-savory flavor and chewy texture make it an excellent contrast to spicy dishes like [kimchi-jjigae](/recipes/kimchi-jjigae) or [dakdoritang](/recipes/dakdoritang). Serve alongside steamed rice and a spread of other side dishes for a traditional Korean home meal. Its visual appeal, with those distinctive lotus root holes filled with dark glaze, makes it a natural choice for special occasion tables and holiday spreads.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store in an airtight container for up to 2 weeks. The flavor continues to develop over the first two to three days as the glaze settles into the lotus root. The texture remains pleasantly chewy throughout the storage period.

**Freezer:** Can be frozen for up to 2 months. Thaw in the refrigerator overnight before serving. The texture may soften slightly after freezing.

**Reheating:** Yeon-geun-jorim is typically served at room temperature or cold from the refrigerator. If you prefer it warm, reheat gently in a small saucepan over low heat with a splash of water to loosen the glaze. Avoid microwaving, which can make the edges tough.
