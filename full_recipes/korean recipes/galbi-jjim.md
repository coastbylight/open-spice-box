# Galbi-jjim (갈비찜)

*Beef short ribs braised with carrots, radish, chestnuts, and jujubes in a sweet soy-garlic sauce until fall-off-the-bone tender*

---

## Headnote

Galbi-jjim is the dish that Korean families bring out for Chuseok, Seollal, birthdays, and any occasion that deserves something more than the everyday. Beef short ribs are soaked to draw out blood, blanched to remove impurities, and then braised low and slow in a sauce of soy, mirin, sugar, garlic, and ginger until the meat is so tender it barely clings to the bone. Dried shiitake mushrooms, whole chestnuts, jujubes, and carefully rounded balls of radish and carrot join the pot at different stages, each one contributing its own flavor and texture to the finished dish.

The presentation is part of what makes galbi-jjim special. In traditional Korean cooking, the vegetables for this dish are shaped into perfect spheres or ovals using a melon baller or careful knife work. Carrots and radish are cut into rounds and then their edges are pared away until they are smooth and egg-shaped. This is not mere decoration. The rounded shapes represent prosperity and completeness in Korean food culture, and they signal to everyone at the table that this meal was prepared with care and intention. If the process feels too fussy, cutting the vegetables into large chunks works just as well for flavor, though the dish loses some of its ceremonial quality.

The braising liquid starts with dried shiitake mushrooms soaked in water. The mushroom soaking liquid becomes the base of the sauce, adding a layer of umami that beef broth alone cannot match. Soy sauce and mirin provide salt and sweetness, while a generous amount of garlic and ginger cut through the richness of the short ribs. The sauce reduces over the long braising time into a thick, dark glaze that coats each rib and vegetable.

The final minutes of cooking are dedicated to building the glaze. Rice syrup is added near the end for its characteristic Korean sheen, and the heat is raised to reduce the sauce until it turns thick and lacquer-like. The short ribs emerge dark, glossy, and intensely flavored, surrounded by tender vegetables and studded with chestnuts and ruby-red jujubes. A scattering of pine nuts and ginkgo nuts over the top provides the finishing touch. This is a dish that takes time but rewards every minute of it.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For soaking
- 8 beef short ribs, cut flanken-style or English-cut, about 1.4 kg (3 lb) total
- 4 dried shiitake mushrooms

### For the braising sauce
- 480 ml shiitake mushroom soaking water (2 cups), strained
- 80 ml soy sauce (1/3 cup)
- 120 ml mirin (1/2 cup)
- 50 g sugar (1/4 cup)
- 1/2 teaspoon black pepper
- 8 cloves garlic, minced
- 15 g fresh ginger, minced (about 1 tablespoon)

### For the vegetables and garnish
- 4 soaked shiitake mushroom caps (from above), halved
- 8 to 10 whole peeled chestnuts (fresh, frozen, or vacuum-packed)
- 200 g Korean radish, cut into large chunks or rounded into balls
- 200 g carrots, cut into large chunks or rounded into balls
- 8 dried jujubes (daechu)
- 30 ml rice syrup (2 tablespoons)
- 15 ml toasted sesame oil (1 tablespoon)
- 15 g pine nuts (about 1 tablespoon)
- 10 to 12 ginkgo nuts (optional, canned or vacuum-packed)

---

## Method

1. Place the short ribs in a large bowl and cover with cold water. Soak for 30 to 60 minutes, changing the water once or twice, until the water runs mostly clear. This draws out excess blood and produces a cleaner-tasting braise. In a separate bowl, soak the dried shiitake mushrooms in 2 cups of warm water for at least 30 minutes until fully hydrated. Reserve the soaking liquid and strain it through a fine mesh strainer. Remove the stems from the mushrooms and halve the caps.

2. Drain the short ribs. Bring a large pot of water to a boil. Add the ribs and blanch for 5 minutes, skimming any foam that rises to the surface. Drain the ribs, rinse them under cold running water, and clean the pot.

3. Prepare the braising sauce. In a bowl, combine the strained mushroom soaking water, soy sauce, mirin, sugar, black pepper, minced garlic, and minced ginger. Stir until the sugar dissolves.

4. Return the blanched short ribs to the clean pot. Pour the braising sauce over the ribs. Bring to a boil over medium-high heat, then reduce the heat to low. Cover and braise for 20 minutes, turning the ribs once.

5. Add the shiitake mushroom caps, chestnuts, radish pieces, and carrot pieces to the pot. Arrange them around the ribs so they are partially submerged in the liquid. Cover and continue braising on low heat for 1 hour, turning the ribs and stirring the vegetables gently every 20 minutes. The meat should be very tender and nearly falling off the bone.

6. Add the jujubes and rice syrup to the pot. Stir gently to distribute. Cover and cook for 10 more minutes.

7. Remove the lid and increase the heat to medium-high. Cook uncovered for 5 to 10 minutes, spooning the sauce over the ribs and vegetables frequently, until the sauce has reduced to a thick, glossy glaze that coats everything.

8. Remove from heat. Drizzle with sesame oil and toss gently. Transfer to a large serving bowl or platter. Garnish with pine nuts and ginkgo nuts. Serve immediately with steamed rice.

## Why This Works

The soaking and blanching of the short ribs is a two-stage purification process that Korean cooks consider essential. Soaking draws blood out of the bone and marrow gradually, while the blanching step forces remaining impurities and blood proteins to coagulate and float to the surface. Skipping either step produces a murky, greasy sauce with an unpleasant gamey flavor that no amount of seasoning can mask.

The mushroom soaking liquid is more than just a convenient braising base. Dried shiitake mushrooms are one of the richest natural sources of guanylate, a nucleotide that amplifies umami perception when combined with the glutamate found in soy sauce. Using the soaking liquid captures all of the flavor compounds that leached out during rehydration, creating a braising liquid with an unusually deep savory character.

Adding the vegetables in stages ensures that each component reaches its ideal texture. The radish and carrot need a full hour of braising to become tender and translucent, while the jujubes need only ten minutes to soften. Adding everything at once would result in mushy jujubes and underdone root vegetables.

The final uncovered reduction concentrates the sauce and triggers caramelization of the sugars from the mirin, rice syrup, and sugar. This creates the dark, lacquer-like glaze that is the hallmark of a well-made galbi-jjim.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Beef short ribs:** Rich in protein, iron, zinc, and vitamin B12. Short ribs are a fattier cut of beef, with marbling that contributes to their tenderness during braising. The connective tissue in short ribs is primarily collagen, which converts to gelatin during long cooking, creating the silky, body-rich sauce characteristic of this dish.

**Dried shiitake mushrooms:** Contain lentinan, a polysaccharide that has been studied for immune-modulating properties. They are also one of the few non-animal sources of vitamin D (when sun-dried) and provide significant amounts of B vitamins, selenium, and copper.

**Chestnuts:** Lower in fat than most nuts, chestnuts are primarily composed of complex carbohydrates. They provide vitamin C, folate, and manganese. In Korean food culture, chestnuts are associated with autumn and are a standard component of holiday and celebratory dishes.

**Jujubes (daechu):** Rich in vitamin C, potassium, and various antioxidant compounds. In Korean and Chinese traditional medicine, jujubes are considered a warming food that supports digestion and overall vitality. They are a fixture of Korean celebratory and restorative cooking.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Meat alternatives:** Bone-in beef chuck or oxtail can replace short ribs. Both require longer braising, approximately 2 to 2.5 hours, to become tender. Boneless short rib meat will cook faster but lacks the gelatin contribution from the bones.

**Without chestnuts:** Ginkgo nuts can be increased, or the chestnuts can simply be omitted. Some versions add cubed sweet potato as a starchy substitute.

**Without jujubes:** Dried dates or dried apricots can approximate the sweetness, though the flavor will be different. Jujubes can also be omitted entirely without significantly changing the dish.

**Simplified vegetables:** If rounding the vegetables into balls feels like too much work, cutting them into large chunks or thick half-moons is perfectly acceptable. The rounded shapes are traditional for special occasions but are not required for flavor.

**Pressure cooker adaptation:** The braising time can be reduced to about 30 to 40 minutes in a pressure cooker. Add the vegetables for the last 10 minutes of cooking at natural release.

## Serving Suggestions

Galbi-jjim is the centerpiece of a Korean special occasion meal. Serve it with steamed white rice, which is essential for absorbing the rich sauce. Surround it with a selection of banchan: [tongbaechu-kimchi](/recipes/tongbaechu-kimchi), [sigeumchi-namul](/recipes/sigeumchi-namul), [kongnamul-muchim](/recipes/kongnamul-muchim), and [kkakdugi](/recipes/kkakdugi) are traditional choices. A light soup like [miyeok-guk](/recipes/miyeok-guk) provides a refreshing counterpoint to the richness of the braised ribs.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store in an airtight container with the sauce for up to 4 days. The sauce will solidify into a firm jelly as it cools, which is a sign of the gelatin extracted from the bones. The flavor deepens significantly on the second day, and many Korean cooks consider reheated galbi-jjim superior to freshly made.

**Reheating:** Reheat gently in a covered pot over medium-low heat, adding a splash of water if the sauce has reduced too much. Stir carefully to avoid breaking the vegetables. The jelly-like sauce will melt back into a glossy glaze as it warms.

**Freezing:** Can be frozen for up to 2 months. Freeze the ribs and sauce together in an airtight container. Thaw overnight in the refrigerator before reheating. The vegetables may soften slightly after freezing, but the overall quality remains high.
