# Dubu-jorim (두부조림)

*Firm tofu pan-fried golden then braised in a spicy soy-gochugaru sauce with garlic and scallions*

---

## Headnote

Dubu-jorim sits near the top of any list of essential Korean banchan. It appears at family dinners and packed lunches, in restaurant banchan spreads and late-night refrigerator raids. The dish is nothing more than slabs of tofu, fried until golden and then simmered briefly in a sauce of soy, gochugaru, garlic, and scallion. It takes about twenty-five minutes from cutting board to table, and the result is deeply satisfying in the way that only simple food done well can be.

The success of dubu-jorim depends almost entirely on two things: getting a proper sear on the tofu and building a sauce with the right balance. The sear creates a thin crust that gives each piece structure and a faintly nutty flavor. Without it, the tofu would simply fall apart in the sauce. The sauce itself is a classic Korean combination of salty, sweet, spicy, and aromatic, with soy sauce providing the savory foundation, a touch of sugar rounding the edges, gochugaru adding gentle heat and color, and garlic and scallions lifting everything with freshness.

Korean tofu, sold as dubu, tends to be slightly denser and more flavorful than many Western brands. If you have access to a Korean grocery store, look for the packages labeled for pan-frying or braising (buchimdang or jorimdang). Any firm or extra-firm tofu will work, but the Korean varieties hold their shape better during searing and absorb the sauce more evenly.

This is a forgiving recipe. The tofu does not need to be perfectly golden on every surface. The sauce does not need to reduce to an exact consistency. As long as you pat the tofu dry before frying and let the sauce simmer long enough to penetrate the slabs, you will end up with something that tastes like it belongs on a Korean dinner table. It is the kind of banchan that makes a bowl of plain rice feel like a complete meal.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the tofu
- 400 to 510 g firm tofu (14 to 18 oz, one standard block)
- 15 ml vegetable oil (1 tablespoon)

### For the sauce
- 45 ml soy sauce (3 tablespoons)
- 45 ml water (3 tablespoons)
- 15 ml toasted sesame oil (1 tablespoon)
- 5 g sugar (1 teaspoon)
- 5 g gochugaru (1 teaspoon)
- 5 g toasted sesame seeds (1 teaspoon)
- 2 cloves garlic, minced fine
- 2 scallions, thinly sliced (white and green parts separated)

---

## Method

1. Remove the tofu from its packaging and drain. Wrap the block in a double layer of paper towels or a clean kitchen towel. Place on a cutting board and press gently to remove excess moisture, letting it sit for about 5 minutes. Cut the tofu crosswise into slabs about 1 cm (1/2 inch) thick. Pat each slab dry with paper towels.

2. Prepare the sauce. In a small bowl, combine the soy sauce, water, sesame oil, sugar, gochugaru, sesame seeds, minced garlic, and the white parts of the scallions. Stir until the sugar is dissolved.

3. Heat the vegetable oil in a large non-stick skillet or well-seasoned cast iron pan over medium-high heat. When the oil shimmers, arrange the tofu slabs in a single layer without crowding. Cook undisturbed for 3 to 4 minutes until the bottom is golden brown. Flip carefully and cook the other side for another 3 to 4 minutes until equally golden. Work in batches if necessary.

4. Reduce the heat to medium-low. Pour the sauce evenly over the tofu in the pan. Let the sauce come to a gentle simmer. Spoon sauce over the tops of the tofu slabs as it bubbles. Continue simmering for 3 to 4 minutes, occasionally spooning sauce over the tofu, until the liquid has reduced by about half and the tofu has absorbed some of the flavor.

5. Scatter the green parts of the scallions over the tofu. Give the pan a gentle shake to distribute. Remove from heat and transfer to a serving plate, spooning any remaining sauce from the pan over the top.

## Why This Works

Patting the tofu dry before frying is the single most important step. Surface moisture creates a barrier of steam between the tofu and the hot oil, preventing the Maillard reaction that produces the golden crust. Dry tofu sears quickly and evenly, creating a thin, slightly chewy exterior that contrasts with the soft interior.

The sauce is intentionally thin when it first goes into the pan. This allows it to flow around and beneath the tofu slabs, making contact with all surfaces. As it simmers and reduces, the soy sauce concentrates and the sugar and rice syrup components in the gochugaru create a light glaze. The tofu acts like a sponge during this process, drawing the seasoned liquid into its porous structure through the crust that was created during searing.

Separating the scallion whites and greens is a small detail that makes a noticeable difference. The whites cook in the sauce and mellow into a sweet, aromatic base. The greens, added at the very end, stay bright and fresh, providing color and a sharp contrast to the warm, salty-sweet tofu.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Tofu:** A complete protein source containing all essential amino acids, with roughly 10 g of protein per 100 g serving. Firm tofu is also a good source of calcium (especially if made with calcium sulfate), iron, and manganese. Soy isoflavones, particularly genistein and daidzein, have been extensively studied for their potential cardiovascular and bone health benefits.

**Gochugaru:** Korean red pepper flakes have a fruity, slightly smoky flavor that distinguishes them from other chili flakes. They contain capsaicin, which has been researched for anti-inflammatory and metabolic effects. The coarser grind used in Korean cooking provides texture rather than just heat.

**Sesame oil:** Toasted sesame oil is rich in sesamol and sesamin, compounds with studied antioxidant properties. It is used as a finishing oil rather than a cooking fat because its flavor compounds break down at high temperatures.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Tofu firmness:** Extra-firm tofu will hold its shape even better during searing but will absorb less sauce. Medium-firm is acceptable but requires more careful handling. Silken tofu is not suitable for this recipe.

**Vegan adaptation:** This recipe is naturally vegan as written. For a fully plant-based version, verify that your soy sauce does not contain fish-based additives (some Korean soy sauces do).

**Spice level:** Increase gochugaru to 1 tablespoon for more heat, or omit it entirely for a non-spicy version. Adding a teaspoon of gochujang to the sauce creates a richer, more complex flavor.

**Protein addition:** Ground pork or beef can be browned in the pan before adding the tofu, creating a heartier version that works as a main dish rather than a side.

---

## Serving Suggestions

Dubu-jorim is a banchan that pairs with almost anything. Serve it alongside steamed rice and [kimchi-jjigae](/recipes/kimchi-jjigae) for a classic weeknight dinner. It complements mild soups like [kongnamul-guk](/recipes/kongnamul-guk) and richer main dishes like [bulgogi](/recipes/bulgogi) equally well. For a simple lunch, dubu-jorim over a bowl of hot rice with a fried egg on top makes a satisfying and protein-rich meal.

---

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store in an airtight container for up to 4 days. The tofu will continue to absorb the sauce as it sits, becoming more flavorful but also slightly softer.

**Reheating:** Reheat gently in a skillet over medium-low heat with a splash of water, or microwave in 30-second intervals. The tofu reheats well and maintains its texture for the first few days.

**Freezing:** Not recommended. Freezing and thawing tofu changes its texture dramatically, making it spongy and porous. While some dishes benefit from this transformation, dubu-jorim does not.
