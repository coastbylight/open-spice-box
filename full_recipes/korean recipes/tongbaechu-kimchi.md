# Napa Cabbage Kimchi (통배추김치)

*The foundational Korean ferment of salted napa cabbage packed with gochugaru, garlic, ginger, and fish sauce, aged to complex, tangy perfection*

---

## Headnote

This is the most important recipe in Korean cooking. That is not an exaggeration. Kimchi is not a side dish or a condiment in the way that pickles or sauerkraut function in Western meals. It is the structural foundation of the Korean table, present at virtually every meal, its flavors woven so deeply into the cuisine that dozens of other dishes depend on it as a primary ingredient. Kimchi-jjigae, kimchi fried rice, kimchi pancakes, kimchi dumplings: these are not ways to use up leftover kimchi. They are dishes that exist because kimchi exists.

The tradition of making kimchi in large batches is called kimjang. Historically, this was a late autumn communal event where families and neighbors gathered to prepare enough kimchi to last through winter. In 2013, UNESCO recognized kimjang as an Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity, acknowledging it as a practice that strengthens community bonds and passes knowledge between generations. Even today, many Korean families set aside a weekend in November or December to make their year's supply, filling dedicated kimchi refrigerators that maintain the ideal fermentation temperature.

Freshly made kimchi, before fermentation has begun, is called geotjeori. It tastes bright, garlicky, and peppery, more like a spicy salad than the tangy, funky kimchi most people picture. As it ferments over days, weeks, and months, lactic acid bacteria convert the sugars in the vegetables into acid, developing the characteristic sour depth. Young kimchi, fermented for just a few days, is crisp and mildly tangy, perfect for eating alongside grilled meat. Well-aged kimchi, soft and deeply sour after months in the refrigerator, is what you want for cooking. A proper Korean kitchen has kimchi at every stage of its life, each one suited to a different purpose.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For salting the cabbage
- 2 large heads napa cabbage (about 4 to 5 lb / 2 kg total)
- 1 cup coarse sea salt (Korean solar salt preferred)

### For the rice flour paste
- 2 cups water
- 2 tbsp glutinous rice flour (sweet rice flour)
- 2 tbsp sugar

### For the kimchi paste
- 1 cup gochugaru (Korean red pepper flakes)
- 1/4 to 1/2 cup fish sauce, to taste
- 24 cloves garlic (about 2 heads), minced or processed to a paste
- 1 inch piece fresh ginger, peeled and finely grated
- 1 medium onion, roughly chopped and blended to a puree

### For the vegetables
- 2 cups Korean radish or daikon, cut into thin matchsticks
- 1 cup carrot, cut into thin matchsticks
- 1 cup scallions, cut into 1.5-inch lengths
- 1 cup Asian chives (buchu), cut into 1.5-inch lengths

---

## Method

1. Quarter each head of napa cabbage lengthwise, keeping the root end intact so the leaves stay connected. Rinse the quarters under cold water to dampen them.

2. Sprinkle the coarse sea salt between the leaves of each quarter, concentrating more salt on the thick, white base portions and less on the thinner green leaf tips. The thick stems need more salt to draw out moisture and soften properly.

3. Place the salted cabbage quarters in a large basin or clean plastic tub. Let them sit at room temperature for 6 to 8 hours, flipping them over halfway through. The cabbage is properly salted when the thick white parts bend easily without snapping. They should feel flexible like a thick rubber band rather than rigid or crispy.

4. Rinse each cabbage quarter thoroughly under cold running water three times, gently separating the leaves to flush out excess salt from between them. Squeeze out as much water as possible and set them cut-side down on a rack or in a colander to drain for 30 minutes.

5. While the cabbage drains, make the rice flour paste. Combine the water and glutinous rice flour in a small saucepan. Whisk until smooth and place over medium heat. Cook, stirring constantly, until the mixture thickens and begins to bubble, about 3 to 4 minutes. Stir in the sugar until dissolved. Remove from heat and let cool completely. This paste acts as a binder for the kimchi seasoning and provides food for the lactobacillus bacteria during fermentation.

6. In a large mixing bowl, combine the cooled rice flour paste with the gochugaru, fish sauce, garlic paste, grated ginger, and blended onion. Mix thoroughly into a uniform, thick red paste. Taste it. The paste should be intensely salty, spicy, and savory. It will mellow considerably once it is spread on the cabbage and begins to ferment.

7. Add the radish matchsticks, carrot matchsticks, scallions, and Asian chives to the kimchi paste. Toss everything together until the vegetables are evenly coated.

8. Put on rubber or food-safe gloves. Take one cabbage quarter and spread the kimchi paste between every leaf, working from the outermost leaves inward. Use a generous amount on each leaf, making sure to coat the thick stem portions as well as the green tips. When every leaf is coated, fold the outer leaves over the quarter and tuck it into a tight bundle.

9. Repeat with all remaining cabbage quarters. Pack the bundles tightly into a large glass jar, ceramic crock, or food-safe plastic container, pressing down firmly to eliminate air pockets. The cabbage should be submerged in its own liquid as much as possible. Leave at least 1 to 2 inches of headspace, as the kimchi will expand as carbon dioxide is produced during fermentation.

10. Press a piece of plastic wrap directly onto the surface of the kimchi, then seal the container with its lid. Do not seal it airtight if using a jar without an airlock, as the fermentation will produce gas that needs to escape. Leave the lid slightly loose or open it briefly once a day to release pressure.

11. Leave the container at room temperature (around 65 to 75°F / 18 to 24°C) for 1 to 2 days. You will see small bubbles forming, and the kimchi will start to smell tangy rather than just garlicky and spicy. Taste it daily. When it reaches a level of sourness you enjoy, transfer it to the refrigerator, where fermentation will slow dramatically but continue to develop flavor over weeks and months.

## Why This Works

The salting step accomplishes two things. First, it draws water out of the cabbage through osmosis, concentrating the flavor and creating a crunchier texture. Second, the salt concentration suppresses harmful bacteria while allowing beneficial lactobacillus to thrive. These lactic acid bacteria are naturally present on the cabbage leaves and are responsible for the fermentation that gives kimchi its characteristic tang.

The rice flour paste serves as a carbohydrate source that feeds the lactobacillus during the early stages of fermentation, helping them establish a dominant colony before any competing organisms can gain a foothold. The sugar in the paste accelerates this process.

Fish sauce provides both salt and glutamates, the amino acids responsible for umami. Traditional recipes often use salted shrimp (saeujeot) in addition to or instead of fish sauce. Both accomplish the same goal of deepening the savory dimension of the finished kimchi.

Packing the kimchi tightly and minimizing air exposure promotes anaerobic fermentation, which is the type that produces lactic acid. Aerobic conditions would instead promote mold growth and off-flavors.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Gochugaru** is not interchangeable with other chili flakes. Korean red pepper flakes have a specific texture, somewhere between flakes and powder, and a flavor that balances fruity sweetness with moderate heat. They are sun-dried and have a vibrant red color that gives kimchi its characteristic appearance.

**Fermented foods** like kimchi are rich in live lactobacillus bacteria, which research has associated with improved gut health, enhanced immune function, and better nutrient absorption. These benefits are most pronounced in kimchi that has not been heat-treated or pasteurized after fermentation.

## Substitutions and Variations

- **Vegan kimchi:** Omit the fish sauce and substitute with soy sauce or Korean soup soy sauce (guk-ganjang). Add a sheet of dried kelp to the rice flour paste while cooking to boost umami, then discard it.
- **Milder version:** Reduce the gochugaru to 1/2 cup and increase the sugar in the paste to 3 tablespoons.
- **Quick version (geotjeori):** Salt the cabbage for only 2 hours, mix with the paste, and eat immediately as a fresh salad. This will not have the fermented tang but is delicious in its own right.
- **Without Asian chives:** Increase the scallions to 2 cups. The flavor will be slightly different but still excellent.

---

## Serving Suggestions

Young, freshly fermented kimchi is best eaten as banchan alongside grilled meats such as [Bossam](/recipes/bossam) or [Bulgogi](/recipes/bulgogi), where its crunch and acidity cut through the richness. Well-aged kimchi, sour and soft after months of fermentation, is ideal for cooking into [Kimchi-jjigae](/recipes/kimchi-jjigae) or [Kimchi Fried Rice](/recipes/kimchi-fried-rice). Keep jars at different stages of fermentation so you always have the right kimchi for the right dish.

---

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Kimchi stored in a sealed container in the refrigerator will continue to ferment slowly and remain good for 6 months to a year or longer. The flavor becomes progressively more sour and complex over time. Always use clean utensils when removing kimchi from the container, and press the remaining kimchi back down to minimize air exposure.

**Freezing:** Not recommended, as freezing kills the beneficial bacteria and dramatically changes the texture of the cabbage.
