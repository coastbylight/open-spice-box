# Larb Gai (ลาบไก่)

*Thai chicken lettuce wraps with lime, fish sauce, mint, and cilantro, served in crisp lettuce cups with toasted rice powder and crushed peanuts*

---

## Headnote

Larb is the national dish of Laos and one of the most popular salads in Thai cooking, a category of food that barely exists in the Western sense of the word. It is not leafy greens with dressing. It is a warm, intensely seasoned ground meat dish built on a foundation of three flavors: lime juice for sour, fish sauce for salty, and sugar for sweet, with enough fresh chili to make your eyes water and enough fresh herbs to make your nose happy at the same time.

The aromatics do the heavy lifting. Ginger, garlic, lemongrass, and chili go into hot oil for barely a minute, just long enough to turn fragrant and golden without burning. The ground chicken follows and cooks fast, broken into small crumbles that catch the sauce in every crevice. The sauce itself is almost nothing: lime juice, fish sauce, sugar, and a little starch to help it cling. But when those three flavors hit in the right ratio, with the warmth of the aromatics underneath, the result is one of the most complete bites in any cuisine.

The fresh herbs go in off-heat. Mint and cilantro, roughly torn or chopped, along with thin slices of red onion that still have their sharp bite. Everything gets spooned into crisp lettuce cups, the kind that crunch when you pick them up and shatter slightly as you bite through to the warm filling inside. The traditional version uses toasted rice powder for texture, rice grains dry-roasted in a wok until dark golden and then ground to a coarse powder. Crushed peanuts work as a simpler alternative and add their own richness. Either way, the contrast of temperatures and textures, warm meat and cool herbs and crisp lettuce, is what makes this dish more than the sum of its ingredients.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the sauce
- 2 tbsp toasted rice powder (see note below) or 2 tsp cornstarch
- 3 tbsp water
- 2 1/2 tbsp fresh lime juice (1 to 2 limes)
- 2 tbsp fish sauce
- 2 tsp brown sugar or palm sugar

### For the filling
- 2 tbsp peanut oil (or other neutral high-heat oil)
- 1 tbsp fresh ginger, grated or very finely chopped
- 2 large cloves garlic, minced
- 1 stalk lemongrass, white and pale green part only, finely chopped
- 2 Thai bird's eye chilies, deseeded and finely chopped (adjust to taste)
- 500 g ground chicken (about 1 lb)

### For finishing
- 1/2 red onion, quartered and thinly sliced
- 1/3 cup fresh cilantro leaves, roughly chopped, plus extra for garnish
- 1/3 cup fresh mint leaves, roughly torn, plus extra for garnish
- 3 tbsp crushed roasted peanuts

### For serving
- 6 to 8 small lettuce leaves (baby romaine or butter lettuce, choose ones with a natural cup shape)
- Lime wedges
- Extra sliced chili

---

## Method

1. Make the sauce. If using toasted rice powder, combine it with the water in a small bowl and stir. If using cornstarch, do the same. Add the lime juice, fish sauce, and sugar. Stir until the sugar dissolves. Set aside.

2. Cook the aromatics. Heat the peanut oil in a wok or large skillet over medium-high heat. Add the ginger, garlic, lemongrass, and chilies. Saute for 45 seconds to 1 minute until fragrant and just beginning to turn golden. Do not let the garlic brown, as it will taste bitter.

3. Cook the chicken. Add the ground chicken and turn the heat to high. Break the meat into small crumbles with a spatula, pressing it against the wok to separate the pieces. Cook for 3 to 4 minutes until the chicken is white and nearly cooked through. There should be no pink remaining.

4. Add the sauce. Pour the sauce over the chicken and cook for another 45 seconds to 1 minute, tossing constantly, until the sauce coats the chicken and thickens slightly into a glossy glaze.

5. Add the fresh herbs. Remove the wok from heat. Stir in the sliced red onion, cilantro, and mint. The residual heat will wilt the herbs just slightly without cooking them. Toss to combine.

6. Serve. Transfer the filling to a bowl. Arrange the lettuce cups on a plate alongside the crushed peanuts, extra herbs, lime wedges, and sliced chili. Let everyone assemble their own: spoon filling into a lettuce cup, top with peanuts and herbs, squeeze lime over the top, and eat.

### Toasted Rice Powder

The traditional thickener and textural element in larb. To make it: heat a dry wok or skillet over high heat. Add 2 tablespoons of uncooked rice (any variety works, jasmine is traditional). Dry-roast, stirring constantly, for about 5 minutes until the rice turns a deep golden brown and smells nutty and toasted. Transfer to a mortar and pestle or spice grinder and pound or grind to a coarse powder. It should have visible texture, not be ground to flour. Toasted rice powder adds a subtle smokiness and a sandy crunch that cornstarch cannot replicate. It keeps in a sealed jar for weeks.

## Why This Works

The sauce ratio of lime juice, fish sauce, and sugar is the foundation of Thai salad dressing. The three flavors check each other: the acid of the lime, the salt and umami of the fish sauce, and the sweetness of the sugar. When balanced correctly, none of them dominates. You taste all three simultaneously, which is the sensation that makes Thai food Thai.

Cooking the aromatics before the meat means the ginger, garlic, lemongrass, and chili infuse the oil, and that flavored oil then coats every crumble of chicken. This distributes the aromatic flavors more evenly than adding them after the meat.

The fresh herbs and onion go in off-heat for a reason. Mint and cilantro lose their bright, volatile flavors when cooked. The residual heat in the wok is enough to soften them slightly and release their aromas without destroying the essential oils that make them taste fresh.

The lettuce serves as both vessel and ingredient. The cold crunch of the lettuce against the warm, intensely seasoned filling creates a temperature and texture contrast that makes each bite feel layered and complete.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Lemongrass:** The essential oil in lemongrass, citral, gives it its distinctive citrusy flavor and has been studied for antibacterial and antifungal properties. In Thai traditional medicine, lemongrass tea is used as a digestive aid and fever reducer. The tough outer layers must be peeled away to reach the tender, aromatic core.

**Fish sauce:** A fermented condiment that is the primary source of salt and umami in Thai cooking. High-quality fish sauce (made from anchovies and salt, aged for a year or more) contains amino acids, B vitamins, and minerals produced during fermentation. It provides a savory depth that salt alone cannot.

**Mint:** Fresh mint contains menthol, which provides a cooling sensation on the tongue and has documented digestive benefits. In Thai cooking, mint is almost always used raw and added at the last moment to preserve its volatile oils.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Ground pork:** The most common alternative to chicken in larb. Pork is slightly richer and produces a more traditional Isan-style larb. Use the same amount and technique.

**Lemongrass paste:** If fresh lemongrass is unavailable, use 1 tablespoon of lemongrass paste. Add it with the chicken rather than with the aromatics, as paste burns more easily than fresh.

**Turkey or mushroom:** Ground turkey works with the same proportions. For vegetarian, use finely chopped mushrooms (a mix of shiitake and cremini produces the best texture).

**Toasted rice powder vs. cornstarch:** Toasted rice powder is traditional and adds a smoky, sandy texture. Cornstarch is a shortcut that thickens the sauce but lacks the toasted flavor. Both work, but toasted rice powder is worth the extra five minutes.

**More lettuce options:** Butter lettuce has the softest, most pliable cups. Baby romaine is crispier and sturdier. Iceberg works for pure crunch. Endive leaves make elegant, smaller cups for appetizer portions.

## Serving Suggestions

As a starter, one or two lettuce cups per person with the rest of a Thai meal: green or red curry, steamed jasmine rice, and a som tum (green papaya salad) alongside. As a light main, serve three to four cups per person with a side of steamed rice and a simple cucumber salad. This also works well as party food, set out on a platter with all the toppings and let guests build their own. For a complete Thai spread, pair with [Thai Coconut Curry Mussels](/recipes/thai-coconut-curry-mussels) and steamed rice.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store the filling and herbs separately for up to 2 days. The filling reheats well; the herbs should be added fresh.

**Reheating:** Warm the filling in a wok or skillet over medium heat for 1 to 2 minutes. Add a squeeze of fresh lime juice after reheating to brighten the flavors.

**Freezing:** The cooked filling (without herbs or lettuce) freezes well for up to 2 months. Thaw overnight and reheat in a skillet. Add fresh herbs, onion, and lime juice when serving.
