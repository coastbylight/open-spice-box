# Rad Na (ราดหน้า)

*Charred wide rice noodles topped with a thick pork and Chinese broccoli gravy seasoned with fermented soybean paste and white pepper*

---

## Headnote

Rad na means "pour on the face," which is exactly what you do: char wide rice noodles in a hot wok until they stick together slightly and develop dark, toasted edges, then ladle a thick, savory gravy over the top. The gravy breaks the noodles apart, the starch-thickened sauce coats every strand, and the contrast between the slightly chewy, smoky noodles and the rich, silky gravy is what makes this one of Thailand's most beloved comfort foods.

The dish has Chinese origins, which is obvious from the ingredient list: no chilies, no lemongrass, no coconut milk, no fish sauce. Instead, fermented soybean paste (tao jiew) provides the savory backbone, alongside soy sauce, oyster sauce, and Golden Mountain sauce. Chinese broccoli (gai lan) is the traditional vegetable, its thick stems sliced on a bias and its leaves roughly chopped. Pork shoulder is marinated with egg white and tapioca starch in the Chinese velveting style, which keeps it silky and tender in the gravy.

The noodles are the part that requires courage. You want them to char. Really char. Dark brown spots on the bottom, slightly stuck together, almost burnt. This is not a mistake. The toasted noodle flavor is what makes rad na taste different from any other noodle-in-gravy dish. When the gravy goes on, the charred surfaces soften just enough to eat comfortably while keeping their smoky, toasted character. Serve it with chili vinegar on the side, Thai chilies sliced thin and soaked in white vinegar, which provides a sharp, hot acidity that cuts through the rich gravy perfectly.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the marinated pork
- 340 g pork shoulder (12 oz), sliced into bite-sized pieces about 1/8 inch thick
- 1 tbsp soy sauce
- 1 tbsp oyster sauce
- 1 tsp toasted sesame oil
- 1/2 tsp sugar
- 1/4 tsp white pepper
- 2 tbsp tapioca starch (or cornstarch)
- 1 large egg white

### For the noodles
- 700 g fresh wide rice noodles (25 oz)
- 1 tsp black soy sauce (or dark soy sauce)
- 1 tbsp neutral oil

### For the gravy
- 2 tbsp neutral oil
- 6 cloves garlic, chopped
- 3 tbsp Thai fermented soybean paste (tao jiew; or low-sodium miso)
- 720 ml pork or chicken stock (3 cups), unsalted, preferably homemade
- 1 1/2 tbsp soy sauce
- 1 tbsp Golden Mountain sauce (or Maggi seasoning or oyster sauce)
- 1 1/2 tbsp sugar
- 1 tsp toasted sesame oil
- 1/2 tsp ground white pepper
- 200 g Chinese broccoli (gai lan, 7 oz), stems thinly sliced on a bias, leaves roughly chopped
- 60 g tapioca starch (1/4 cup) mixed with 60 ml water (1/4 cup, slurry)

### For serving
- Chili vinegar: Thai chilies thinly sliced, covered with white vinegar, rested at least 15 minutes
- Roasted chili flakes (optional)

---

## Method

1. Marinate the pork. Combine the pork with the soy sauce, oyster sauce, sesame oil, sugar, white pepper, tapioca starch, and egg white. Mix well. Marinate for at least 20 minutes.

2. Make the chili vinegar. Thinly slice Thai chilies and cover with white vinegar in a small bowl. Let sit for at least 15 minutes. This keeps indefinitely in the fridge.

3. Char the noodles. Toss the noodles with the black soy sauce to stain them. Heat a large wok over high heat. Add the oil, then the noodles. Spread them out and let them sit and char without stirring. Once the bottom is toasted and dark, flip and repeat. The noodles will start to clump, which is fine. Once they look charred and slightly stuck together, remove from the wok and divide into serving bowls.

4. Build the gravy. In a wok or large saucepan over medium heat, add the oil, garlic, and fermented soybean paste. Saute until the garlic starts to turn golden brown. Add the stock, soy sauce, Golden Mountain sauce, sugar, white pepper, and sesame oil. Bring to a full boil.

5. Add the pork and greens. While waiting for the sauce to boil, stir the tapioca starch slurry. Once boiling, add the pork and spread the pieces out quickly. Once separated, stir in the Chinese broccoli and return to a boil.

6. Thicken the gravy. Give the starch slurry another stir. Pour half into the boiling gravy and stir. Once it returns to a boil, check the thickness. Add more slurry if you want it thicker. The gravy should be thick enough to coat the noodles but not gloopy.

7. Taste and adjust. Add more tao jiew or miso if it needs salt. Add more white pepper to taste.

8. Serve. Ladle the gravy over the charred noodles. Serve with chili vinegar and roasted chili flakes on the side.

## Why This Works

Charring the noodles in the wok creates Maillard reactions on the surface of the rice noodles, producing a toasted, slightly smoky flavor that is the defining characteristic of good rad na. The char also creates a texture contrast: crispy on the outside, soft and chewy inside, which then softens further when the hot gravy is poured over.

Tapioca starch rather than cornstarch is the traditional thickener. It cooks up clear and produces a silkier, more translucent gravy. It also reheats better because cornstarch tends to gel and become rubbery when refrigerated.

The egg white and tapioca starch in the pork marinade create a Chinese-style velvet that forms a protective shell around each piece of meat. This keeps the pork juicy and tender even as it cooks in the boiling gravy.

Fermented soybean paste (tao jiew) is the backbone of the flavor. It provides salty, umami, and a deep fermented complexity that soy sauce alone cannot achieve. Low-sodium miso is the closest substitute.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Fermented soybean paste (tao jiew):** A Thai condiment of Chinese origin, similar to Japanese miso but chunkier with visible whole or broken beans. The fermentation produces beneficial bacteria, glutamate, and B vitamins.

**Chinese broccoli (gai lan):** A cruciferous vegetable rich in vitamins A, C, and K. The thick stems hold up to the hot gravy without going limp.

**White pepper:** Traditional in both Thai and Chinese soups and gravies. Provides a subtle, woody heat that registers in the nose.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Noodle substitutes:** 225g dried wide rice noodles, 225g dried rice vermicelli, or 340g fresh wonton noodles all work.

**Chicken:** Substitute for pork with the same marinade. Slice thin.

**Seafood version:** Use shrimp, squid, or a combination. Higher-end restaurants serve rad na talay (seafood rad na).

**Miso for tao jiew:** Use the same quantity of low-sodium miso. If using regular miso, start with 2 tablespoons and add more at the end.

**Stock:** Homemade pork stock gives the best flavor. Good chicken stock works well.

---

## Serving Suggestions

Rad na is a complete meal in a bowl. The chili vinegar on the side is not optional, it is the condiment that makes the dish sing. For a bigger spread, serve alongside [Larb Gai](/recipes/larb-gai) or a simple Thai soup.

---

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store the gravy and noodles separately for up to 2 days. Tapioca-thickened gravy reheats better than cornstarch-thickened.

**Reheating:** Warm the gravy in a saucepan. Re-char the noodles briefly in a hot wok if possible, or microwave and accept softer noodles.

**Freezing:** The gravy freezes well for up to 2 months. Do not freeze the noodles.
