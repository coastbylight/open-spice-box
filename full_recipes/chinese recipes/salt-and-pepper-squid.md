# Salt and Pepper Squid (椒鹽鮮魷)

*Double-fried squid with a wet batter coating, tossed with garlic, chili, and scallion in a salt-white pepper seasoning*

---

## Headnote

Salt and pepper squid is one of the great Cantonese seafood dishes, and the version you make at home can be better than what most restaurants serve if you get two things right: the scoring and the double fry.

Scoring means cutting a cross-hatch pattern into the inside surface of the squid body without cutting all the way through. When the squid hits hot oil, the scored pieces curl into beautiful spirals and tubes, creating a dramatic presentation and more surface area for the batter to cling to. The scoring also shortens the muscle fibers, which is what keeps the squid tender rather than rubbery.

The batter is wet, not dry. Flour and potato starch mixed with water, baking powder, and a little oil into a thick paste that coats the squid and sets into a crispy shell during frying. A wet batter seals in more moisture than a dry dredge, which is important for squid because overcooking even slightly turns it tough. The first fry at 175°C (350°F) cooks the squid through and sets the batter. The second fry at 190°C (380°F) drives out remaining moisture from the coating and makes it shatteringly crisp.

After frying, the squid goes into a clean wok with garlic, chili, scallion, and red bell pepper for a fast toss, then gets hit with a simple seasoning of garlic salt and white pepper. No sauce, no glaze, just salt and heat and crunch. The white pepper is essential. It provides a subtle, woody warmth that black pepper cannot replicate. It is the seasoning that makes this dish Cantonese rather than Italian.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the squid
- 450 g squid (1 lb), cleaned, scored, and cut into pieces (see method)
- 1/4 tsp white pepper (for marinade)
- 1 tbsp cooking wine (for marinade)
- 1 large egg yolk
- 1 tbsp potato starch (for marinade)

### For the batter
- 4 tbsp all-purpose flour
- 2 tbsp potato starch
- 4 tbsp water
- 1 tsp baking powder
- 1/2 tsp salt
- 1 tsp oil

### For the seasoning
- 2 tsp garlic salt
- 1/4 tsp ground white pepper

### For the stir-fry
- 480 ml neutral oil (2 cups, for frying)
- 1/4 red bell pepper, cut into thin strips
- 2 small Thai bird's eye chilies, diced
- 3 cloves garlic, minced
- 3 stalks scallion, white parts diced
- 1 tsp cooking wine (for finishing)

---

## Method

1. Clean and score the squid. Separate the body from the tentacles. Cut the body open flat. Score the inside surface diagonally every 1/4 inch, then rotate 90 degrees and score again to create a cross-hatch pattern. Do not cut through. Cut into roughly 1-inch triangular pieces. Cut tentacles into 1 to 2 inch pieces. Pat everything dry.

2. Marinate. Toss the squid with white pepper, cooking wine, and egg yolk. Mix well. Add the potato starch and mix again. Set aside.

3. Make the batter. Combine the flour and potato starch. Add water gradually, mixing to a thick paste (like gummy mashed potatoes). Add baking powder, salt, and oil. Stir to combine.

4. Mix the seasoning. Combine garlic salt and white pepper in a small bowl.

5. First fry. Heat the oil to 175°C (350°F). Add the batter to the marinated squid and coat by hand. Drop pieces into the oil a few at a time. Fry for 2.5 to 3 minutes until golden and curled. Remove and drain.

6. Second fry. Heat the oil to 190°C (380°F). Fry each batch again for 30 to 40 seconds until deeply golden and very crispy. Remove and drain. Reserve 1 tablespoon of frying oil.

7. Toss with aromatics. Heat a clean wok on high. Add the reserved tablespoon of oil. Lower heat. Add garlic (20 to 30 seconds), then chilies (10 to 20 seconds), then scallion whites (10 to 20 seconds), then bell pepper (10 to 20 seconds). Add the squid, stir-fry for 40 to 50 seconds. Swirl in the cooking wine, toss for 15 to 20 seconds. Sprinkle the salt-pepper seasoning gradually while tossing for 30 to 40 seconds.

8. Serve immediately.

## Why This Works

The cross-hatch scoring shortens the squid's muscle fibers, preventing them from contracting into tough, rubbery rings during frying. The cuts also create surface area that catches batter and curls into attractive spirals in the hot oil.

The wet batter seals moisture inside the squid more effectively than a dry dredge. Baking powder creates tiny gas bubbles in the batter that expand during frying, producing a lighter, crunchier crust. The double fry ensures the coating is crispy enough to survive the final stir-fry toss without softening.

White pepper rather than black is the traditional Chinese seasoning for this dish. It provides a more subtle, woody heat that complements seafood without overpowering it.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Squid:** High in protein (about 18g per 100g) and very low in fat. A good source of B12, selenium, and zinc. The quick cooking time preserves its tender texture.

**White pepper:** Ground from fully ripened peppercorns with the outer husk removed. Contains piperine, which may enhance nutrient absorption.

---

## Substitutions and Variations

**Frozen pre-cooked squid:** Skip the marinade. Defrost, squeeze dry, coat in batter, and fry at 350°F for 30 to 40 seconds. Double fry 20 to 30 seconds.

**Shrimp:** Works with the same batter, seasoning, and technique.

**Without potato starch:** Cornstarch substitutes at a 1:1 ratio. Slightly less crispy but very close.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store for up to 1 day. The coating softens.

**Reheating:** Oven at 200°C (400°F) for 5 minutes on a wire rack. Avoid the microwave.

**Freezing:** Not recommended.
