# Yu Choy with Oyster Sauce (蚝油油菜)

*Blanched yu choy sum drizzled with a warm oyster sauce dressing, the dim sum side dish that belongs on every dinner table*

---

## Headnote

Yu choy is the sweetest of the Chinese leafy greens, noticeably sweeter and milder than its more famous cousin gai lan. The stems are tender and juicy, the leaves soft and mild, and the small yellow flowers that sometimes appear at the tips are edible and faintly honey-scented. It is the green that people who think they do not like Chinese greens end up liking.

This preparation is about as minimal as cooking gets. Blanch the yu choy for 30 seconds in boiling water with a splash of oil. Plate it. Pour a warm sauce of oyster sauce, soy sauce, and sesame oil over the top. That is the entire recipe. You will see this dish at dim sum restaurants next to plates of char siu and har gow, sitting quietly on the table doing exactly what it is supposed to do: providing something fresh and green to balance everything else.

The technique has one detail that makes the difference between a good result and a watery one. After blanching and plating, water will pool under the greens on the plate. Pour it off before adding the sauce. If you skip this step, the sauce dilutes and you lose the glossy, concentrated coating that makes this dish satisfying rather than bland. It is the kind of small thing that Chinese grandmothers do without thinking and that recipes often forget to mention.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the yu choy
- 450 g yu choy (about 1 lb)
- 2 tbsp vegetable oil, divided

### For the sauce
- 1 tbsp oyster sauce
- 1 tbsp light soy sauce
- 1/2 tsp sesame oil
- 1/8 tsp ground white pepper

---

## Method

1. Wash the yu choy. Leafy Chinese greens trap dirt and sand in their stems and leaves. Wash the yu choy three times in cold water, swishing it around to dislodge any grit. Drain in a colander.

2. Make the sauce. Heat 1 tablespoon of oil in a small saucepan over low heat for 30 seconds. Add the oyster sauce, soy sauce, sesame oil, and white pepper. Stir and bring to a gentle simmer, then immediately turn off the heat. Set aside. The sauce should be warm when it goes over the greens.

3. Blanch the yu choy. Bring a large pot of water to a rolling boil. Add the remaining 1 tablespoon of oil to the water. Add the yu choy and stir gently. The leaves will turn a deep, vivid green almost immediately. Blanch for 30 seconds for thin-stemmed yu choy, up to 1 minute if the stems are thick. Fish out a piece and taste if you are unsure. It should be tender but not mushy.

4. Plate and drain. Use tongs or chopsticks to lift the yu choy from the water and arrange it on a serving plate. Let it sit for a moment, then carefully tilt the plate and pour off any water that has pooled underneath. This step is important. If you skip it, the sauce will be diluted and watery.

5. Dress and serve. Pour the warm sauce evenly over the yu choy. If the stalks are long, use kitchen scissors to cut them in half on the plate. Serve immediately.

---

## Why This Works

Blanching in water with oil serves two purposes. The oil coats the surface of the greens and gives them a subtle sheen, and the boiling water cooks the stems and leaves quickly and evenly. The 30-second blanch is enough because yu choy stems are thinner and more tender than gai lan or broccoli. They are already close to the right texture when raw, and overcooking makes them slimy rather than crisp.

The sauce is warmed rather than cooked. Oyster sauce straight from the bottle is thick and cold, and it does not spread evenly. A brief simmer with oil, soy sauce, and sesame oil loosens the consistency and lets the flavors meld into a smooth, pourable dressing. The warmth also helps the sauce coat the greens rather than sliding off.

Draining the water from the plate before saucing is the difference between a clean, well-dressed vegetable and a plate of greens sitting in a diluted puddle. It takes five seconds and changes the entire dish.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Yu choy:** One of the most nutritionally dense leafy greens available. A single serving provides over 200% of the daily recommended vitamin A and 175% of vitamin C. It is also a significant source of calcium, iron, and folate. Like all cruciferous vegetables, it contains glucosinolates, compounds that the body converts to sulforaphane, which has been extensively studied for anti-cancer properties. In traditional Chinese medicine, leafy greens like yu choy are classified as cooling foods, used to clear excess heat and promote healthy digestion.

**Oyster sauce:** A thick, savory condiment made from oyster extracts, sugar, and soy sauce. It provides umami depth without the sharp saltiness of straight soy sauce. The combination of oyster sauce and soy sauce in Chinese vegetable dishes follows the principle of layering different umami sources for a more complex, rounded flavor.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Chinese broccoli (gai lan):** The classic dim sum substitute. Blanch for 1 to 2 minutes instead, as gai lan stems are thicker. The flavor is more bitter and robust.

**Broccolini:** Works well with the same sauce and timing as yu choy. Slightly more crunch, slightly less sweetness.

**Bok choy:** Baby bok choy halved lengthwise is another good option. Blanch for 1 minute.

**Vegetarian oyster sauce:** Mushroom-based oyster sauce substitutes directly. Slightly less sweet, but the overall effect is very similar.

**Garlic version:** Add 2 minced garlic cloves to the oil before adding the sauce ingredients for a garlic-forward variation.

## Serving Suggestions

This is the vegetable side that goes with everything. It sits on the table next to roast duck, next to steamed fish, next to a bowl of congee. At dim sum, it appears alongside heavier dishes as a palate cleanser. At home, serve it with [Mapo Tofu](/recipes/mapo-tofu), [Shaxian Peanut Sauce Noodles](/recipes/shaxian-peanut-sauce-noodles), or any rice-and-protein meal where the table needs something green and simple. It pairs especially well with rich, saucy dishes because its clean flavor provides contrast.

---

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store undressed in an airtight container for up to 1 day. Add fresh sauce when serving. Pre-dressed leftovers are fine for up to a day but will lose their crispness.

**Reheating:** A quick dip in boiling water for 10 seconds refreshes the texture. Alternatively, eat cold, which is perfectly acceptable for blanched Chinese greens.

**Freezing:** Not recommended. Blanched leafy greens become waterlogged and limp when thawed.
