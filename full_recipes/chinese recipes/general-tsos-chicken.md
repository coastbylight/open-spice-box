# General Tso's Chicken (左宗棠鸡)

*Double-fried crispy chicken thighs in a sweet, tangy, subtly spicy sauce with dried chilies and broccoli, the Chinese-American takeout legend*

---

## Headnote

General Tso's chicken is named after a real person, Zuo Zongtang, a Qing dynasty military leader from Hunan province. He almost certainly never ate this dish. The version we know, crispy fried chicken in a sweet and spicy sauce, was created by Chef Peng Chang-kuei in the 1950s, first in Taiwan and later at his restaurant in New York. The original Hunanese version was reportedly drier and less sweet. American customers wanted it sweeter and saucier, and the dish evolved to meet them. By the 1980s it was on every Chinese takeout menu in the country, and it has stayed there ever since.

The technique is what separates a good version from a mediocre one. The chicken is marinated with Shaoxing wine, sesame oil, and white pepper, then coated in a mixture of cornstarch, flour, and toasted sesame seeds. It is deep-fried twice: the first fry at a moderate temperature cooks the chicken through, and the second fry at higher heat creates the shattering crunch that defines the dish. Double frying is the difference between a coating that turns soggy in sauce and one that holds its crunch long enough for you to eat it.

The sauce is a fast reduction in a clean wok: dried chilies, garlic, Shaoxing wine, and a pre-mixed sauce of chicken stock, soy sauce, brown sugar, and rice vinegar. A cornstarch slurry thickens it into a glossy glaze. The fried chicken and blanched broccoli go in for a final toss, and the sauce clings to the craggy surface of the coating without penetrating it. Serve it immediately. Crispy dishes do not wait.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the chicken
- 450 g boneless skinless chicken thighs (1 lb), cut into 1-inch pieces
- 1 tsp Shaoxing wine
- 1 tbsp water
- 1/4 tsp salt
- 1/4 tsp ground white pepper
- 1 tsp sesame oil
- 1 tsp toasted sesame seeds
- 1 tbsp all-purpose flour
- 60 g cornstarch (1/4 cup)

### For the sauce
- 80 ml low-sodium chicken stock (1/3 cup)
- 1 tsp dark soy sauce
- 1 tbsp soy sauce
- 3 1/2 tbsp brown sugar
- 1/2 tsp rice wine vinegar

### For cooking
- 225 g broccoli florets (8 oz, about 3 cups)
- 960 ml peanut or vegetable oil (4 cups, for frying)
- 5 to 6 dried red chili peppers
- 2 cloves garlic, minced
- 2 tsp Shaoxing wine
- 2 tbsp cornstarch mixed with 2 tbsp water (slurry)

---

## Method

1. Marinate the chicken. Combine the chicken with the Shaoxing wine, water, salt, white pepper, and sesame oil. Mix until the liquid is absorbed. Add the sesame seeds, flour, and cornstarch. Stir to coat evenly. Set aside.

2. Mix the sauce. Combine the chicken stock, dark soy sauce, soy sauce, brown sugar, and rice vinegar. Stir until the sugar dissolves. Set aside.

3. Blanch the broccoli. Boil 6 cups of water. Blanch the broccoli for 30 seconds to 1 minute. Drain and set aside. For brighter green, transfer to an ice bath before draining.

4. First fry. Heat the frying oil in a deep pot to 170°C (335°F). Drop the chicken pieces in small batches, making sure the coating covers all sides. Fry for about 5 minutes until light golden. Transfer to a wire rack or paper towels. Repeat with all the chicken.

5. Second fry. Once all chicken has been fried once, fry everything a second time for 3 to 5 minutes until deeply golden and very crispy. Transfer to a rack. Do not pile the pieces.

6. Build the sauce. Heat a clean wok over medium heat. Add 1 tablespoon of oil and the dried chilies. Cook for 5 seconds. Add the garlic and Shaoxing wine. Pour in the sauce and bring to a simmer. Stir in the cornstarch slurry until the sauce thickens to a glossy glaze.

7. Finish. Add the crispy chicken and broccoli. Toss until everything is coated. Serve immediately with steamed rice.

---

## Why This Works

Double frying is the technique that makes this dish. The first fry at a moderate temperature (335°F) cooks the chicken through and partially sets the coating. The second fry at higher temperature drives out remaining moisture from the coating, creating a glass-like crunch that resists the sauce. Single-fried chicken absorbs the sauce and turns soggy within minutes.

The sauce is intentionally glossy and concentrated. The cornstarch slurry transforms the thin stock-based liquid into a coating that clings to the craggy surface of the fried chicken. The brown sugar provides sweetness, the soy sauce provides salt and umami, and the rice vinegar provides the subtle tang that keeps the sweetness from being cloying.

The dried chilies are cooked briefly in oil before the sauce goes in, which releases their aromatic compounds without burning them. They are present for fragrance and a gentle background heat, not to make the dish spicy. Break one or two in half if you want more heat.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Chicken thighs:** Darker and more flavorful than breast meat, with enough fat to stay juicy through double frying. About 26 grams of protein per 100 grams.

**Toasted sesame seeds:** Mixed into the coating, they add a nutty flavor and visual texture that plain cornstarch lacks. They contain calcium, iron, and lignans with antioxidant properties.

---

## Substitutions and Variations

**Chicken breast:** Works but is leaner and more prone to drying out during double frying. Cut into slightly larger pieces to compensate.

**Baked version:** Coat the chicken, spread on a lined baking sheet, and bake at 220°C (425°F) for 20 to 25 minutes, turning once. Less crispy than fried but much less oil.

**More heat:** Use hotter dried peppers (chile de arbol), or add red pepper flakes or sambal to the sauce.

**Extra sauce:** Double the sauce quantities if you like extra for rice. The base recipe produces a concentrated coating.

## Serving Suggestions

Serve over steamed white rice. For a full Chinese-American spread, pair with [Lo Mein](/recipes/lo-mein), [Egg Drop Soup](/recipes/egg-drop-soup), and spring rolls.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store for up to 2 days. The coating will soften.

**Reheating:** Oven at 200°C (400°F) for 8 to 10 minutes on a wire rack to re-crisp. Avoid the microwave.

**Freezing:** Not recommended. The coating and sauce do not survive freezing.
