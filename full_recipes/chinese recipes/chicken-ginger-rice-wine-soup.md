# Chicken Ginger Rice Wine Soup (薑雞酒湯)

*A warming traditional soup with whole chicken, generous ginger, rice wine, peanuts, wood ear mushrooms, and jujubes*

---

## Headnote

This soup sits at the intersection of food and medicine in Chinese culture. It is most closely associated with "sitting the month" (zuo yue zi), the traditional Chinese postpartum confinement period during which a new mother rests for approximately 30 days while eating specific foods believed to restore her body after childbirth. Ginger chicken rice wine soup is perhaps the single most iconic dish of this tradition, prepared by mothers and mothers-in-law for new mothers across Chinese communities worldwide.

Each ingredient has a purpose rooted in traditional Chinese medicine (TCM). Ginger is considered warming and is believed to dispel cold from the body, which is especially important in the TCM understanding of postpartum recovery, when the body is thought to be in a weakened, "cold" state. Rice wine warms the blood and promotes circulation. Jujubes (red dates) are considered blood tonics that replenish qi and nourish the spleen. Wood ear mushrooms are believed to promote blood circulation and have been used in TCM for centuries. Dried daylily (golden needles) is traditionally thought to help with mood and lactation. Peanuts are included for their nourishing, blood-building properties.

Whether or not you subscribe to the TCM framework, the soup is genuinely delicious and deeply comforting. The broth is rich and golden from the chicken, strongly fragrant with ginger, and warmed by a generous pour of rice wine that gives it a subtle sweetness and an almost rosy glow. It is a soup that heats you from the inside, perfect for cold winter nights regardless of whether you are recovering from childbirth or just recovering from a long week.

One important note: the recipe calls for drinking rice wine, not cooking rice wine. Cooking rice wine has added salt and a harsh flavor. Drinking rice wine (sometimes labeled "sweet rice wine" or mijiu) has a smooth, sweet character that is essential to the soup's flavor.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### Main
- 1 whole chicken (about 1.2 to 1.5 kg), cut into pieces through the bone
- 80 g fresh ginger, sliced into coins about 3 mm thick (do not peel)
- 500 ml Chinese rice wine (drinking rice wine / mijiu, NOT cooking wine)
- 500 ml water
- 50 g raw peanuts (skin on)
- 15 g dried wood ear mushrooms, soaked in warm water for 20 minutes
- 10 jujubes (dried red dates), rinsed
- 15 g dried daylily (golden needle / 金針), soaked in warm water for 15 minutes (optional)
- 30 ml sesame oil
- 5 g salt, or to taste
- 10 g rock sugar or brown sugar (optional)

---

## Method

1. Soak the dried wood ear mushrooms in warm water for 20 minutes until fully expanded and pliable. Trim off any hard stem bases. If using dried daylily, soak it separately in warm water for 15 minutes, then drain and tie each strand into a loose knot. The knot keeps the strands from falling apart during cooking and is the traditional presentation.

2. Cut the chicken into pieces through the bone. If your butcher has not done this, use a heavy cleaver to chop through the joints and cut breast and thigh pieces into 4 to 5 cm sections. Rinse the pieces and pat dry.

3. Bring a pot of water to a boil. Add the chicken pieces and parboil for 3 minutes to remove blood and impurities. Drain, rinse under cold water, and set aside.

4. Heat the sesame oil in a large soup pot or Dutch oven over medium heat. Add the ginger coins and cook, stirring occasionally, for 3 to 4 minutes until the edges are golden and curled and the kitchen smells intensely of ginger. The sesame oil is traditional here and contributes its own warming properties according to TCM.

5. Add the chicken pieces to the pot with the ginger. Stir and cook for 2 to 3 minutes, letting the chicken pick up the ginger-sesame flavor.

6. Pour in the rice wine and water. Add the peanuts, jujubes, soaked wood ear mushrooms, and daylily knots. Stir to combine.

7. Bring to a boil over high heat, then immediately reduce to a gentle simmer. Cover and cook for 40 to 45 minutes, until the chicken is fall-off-the-bone tender and the peanuts are soft.

8. In the last 5 minutes, add the rock sugar if using. Season with salt to taste. The soup should taste warmly of ginger and rice wine, with a sweet undertone from the jujubes.

9. Ladle into deep bowls, making sure each serving gets chicken, peanuts, jujubes, mushrooms, and plenty of ginger-rich broth.

10. Serve hot. Traditionally, this soup is eaten with plain steamed rice or thin rice noodles.

## Why This Works

Searing the ginger in sesame oil before adding liquid is not just for flavor. Heat activates gingerol and shogaol, the pungent compounds in ginger, making them more potent and more easily extracted into the broth. The oil also carries these fat-soluble compounds throughout the soup.

Using drinking rice wine instead of cooking wine makes a significant difference. Drinking rice wine (mijiu) is naturally fermented, with a smooth sweetness and a clean flavor. Cooking rice wine has added salt, preservatives, and often a harsher alcohol taste. The generous amount used here (500 ml) forms a substantial part of the braising liquid, so its quality directly impacts the final soup.

The combination of parboiling the chicken and then searing it with ginger accomplishes two things: the parboil removes blood and impurities for a cleaner broth, while the sear builds flavor through the interaction of rendered chicken fat, sesame oil, and ginger.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Ginger** contains gingerols and shogaols, bioactive compounds with documented anti-inflammatory and anti-nausea properties. Multiple studies support ginger's effectiveness in reducing nausea, including during pregnancy. In TCM, ginger is classified as warming and is used to promote circulation and dispel cold.

**Jujubes (red dates)** are rich in vitamin C, potassium, and various antioxidants. In TCM, they are classified as a blood tonic and qi builder. Some research suggests jujube extracts may have sedative and anti-anxiety effects, which could be relevant in the postpartum context.

**Wood ear mushrooms** are a source of dietary fiber, iron, and polysaccharides. Research has explored their potential anticoagulant properties. In TCM, they are used to promote blood circulation and nourish yin.

**Rice wine** is a fermented grain alcohol with a lower alcohol content than spirits. During the long simmer, much of the alcohol evaporates, but some remains. The fermentation process produces amino acids and organic compounds that contribute flavor and, in TCM thinking, warming properties.

**Peanuts** provide protein, healthy fats, and folate. In TCM, they are considered a blood-nourishing food, which is part of their inclusion in postpartum diets.

## Substitutions and Variations

- **Chicken:** Use a whole free-range chicken if possible. The flavor and gelatin content are noticeably better than conventionally raised birds. Chicken legs and thighs can substitute for a whole chicken.
- **Rice wine:** There is no good substitute. Do not use sake, sherry, or cooking wine. Look for Chinese drinking rice wine (mijiu) at Asian grocery stores. Some brands to look for include TTL Taiwan Rice Wine or Shaohsing Huadiao wine (which is technically a different style but works).
- **Wood ear mushrooms:** Dried shiitake mushrooms can substitute, though the texture and TCM properties are different.
- **Without daylily:** The soup is complete without it. Dried daylily adds a distinctive floral, slightly sweet flavor, but it is not always easy to find.
- **Stronger ginger flavor:** Increase the ginger to 120 g for a more intensely warming version, common in the first two weeks of the postpartum period.
- **With rice noodle:** Cook thin rice vermicelli separately and place in the bottom of each bowl before ladling the soup over. This makes it a more substantial one-bowl meal.

---

## Serving Suggestions

Serve this soup as the main course of a simple, nourishing meal with steamed rice. In the postpartum tradition, it is served daily, sometimes twice daily, as part of a broader confinement diet. Outside of that context, it is a wonderful winter soup for anyone who enjoys bold ginger flavor and warming, fortifying broth. A side of blanched leafy greens dressed with soy sauce and sesame oil rounds out the meal.

---

## Storage and Reheating

This soup stores beautifully and tastes even better the next day as the ginger and rice wine flavors continue to develop. Refrigerate in an airtight container for up to 4 days. Reheat gently on the stovetop over medium heat. The broth may gel slightly when cold due to the gelatin from the chicken bones; this is a sign of a well-made broth and it will return to liquid form when heated. Freezes well for up to 2 months. The peanuts may soften further after freezing but the soup remains delicious.
