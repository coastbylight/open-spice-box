# Ginger Beef

*Crispy cornstarch-coated flank steak tossed in a gingery sweet-savory sauce with fried julienned ginger, the Chinese-Canadian classic from Calgary*

---

## Headnote

Ginger beef is a Chinese-Canadian invention, born at the Silver Inn restaurant in Calgary, Alberta, and now a staple of Chinese restaurants across western Canada. You will not find it in China. It belongs to the same family of diaspora dishes as General Tso's chicken and orange beef: crispy battered meat tossed in a sweet-savory sauce, engineered for the North American palate but built on Chinese technique.

What makes ginger beef different from its cousins is restraint. The sauce is lighter and less sweet than orange beef or Mongolian beef, and the ginger is the actual star rather than a background note. Julienned fresh ginger gets fried until crispy in the same oil used for the beef, and it stays in the dish as a crunchy, aromatic garnish. Minced ginger goes into the sauce itself, infusing it with a warm, sharp flavor that cuts through the richness of the fried beef.

The beef is flank steak, sliced thin against the grain, marinated with cornstarch and baking soda, then dredged in more cornstarch and seared in a moderate amount of oil until the coating is crispy and golden. The sauce simmers briefly in the wok, thickened with a cornstarch slurry, and the beef goes back in for a final toss that glazes every piece. The crispy coating absorbs just enough sauce to become sticky and flavorful while keeping its crunch underneath. Eat it fast. Crispy fried dishes wait for no one.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the marinade
- 450 g flank steak (1 lb), sliced against the grain into 1/4-inch strips
- 2 tsp cornstarch
- 2 tsp neutral oil
- 1 tsp oyster sauce
- 1 tbsp water
- 1/4 tsp baking soda

### For the sauce
- 160 ml water (2/3 cup)
- 1 1/2 tbsp light brown sugar
- 1 tbsp light soy sauce
- 1 tbsp oyster sauce
- 3/4 tsp dark soy sauce
- 1/2 tsp sesame oil
- 1 tbsp fresh ginger, minced

### For cooking
- 80 to 120 ml neutral oil (1/3 to 1/2 cup, for searing)
- 60 g cornstarch (about 1/3 cup, for dredging) plus 1 tbsp reserved for slurry
- 2 tbsp fresh ginger, finely julienned
- 1 scallion, thinly sliced on an angle

---

## Method

1. Marinate the beef. Combine the sliced flank steak with the cornstarch, oil, oyster sauce, water, and baking soda. Mix well. Marinate for at least 30 minutes, or overnight in the refrigerator.

2. Mix the sauce. Combine the water, brown sugar, soy sauce, oyster sauce, dark soy sauce, sesame oil, and minced ginger in a bowl. Set aside.

3. Dredge the beef. Spread the cornstarch in a shallow dish. Dredge each marinated beef slice in the cornstarch until thoroughly coated. Place on a clean plate.

4. Sear the beef. Heat the oil in a wok over high heat. When the oil shimmers, add half the beef in an even layer. Sear for 1 to 2 minutes per side until the coating is crispy and golden. Remove to a plate. Repeat with the remaining beef. Turn off the heat and transfer all beef to a plate.

5. Fry the ginger. Drain the oil from the wok, leaving about 2 tablespoons. Return to medium-high heat. Add the julienned ginger and fry until crispy and golden, about 1 minute. Reserve a few pieces for garnish if you like.

6. Build the sauce. Pour the premixed sauce into the wok and bring to a simmer. Cook for 2 minutes. Mix the reserved 1 tablespoon of cornstarch with 3 tablespoons of water and drizzle into the sauce. Stir until it thickens enough to coat the back of a spoon.

7. Finish. Add the beef and scallions to the wok and toss for 30 seconds until every piece is glazed. The sauce should cling to the beef with almost no liquid remaining. Serve immediately with steamed rice.

---

## Why This Works

The double cornstarch technique, first in the marinade and then as a dredge, creates two layers of protection around the beef. The inner layer (mixed with the marinade) helps the outer layer adhere. The outer dredge sets into a crispy shell during searing that provides crunch even after being tossed in sauce.

Frying the julienned ginger in oil before building the sauce serves two purposes: it crisps the ginger into an aromatic garnish, and it infuses the oil with ginger flavor that carries into the sauce. The minced ginger already in the sauce provides a deeper, more diffuse ginger presence, while the fried julienne provides concentrated, crunchy bursts.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Ginger:** Fresh ginger contains gingerols with documented anti-inflammatory and digestive properties. The two forms of ginger in this dish (minced in sauce, julienned and fried) provide different flavor and texture experiences. Frying ginger intensifies its warmth and adds nuttiness.

**Flank steak:** A lean, flavorful cut with long muscle fibers that become tender when sliced thin against the grain. The baking soda velvet ensures it stays silky even after high-heat searing.

---

## Substitutions and Variations

**Skirt steak or sirloin:** Both work well. Slice thin against the grain.

**More ginger:** Increase the julienned ginger to 3 tablespoons and add a teaspoon of honey to the sauce for a sweeter, more intense ginger flavor closer to some Calgary restaurant versions.

**Air fryer:** Dredged beef can be air-fried at 400°F for 8 to 10 minutes instead of shallow-fried.

---

## Serving Suggestions

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store for up to 2 days. The crispy coating will soften.

**Reheating:** Reheat in a hot oven at 400°F for 5 to 8 minutes on a wire rack to re-crisp. Avoid the microwave.

**Freezing:** Not recommended.
