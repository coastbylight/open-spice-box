# Chicken Chow Mein (鷄肉炒麵)

*Stir-fried egg noodles with chicken, cabbage, and bean sprouts tossed in a savory oyster sauce glaze*

---

## Headnote

Chow mein means "fried noodles," and the key word is fried. The noodles should have golden, lightly crispy edges where they made contact with the hot wok, mixed with softer strands that absorbed the sauce. Getting this contrast right is what separates a good plate of chow mein from a limp pile of steamed noodles dressed with sauce.

The trick starts before the noodles even touch the wok. Most fresh chow mein noodles benefit from a brief steam before boiling. This may sound counterintuitive, but steaming the raw noodles for a few minutes partially sets their surface starch, which makes them more resilient to overcooking. When you boil them afterward, they cook through without turning mushy. The result is a noodle with real chew that can handle the aggressive heat of a wok without falling apart.

Once cooked, the noodles go into a blazing hot wok with oil. The temptation is to stir immediately, but patience pays off here. Let the noodles sit against the hot metal for 30 to 60 seconds without touching them. This is how you develop that golden, slightly crispy layer on the bottom. Then flip, let the other side sear, and only then start tossing.

The vegetables matter too. Everything should be cut into long, thin strips that match the shape of the noodles. This is not just for looks: uniform shapes mean every forkful or chopstick grab gets a mix of noodle, vegetable, and protein. Cabbage and bean sprouts are the classic pairing. The cabbage adds sweet crunch, and the sprouts contribute a fresh, watery snap that lightens the richness of the oyster sauce.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### Noodles
- 400 g fresh chow mein egg noodles (or dried thin egg noodles)
- 15 ml vegetable oil (for noodles)

### Chicken
- 300 g boneless, skinless chicken thigh, sliced into thin strips
- 10 g cornstarch
- 10 ml light soy sauce
- 5 ml Shaoxing wine
- 2 g white pepper

### Vegetables
- 150 g green cabbage, shredded into thin strips
- 100 g bean sprouts
- 2 scallions, cut into 5 cm lengths
- 1 medium carrot, julienned (optional)

### Sauce
- 20 ml oyster sauce
- 15 ml light soy sauce
- 5 ml dark soy sauce
- 5 g sugar
- 5 ml sesame oil
- 30 ml water

### For Cooking
- 30 ml vegetable oil, divided

---

## Method

1. If using fresh chow mein noodles, set up a steamer and steam the noodles for 5 minutes. This pre-sets the starch and prevents them from turning mushy when boiled. If using dried noodles, skip the steaming and boil according to package directions until just short of done, then drain.

2. Bring a large pot of water to a boil. Add the steamed noodles and cook for 1 to 2 minutes until tender but still firm. Drain immediately and rinse briefly under cold water to stop cooking. Toss with 15 ml of vegetable oil to prevent sticking. Spread on a plate to cool slightly.

3. Combine the chicken strips with cornstarch, soy sauce, Shaoxing wine, and white pepper. Mix well and set aside while you prepare the vegetables.

4. Mix all the sauce ingredients together in a small bowl and set aside.

5. Heat 15 ml of vegetable oil in a wok over high heat until it begins to smoke. Add the chicken strips in a single layer. Let them sear without stirring for 1 minute until golden on the bottom, then stir-fry for another 1 to 2 minutes until cooked through. Remove and set aside.

6. Add the remaining 15 ml of oil to the wok. When smoking, add the noodles and spread them into an even layer. Let them sit undisturbed for 45 to 60 seconds to develop a golden crust on the bottom. Flip sections of the noodles and let the other side crisp for another 30 seconds.

7. Push the noodles to one side. Add the cabbage and carrot to the empty side of the wok. Stir-fry for 1 minute until the cabbage begins to wilt.

8. Toss the noodles and vegetables together. Pour the sauce mixture over everything. Use tongs or chopsticks to lift and toss the noodles, ensuring the sauce coats every strand. Cook for 1 minute, tossing continuously.

9. Return the chicken to the wok. Add the bean sprouts and scallion lengths. Toss everything together for 30 seconds. The bean sprouts should stay crunchy.

10. Transfer to a serving plate. Serve immediately while the noodles still have some crispy edges.

## Why This Works

Steaming the noodles before boiling partially gelatinizes the surface starch, creating a firmer, more resilient noodle. When these pre-steamed noodles are boiled, they cook through without becoming waterlogged. The result is a noodle with enough structural integrity to handle the high heat and aggressive tossing of wok cooking.

Letting the noodles sit undisturbed in the hot wok allows the Maillard reaction to occur on the surface in direct contact with the metal. This produces the golden-brown, slightly crispy texture that defines good chow mein. Constant stirring would prevent this crust from forming.

Dark soy sauce in the sauce mixture serves a specific purpose beyond flavor: it provides color. A few milliliters of dark soy sauce tint the noodles to that appetizing deep golden-brown without making them too salty. Light soy sauce handles the actual seasoning.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Chicken thigh** is used instead of breast because its higher fat content keeps it moist during the intense heat of wok cooking. Thigh meat is also richer in iron and zinc compared to breast meat. The darker color comes from myoglobin, a protein that stores oxygen in muscle tissue.

**Bean sprouts** are a good source of vitamin C and folate. They add volume, crunch, and moisture to the dish without adding many calories. Their high water content helps create steam in the wok, which cooks the noodles and vegetables quickly.

**Oyster sauce** is made from oyster extracts and provides a concentrated source of umami. It contains zinc and iron from the oyster base, though in small quantities per serving.

## Substitutions and Variations

- **Noodles:** Lo mein noodles, thin spaghetti, or ramen noodles can substitute in a pinch. The texture will be different but the dish will still taste good.
- **Protein:** Shrimp, sliced pork, or beef work well. For vegetarian, use extra-firm tofu, pressed and sliced into strips. Marinate it the same way.
- **Vegetables:** Bok choy, snow peas, bell peppers, or mushrooms are all common additions. Keep the strips long and thin to match the noodles.
- **Crispy chow mein:** For a pan-fried crispy noodle cake, press the oiled noodles into a flat disc in the wok and fry without stirring for 3 to 4 minutes per side until golden and crunchy. Serve the stir-fried toppings over the crispy cake.
- **Soy sauce noodles:** For a simpler version, skip the oyster sauce and dress the noodles with just light soy sauce, dark soy sauce, and sesame oil.

---

## Serving Suggestions

Chow mein is a complete one-plate meal that needs nothing more than a cold drink alongside it. If serving as part of a larger spread, pair it with a vegetable dish like stir-fried gai lan or dry-fried green beans. It also works well as the noodle course in a multi-dish Chinese meal, served after soup and before the main protein dishes.

---

## Storage and Reheating

Leftover chow mein stores in the refrigerator for up to 2 days. The noodles will soften as they absorb sauce during storage. Reheat in a hot wok or skillet with a small splash of oil, tossing over high heat for 2 to 3 minutes. This can restore some of the crispy edges. Microwaving works but produces softer noodles. Not ideal for freezing, as the noodles become mushy when thawed.
