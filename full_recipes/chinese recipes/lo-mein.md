# Lo Mein (捞面)

*Fresh lo mein noodles tossed in caramelized soy and oyster sauce with scallions, five ingredients, ten minutes, no excuses*

---

## Headnote

Lo mein means "tossed noodles" in Cantonese, and the name describes the entire technique. You boil noodles, you make a sauce in a wok, you toss them together. That is the whole recipe. This version strips it down to five ingredients: fresh lo mein noodles, soy sauce, oyster sauce, scallions, and oil. It should not be this good with this little effort, but it is.

The trick that makes it work is what happens in the wok before the noodles go in. The soy sauce and oyster sauce get poured directly into the hot oil, where they sizzle and caramelize for about twenty seconds. This brief frying concentrates the sauces, darkens them, and creates a coating that clings to the noodles rather than pooling at the bottom of the bowl. If you just toss the noodles with cold sauce, you get wet noodles. If you fry the sauce first, you get lo mein.

Fresh lo mein noodles cook in under a minute. They go into boiling water, loosen up, and come out. Pre-cooked noodles from the refrigerator section work but tend to be spongier and shred more easily in the wok. Seek out fresh, uncooked lo mein noodles at an Asian market if you can. The texture is completely different: springy, chewy, and able to hold the sauce without falling apart.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

- 450 g fresh uncooked lo mein noodles (1 lb)
- 3 tbsp neutral oil
- 3 scallions, halved lengthwise and cut into 2-inch pieces
- 2 tbsp light soy sauce
- 3 tbsp oyster sauce

---

## Method

1. Cook the noodles. Bring a pot of water to a boil. Loosen the noodles from their package, drop them in, and cook for 45 to 60 seconds. They should be just tender. Drain and set aside.

2. Heat the wok. Heat the wok over medium-high heat until it just starts to smoke. Add the oil and swirl to coat. Add the scallions and toss a few times. Push them to the side.

3. Caramelize the sauce. Increase the heat to high. Pour the soy sauce directly into the oil. It will sizzle immediately. Add the oyster sauce. Let both sauces fry in the oil for about 20 seconds. They should darken slightly and become fragrant.

4. Toss the noodles. Add the drained noodles to the wok. Toss vigorously to distribute the sauce evenly through every strand. This takes about 30 seconds.

5. Serve immediately.

---

## Why This Works

Caramelizing the soy sauce and oyster sauce in hot oil before adding the noodles is the entire technique. The high heat triggers Maillard reactions in the sugars and amino acids in both sauces, producing new flavor compounds that taste deeper and more complex than the raw sauces. The oil also helps the concentrated sauce coat the noodles evenly rather than being absorbed unevenly.

Fresh uncooked lo mein noodles have a different structure than pre-cooked ones. The brief boil hydrates the raw flour and sets the gluten, producing a noodle that is springy and resilient. Pre-cooked noodles have already been steamed or boiled once, so a second cooking makes them soft and prone to breaking.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Oyster sauce:** A thick, savory condiment made from oyster extracts, sugar, and soy sauce. It provides a combination of umami, sweetness, and body that soy sauce alone cannot. The sugars in oyster sauce caramelize quickly in hot oil, which is what produces the glossy, clingy coating.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Add protein:** Sear 350g of sliced chicken, beef, pork, or shrimp in the wok first, remove, then proceed with the recipe. Add the protein back with the noodles.

**Add vegetables:** Napa cabbage, snow peas, julienned carrots, or bean sprouts can all go in with the scallions. Season with a pinch of salt. Add up to 2 to 4 cups without overcrowding.

**Dark soy sauce:** Add 1/4 teaspoon for a darker color. More than that will overpower the dish.

**Sesame oil:** Add 1/2 teaspoon at the end if you want it, but the dish does not need it.

**Dried noodles:** If fresh lo mein noodles are unavailable, use dried Chinese wheat noodles or even spaghetti. Cook according to package directions.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store for up to 2 days. The noodles will absorb sauce as they sit.

**Reheating:** Toss in a hot wok with a splash of water or soy sauce for 1 to 2 minutes.

**Freezing:** Not recommended. The noodles become mushy.
