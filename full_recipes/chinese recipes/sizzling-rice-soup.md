# Sizzling Rice Soup (鍋巴湯)

*A dramatic tableside soup where crispy fried rice crackers sizzle and pop in hot chicken broth with shrimp and vegetables*

---

## Headnote

There are dishes you cook for flavor, and dishes you cook for the moment. Sizzling rice soup is both. The theatrical climax comes at the table, when a plate of searingly hot, freshly fried rice crackers is dropped into a tureen of boiling soup. The result is a loud, sustained crackle, a burst of steam, and a room full of delighted faces. In Chinese banquet cooking, this is one of the signature showstoppers, a dish where sound and spectacle are as important as taste.

The rice crackers themselves, called guoba, are the crunchy crust that forms at the bottom of a rice pot. In the days of cooking rice over wood fires, every pot produced a layer of toasty, golden rice stuck to the bottom. Thrifty cooks turned this into an ingredient rather than a waste product, frying the dried crusts until they puffed and crisped. Today, you can make guoba deliberately by spreading cooked rice thin on a sheet pan and drying it out in a low oven, or you can buy pre-made rice crackers at any Asian grocery store. Either approach works, though homemade crusts have a more rustic, toasty flavor.

The soup itself is straightforward and flexible. Chicken broth forms the base, with cornstarch-velveted chicken and shrimp for protein, and whatever combination of napa cabbage, bok choy, mushrooms, and snow peas you have on hand. The key is that everything must be blazing hot at the moment of assembly. Lukewarm soup will not produce the sizzle. The crackers must go straight from the fryer to the table, and the soup must be at a full rolling boil. Get those two things right, and you will have a dish that people remember long after the meal is over.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### Rice Crackers (Guoba)
- 300 g cooked jasmine rice, day-old and cold
- Vegetable oil for deep frying (about 750 ml)

### Soup
- 150 g chicken breast, sliced into thin strips
- 150 g medium shrimp, peeled and deveined
- 10 g cornstarch
- 1 egg white
- 2 g salt
- 5 ml sesame oil
- 1 L chicken broth
- 100 g napa cabbage, cut into 3 cm pieces
- 100 g baby bok choy, quartered lengthwise
- 80 g shiitake mushrooms, stemmed and sliced
- 50 g snow peas, trimmed and halved on the diagonal (optional)
- 50 g bamboo shoots, sliced (optional)
- 15 ml light soy sauce
- 5 ml Shaoxing wine
- 2 g white pepper
- 5 ml vegetable oil (for soup)
- Salt to taste

---

## Method

1. Spread the cold cooked rice in an even layer, about 5 mm thick, on a parchment-lined baking sheet. Press it down firmly with wet hands so the grains compact together. Place in an oven at 100 C for about 2 hours, or until the rice is completely dry and brittle. Break into rough pieces about 5 cm across. You can also use store-bought rice crackers and skip this step entirely.

2. Place the sliced chicken breast in a bowl with half the cornstarch, half the egg white, and a pinch of salt. Mix until the chicken is evenly coated. In a separate bowl, do the same with the shrimp using the remaining cornstarch and egg white. Refrigerate both for 15 minutes. This velveting step keeps the proteins silky in the hot broth.

3. Bring a pot of water to a boil. Blanch the chicken strips for about 45 seconds, just until the outsides turn white. Remove with a slotted spoon. Blanch the shrimp for 30 seconds, just until they begin to curl. Remove and set aside with the chicken. Both will finish cooking in the soup.

4. In a large soup pot, bring the chicken broth to a boil over high heat. Add the soy sauce, Shaoxing wine, and white pepper. Taste and adjust salt.

5. Add the mushrooms and bamboo shoots first, as they take slightly longer. Simmer for 2 minutes.

6. Add the napa cabbage and bok choy. Cook for 1 to 2 minutes, until the greens are just wilted but still vibrant. Add a small splash of vegetable oil to the soup now. This thin film of oil helps the vegetables keep their bright color.

7. Return the blanched chicken and shrimp to the pot. Let everything come back to a vigorous boil. Add the snow peas in the last 30 seconds if using. Drizzle with sesame oil.

8. While the soup finishes, heat the frying oil in a wok or deep pot to 190 C. Fry the dried rice crackers in batches, pressing them under the oil with a spider or slotted spoon. They will puff up and turn golden within 15 to 30 seconds. Remove immediately and drain briefly on paper towels.

9. Work quickly now. Pour the boiling soup into a warmed tureen or large serving bowl. Bring it to the table immediately. Carry the hot fried rice crackers on a separate plate.

10. At the table, drop the fried rice crackers into the boiling soup in one motion. They will sizzle, pop, and crackle loudly for several seconds. Serve immediately while the crackers still have some texture, spooning broth, vegetables, and softened crackers into individual bowls.

## Why This Works

The dramatic sizzle occurs because the rice crackers are at frying temperature (around 190 C) when they hit the boiling soup (100 C). The extreme temperature difference causes rapid steam generation at the surface of each cracker, producing the crackling sound. The crackers also absorb broth quickly, transitioning from crispy to pleasantly chewy within a minute or two.

Velveting the chicken and shrimp with cornstarch and egg white creates a thin protective coating around each piece. This barrier slows heat transfer, preventing the proteins from seizing up and turning tough in the hot broth. The result is meat and shrimp that stay tender and almost silky.

Adding a small amount of oil to the boiling soup after the vegetables go in creates a thin lipid layer that coats the greens, sealing in their bright color and preventing the dull olive tone that comes from prolonged exposure to the acidic broth.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Chicken broth** provides collagen-derived gelatin, which gives the soup body and a satisfying mouthfeel. Homemade broth will produce a noticeably better result than store-bought, but low-sodium commercial broth works in a pinch.

**Shrimp** are rich in selenium, iodine, and the antioxidant astaxanthin, which gives them their pink color when cooked. They are a lean, high-protein addition that cooks in seconds.

**Napa cabbage** is a cruciferous vegetable rich in folate, vitamin C, and fiber. It contributes a sweet, mild flavor to the broth and a tender texture that contrasts with the chewy rice crackers.

## Substitutions and Variations

- **Rice crackers:** Store-bought rice crackers or rice cake chips work well and skip the drying step. Look for guoba or rice crust crackers at Asian grocery stores.
- **Proteins:** Use only shrimp, only chicken, or substitute sliced pork loin or firm tofu. Scallops are excellent in a more luxurious version.
- **Vegetables:** Snow peas, water chestnuts, baby corn, carrots, or straw mushrooms are all traditional additions. Use what looks fresh.
- **Seafood version:** Replace the chicken broth with a light seafood stock and use shrimp, scallops, and squid rings for a banquet-style presentation.
- **Vegetarian:** Use vegetable broth, firm tofu, and a wider range of mushrooms (wood ear, enoki, oyster) for depth.

---

## Serving Suggestions

This is a first-course soup meant to be served at the start of a multi-dish meal. It pairs well with a spread of stir-fried dishes, steamed fish, or roasted meats. The theatrical presentation makes it ideal for dinner parties and celebrations. Serve with a simple stir-fried green vegetable and steamed rice for the rest of the meal. Each person should get a mix of broth, vegetables, proteins, and softened rice crackers in their bowl.

---

## Storage and Reheating

The soup base (without rice crackers) stores well in the refrigerator for up to 3 days. Reheat to a full boil before serving. Fried rice crackers must be made fresh each time, as they lose their crunch within minutes of frying. Dried, unfried rice crackers can be stored in an airtight container at room temperature for several weeks, ready to fry when needed. Do not freeze the assembled soup, as the proteins will become rubbery and the vegetables will turn mushy.
