# Chinese Tea Eggs (茶叶蛋)

*Soft-boiled eggs marbled with a fragrant brine of black tea, star anise, cinnamon, and soy sauce, soaked overnight for deep flavor*

---

## Headnote

Chinese tea eggs are sold from simmering pots on street corners and in convenience stores across China, their fragrance drifting through morning markets and train stations. You can smell them before you see them. Star anise, cinnamon, black tea, and soy sauce create an aroma that is warm and spiced and deeply savory, the kind that makes you buy one even if you were not hungry.

The technique is simple but requires patience. Eggs are boiled, their shells cracked all over with the back of a spoon to create a web of fine fractures, then submerged in a spiced brine and left to soak for at least 24 hours. The brine seeps through the cracks and stains the egg white in a beautiful marbled pattern, like cracked porcelain. The flavor penetrates gently, giving the white a savory, tea-scented depth without overwhelming the egg itself.

This version uses a shorter initial boil and an ice bath to keep the yolk soft and slightly jammy rather than the traditional fully hard-cooked crumbly yolk. It borrows the timing from Japanese ramen eggs while keeping the Chinese tea egg flavor profile. The result is an egg with a creamy, golden center and a marbled exterior that looks like something from a museum. They keep in the fridge for days and get better as they soak, which makes them one of the best make-ahead snacks in any cuisine.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the brine
- 1000 to 1250 ml water (enough to submerge all eggs)
- 2 slices fresh ginger
- 3 whole star anise
- 1 cinnamon stick
- 2 bay leaves
- 2 tbsp black tea leaves
- 1 tsp Sichuan peppercorns
- 3 tbsp light soy sauce
- 4 tsp dark soy sauce
- 1 tsp sugar
- 2 tsp salt
- 2 tbsp Shaoxing wine

### For the eggs
- 12 large eggs, brought to room temperature

---

## Method

1. Make the brine. Add the water, ginger, star anise, cinnamon stick, bay leaves, tea leaves, Sichuan peppercorns, light soy sauce, dark soy sauce, sugar, salt, and Shaoxing wine to a medium pot. Bring to a boil, then reduce to a low simmer. Cover and simmer for 10 minutes to let the spices infuse. Turn off the heat, remove the lid, and let the brine cool completely. This can be done while you boil the eggs.

2. Boil the eggs. Bring a separate pot of water to a rolling boil. Gently lower the room-temperature eggs into the water one at a time using a large spoon. Do not drop them, as this will crack the shells unevenly. Boil for exactly 7 minutes. Set a timer.

3. Ice bath. When the timer goes off, immediately transfer the eggs to a bowl of ice water using a slotted spoon. Let them sit until completely cool to the touch, about 10 minutes. This stops the cooking and preserves the soft, jammy yolk.

4. Crack the shells. Once the eggs are cool, gently tap each one all over with the back of a small spoon. The goal is a dense web of fine cracks across the entire surface. Do not tap too hard. The yolk is still soft and the egg can break open if you are aggressive. Every crack is a channel for the brine to enter and create the marble pattern.

5. Soak for 24 hours. Place the cracked eggs in the cooled brine, making sure every egg is fully submerged. Cover and refrigerate for at least 24 hours. The longer they soak, the stronger the flavor and the more defined the marble pattern. Two to three days produces the best results.

6. Peel and serve. Remove eggs from the brine and peel carefully. The marbled pattern on the white will be revealed as the shell comes away. Serve whole or halved, at room temperature or cold.

## Why This Works

The cracked shell is the entire mechanism of this dish. Without cracks, the brine cannot reach the egg white and you end up with a plain hard-boiled egg sitting in flavored water. The cracks create channels for the soy sauce, tea, and spices to penetrate, staining the white in a pattern that follows the fracture lines. More cracks mean more flavor penetration and a more intricate pattern.

The 7-minute boil followed by an ice bath produces a yolk that is set but still creamy and slightly jammy in the center. Traditional Chinese tea eggs are typically simmered in the brine for an extended time, which cooks the yolk fully through to a firm, crumbly texture. Both versions are valid. This recipe splits the difference: the soft yolk of a ramen egg with the spiced exterior of a tea egg.

The brine is built on layers of flavor. Black tea provides tannins and a slight bitterness. Star anise and cinnamon add warm, aromatic sweetness. Sichuan peppercorn contributes a faint numbing quality. Light soy sauce provides salt and umami. Dark soy sauce adds color, staining the cracks a deep mahogany. Together they create a complex seasoning that tastes like more than the sum of its parts.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Eggs:** Each egg provides about 6 grams of complete protein, all essential amino acids, and is one of the best dietary sources of choline, a nutrient important for brain function and liver health. The yolk contains vitamin D, B12, and selenium. Traditional Chinese medicine considers eggs nourishing and mildly cooling, recommended for recovery and convalescence.

**Star anise:** Contains anethole, the same compound found in fennel and anise seed, which gives it its distinctive licorice-like aroma. In TCM, star anise is classified as warming and is used to warm the Kidney and Spleen, relieve cold-related abdominal pain, and improve digestion. It is also the natural source of shikimic acid, the precursor compound used in the production of the antiviral drug oseltamivir (Tamiflu).

**Black tea:** The tannins in black tea contribute a slight astringency to the brine that balances the richness of the soy sauce and the warmth of the spices. Tea polyphenols have been studied for antioxidant properties and potential cardiovascular benefits.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Fully hard-cooked yolk:** For the traditional crumbly yolk texture, boil the eggs for 12 minutes instead of 7 and skip the ice bath. Or simmer the cracked eggs directly in the brine over low heat for 2 hours, then let them soak overnight.

**Tea:** Any unflavored black tea works. Loose leaf is preferred over tea bags for stronger flavor. Pu-erh tea makes a darker, earthier version.

**Without Sichuan peppercorn:** Omit it entirely. The remaining spices carry the dish. The numbing quality is subtle here and many versions omit it.

**Five-spice variation:** Replace the individual spices with 2 teaspoons of Chinese five-spice powder for a quicker but less nuanced brine.

## Serving Suggestions

Tea eggs are a snack, a breakfast component, or a topping. Eat them on their own with a sprinkle of salt, halve them over a bowl of congee, slice them into a bowl of noodle soup, or pack them in a lunchbox. They are one of the most portable foods in Chinese cooking. For a complete snack spread, serve alongside [Scallion Pancakes](/recipes/scallion-pancakes) and a cup of hot tea.

---

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Keep the eggs in the brine for up to 3 to 4 days. The flavor continues to develop as they soak. After peeling, eat within 1 day.

**Serving temperature:** Tea eggs are traditionally eaten at room temperature or cold. They do not need reheating, though warming them briefly in the brine is an option if you prefer them warm.

**Freezing:** Not recommended. The egg white becomes rubbery when frozen and thawed.
