# Walnut Shrimp (核桃虾)

*Crispy cornstarch-coated shrimp tossed in a creamy mayo-condensed milk sauce with candied walnuts and blanched broccoli, the Chinese banquet classic*

---

## Headnote

Walnut shrimp is banquet food. It appears at Chinese wedding dinners and New Year celebrations, on tables laden with whole steamed fish and Peking duck, and it is always one of the first dishes to disappear. The combination sounds improbable on paper: fried shrimp, mayonnaise sauce, and candy-coated walnuts. But the combination works because each element provides something the others lack. The shrimp brings protein and a crispy coating. The sauce brings richness and a faint sweetness. The walnuts bring crunch and a toasted nuttiness that ties everything together.

The shrimp are coated in cornstarch only, not battered, which gives them a lighter, more delicate crunch than the heavy breaded versions many restaurants serve. Deepening the cuts along the back of each shrimp helps them curl into an attractive shape and creates more surface area for the coating and sauce to cling to. They fry fast, about two to three minutes, and come out golden and crispy.

The candied walnuts require patience. They are fried first at a moderate temperature, then tossed in a simple syrup of sugar and water that is cooked down until it crystallizes into a thin, hard candy shell. Pull them out too early and the coating will be chewy instead of crunchy. The sauce is mayonnaise, sweetened condensed milk, a drop of rice vinegar for balance, and optionally honey. It comes together in a bowl in thirty seconds and goes onto the hot shrimp right before serving. The broccoli blanches while the shrimp fry and forms a green border around the plate, which is as much about presentation as it is about eating. This is a celebration dish. It should look like one.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the candied walnuts
- 30 walnut halves
- 2 tbsp water
- 2 tbsp granulated sugar
- 1/8 tsp salt

### For the creamy sauce
- 60 ml mayonnaise (1/4 cup)
- 3 tbsp sweetened condensed milk
- 1/4 tsp rice vinegar
- 2 tsp honey (optional)
- 1/8 tsp salt

### For the shrimp and broccoli
- 450 g large shrimp (1 lb, 16/20 count), peeled and deveined
- 80 g cornstarch (1/3 cup)
- 225 g broccoli florets (8 oz)
- 720 to 960 ml neutral oil (3 to 4 cups, for frying)
- 1 tsp salt (for blanching water)
- 1 tsp oil (for blanching water)

---

## Method

1. Candy the walnuts. Heat the frying oil in a small deep pot to 160°C (325°F). Fry the walnut halves for about 4 minutes until they start turning golden. While they fry, combine the water, sugar, and salt in a small saucepan over low heat. Stir occasionally until reduced to a thin syrup, about 3 to 5 minutes. Scoop the fried walnuts into the syrup. Stir over medium-low heat until no liquid remains and the syrup has crystallized into a thin, hard candy shell. Transfer to a parchment-lined sheet and spread so they do not touch.

2. Mix the sauce. Combine the mayonnaise, condensed milk, rice vinegar, honey if using, and salt. Stir until smooth. Set aside at room temperature.

3. Prepare the shrimp. Deepen the cuts along the back of each shrimp so they open up and curl nicely when fried. Dredge the shrimp in cornstarch, set aside, then dredge a second time just before frying for a crispier coating.

4. Blanch the broccoli. Bring a pot of water to a boil with 1 teaspoon of salt and 1 teaspoon of oil. Blanch the broccoli for 30 seconds. Transfer immediately to cold water for 10 to 15 seconds, then drain. Arrange around the edge of a serving plate.

5. Fry the shrimp. Heat the oil (same oil from the walnuts) to 175°C (350°F). Give the shrimp a second dredge in cornstarch. Drop them into the oil one at a time in batches, frying for 2 to 3 minutes until golden. Do not overcrowd. Drain on a wire rack.

6. Toss and serve. Transfer all the fried shrimp to a large bowl. Pour the sauce over and toss gently until every piece is coated. Pour the sauced shrimp onto the broccoli-lined plate. Scatter the candied walnuts on top. Serve immediately.

---

## Why This Works

Cornstarch-only coating (no batter, no flour) produces a lighter, crispier shell than breading. The double dredge, once during prep and once just before frying, ensures a thick enough layer for crunch without heaviness. The shrimp absorb moisture from the marinade, so the second dredge catches what the first one missed.

The candied walnuts need the sugar syrup to fully crystallize. If you remove them while the syrup is still liquid, it will remain chewy and sticky. Cooking until no liquid remains and the coating looks dry and slightly frosted is the indicator of a proper candy shell.

The sauce is applied to the shrimp while they are still hot, which helps it adhere. Work quickly. The mayo-condensed milk base is rich but the rice vinegar provides just enough acidity to prevent it from tasting cloying.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Shrimp:** Lean protein rich in selenium, B12, iodine, and omega-3 fatty acids. The cornstarch coating keeps them crispy while the quick fry preserves their tender texture.

**Walnuts:** One of the richest plant sources of omega-3 fatty acids (alpha-linolenic acid). Also high in antioxidants, including polyphenols that have been studied for anti-inflammatory properties.

---

## Substitutions and Variations

**Cashews or pecans:** Both work as walnut substitutes with the same candying technique.

**Lighter sauce:** Replace the condensed milk with 2 tablespoons of Greek yogurt for a tangier, less sweet version.

**Without deep frying:** Air-fry the cornstarch-coated shrimp at 200°C (400°F) for 8 to 10 minutes, turning once.

---

## Serving Suggestions

This is a banquet dish meant to be part of a multi-course meal. Serve alongside steamed fish, [Lo Mein](/recipes/lo-mein), and a vegetable stir-fry. It also works as a standalone appetizer or party dish.

---

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store for up to 1 day. The coating softens significantly.

**Reheating:** Not recommended. The sauce and coating do not reheat well. Best eaten immediately.

**Freezing:** Not recommended.
