# Mongolian Beef (蒙古牛肉)

*Crispy cornstarch-coated flank steak in a dark soy-brown sugar sauce with garlic, ginger, dried chilies, and scallions*

---

## Headnote

Mongolian beef has nothing to do with Mongolia. In actual Mongolian cooking, meat is typically boiled and served with dipping sauces, not stir-fried in soy and sugar. The name is a Chinese-American invention, likely coined by a restaurant menu writer who needed something exotic-sounding for what is essentially a sweet soy sauce stir-fry with crispy beef and scallions. Nobody in Ulaanbaatar would recognize it, but everyone in a strip mall food court would.

None of which matters, because the dish is excellent. Flank steak is sliced thin, marinated with baking soda for tenderness, dredged in cornstarch, and seared in a hot wok until the coating is crispy. The sauce is brown sugar dissolved in hot water with soy sauce and a touch of dark soy for color. Garlic, ginger, and optional dried chilies go into the wok before the sauce, and scallions go in at the very end so they wilt just enough to soften without losing their color.

This version is intentionally less sweet than what you would get at P.F. Chang's or a typical takeout spot. The brown sugar is still there, but it is balanced by the soy sauce and the heat from the ginger and chilies. The scallions are generous, almost equal to the beef in volume, which is traditional in the better versions of this dish. They provide a fresh, sharp contrast to the rich, sticky sauce that makes each bite feel balanced rather than cloying.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the marinade
- 450 g flank steak (1 lb), sliced against the grain into 1/4-inch strips
- 2 tsp neutral oil
- 2 tsp Shaoxing wine (optional)
- 1 tsp soy sauce
- 1 tbsp cornstarch
- 1 tbsp water
- 1/4 tsp baking soda

### For the sauce
- 60 g light brown sugar (1/4 cup)
- 180 ml hot water or stock (3/4 cup)
- 60 ml soy sauce (1/4 cup)
- 1 tsp dark soy sauce (optional, for color)

### For cooking
- 120 g cornstarch (1/2 cup, for dredging)
- 160 ml neutral oil (2/3 cup, for searing)
- 1 tsp fresh ginger, minced
- 8 dried red chili peppers (optional)
- 3 cloves garlic, finely chopped
- 4 scallions, white and green parts separated, cut on the diagonal into 2-inch pieces
- 1 1/2 tbsp cornstarch mixed with 2 tbsp water (slurry)

---

## Method

1. Marinate the beef. Combine the sliced beef with the oil, Shaoxing wine, soy sauce, cornstarch, water, and baking soda. Marinate for 1 hour. The beef should still be moist after marinating. If it looks dry, add a tablespoon of water.

2. Mix the sauce. Dissolve the brown sugar in the hot water or stock. Stir in the soy sauce and dark soy sauce. Set aside.

3. Dredge the beef. Coat each marinated beef slice thoroughly in the cornstarch.

4. Sear the beef. Heat the oil in a wok over high heat. Just before it smokes, spread the beef in an even layer. Sear for 1 minute per side until the coating is crispy. Turn off the heat and transfer the beef to a plate.

5. Cook the aromatics. Drain the oil from the wok, leaving 1 tablespoon. Return to medium-high heat. Add the ginger and dried chilies (break 1 to 2 in half for more heat). After 15 seconds, add the garlic and the white parts of the scallions. Stir for another 15 seconds.

6. Build the sauce. Pour in the premixed sauce. Let it simmer for about 2 minutes. Slowly stir in the cornstarch slurry. Cook until the sauce thickens enough to coat the back of a spoon.

7. Finish. Add the beef and the green parts of the scallions. Toss everything together for 30 seconds. The scallions should just be wilted. There should be almost no liquid remaining; the sauce should be clinging to the beef. If there is still sauce pooling, increase the heat slightly and stir until thickened.

8. Serve immediately with steamed rice.

## Why This Works

The cornstarch dredge creates a crispy shell around each beef strip that absorbs the sauce without becoming soggy if you eat it quickly. The baking soda velvet in the marinade ensures the meat inside the crust stays tender and juicy.

Brown sugar dissolved in hot water creates a sauce base that is already slightly caramelized. When this reduces in the wok with soy sauce, it becomes a concentrated, glossy coating with a depth of flavor that plain sugar cannot achieve. Dark soy sauce adds color without adding much salt, giving the dish its characteristic dark sheen.

The scallions going in last and just wilting is deliberate. Overcooked scallions become slimy and lose their sharpness. Barely wilted scallions provide a fresh, grassy contrast to the rich, sticky beef.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Flank steak:** A lean cut rich in protein, iron, and B12. Slicing thin against the grain shortens the muscle fibers, making each piece tender enough to chew easily.

**Scallions:** Used generously here as a vegetable, not just a garnish. Raw scallion whites provide sharpness while the green parts add freshness when just wilted.

---

## Substitutions and Variations

**Spice level:** The dried chilies are optional. For more heat, break 2 to 3 in half. For no heat, omit entirely.

**Less oil:** Use 3 tablespoons and saute instead of shallow-frying. Less crispy but still good.

**Chicken or tofu:** Both work with the same sauce and technique. Adjust searing time accordingly.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store for up to 2 days. The coating softens but the flavor remains.

**Reheating:** Hot oven at 400°F for 5 to 8 minutes on a wire rack, or toss in a hot wok for 1 to 2 minutes.

**Freezing:** Not recommended. The cornstarch coating and sauce break down.
