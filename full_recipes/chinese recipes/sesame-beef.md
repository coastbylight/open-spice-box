# Sesame Beef (芝麻牛肉)

*Crispy flank steak dredged in sesame seeds and cornstarch, tossed in a sweet-sour soy sauce with garlic and scallions*

---

## Headnote

Sesame beef is the Chinese-American takeout dish that gets everything right about the crispy-meat-in-sweet-sauce formula. The beef is sliced thin, marinated with cornstarch and baking soda for a velvet texture, then dredged in a mixture of cornstarch and toasted sesame seeds. The sesame seeds toast further during searing and embed themselves into the crispy coating, so every bite has a nutty crunch that plain cornstarch alone cannot provide.

The sauce is where this dish distinguishes itself from Mongolian beef and orange beef. It leans slightly acidic, with rice vinegar cutting through the brown sugar and sesame oil adding a warm, rounded nuttiness. The balance is sweet-sour-savory, with the sesame flavor running through both the coating and the sauce. A cornstarch slurry thickens the sauce into a glaze that clings to each piece without pooling.

The marinating time is the only thing between you and dinner. An hour is the minimum for the baking soda velvet to work, though you can marinate overnight without any harm. The actual cooking takes about fifteen minutes, and most of that is hands-off while the sauce simmers.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the marinade
- 340 g flank steak (12 oz), sliced against the grain into 1/4-inch strips
- 1 tbsp water
- 2 tsp cornstarch
- 2 tsp Shaoxing wine
- 1 tsp sesame oil
- 1 tsp oyster sauce
- 1/4 tsp baking soda

### For the sauce
- 160 ml hot water (2/3 cup)
- 3 tbsp brown sugar
- 2 1/2 tbsp light soy sauce
- 1 tbsp oyster sauce
- 1 1/2 tsp sesame oil
- 1/2 tsp rice vinegar
- Pinch of white pepper

### For dredging and cooking
- 60 g cornstarch (about 1/4 cup)
- 2 1/2 tbsp toasted sesame seeds
- 120 ml neutral oil (1/2 cup, for searing)
- 2 cloves garlic, chopped
- 1 tbsp scallions, chopped
- 2 tsp cornstarch mixed with 1 tbsp water (slurry)

---

## Method

1. Marinate the beef. Combine the sliced beef with the water, cornstarch, Shaoxing wine, sesame oil, oyster sauce, and baking soda. Stir well. Marinate for at least 1 hour, or overnight.

2. Mix the sauce. Dissolve the brown sugar in the hot water. Add the soy sauce, oyster sauce, sesame oil, rice vinegar, and white pepper. Stir and set aside.

3. Dredge the beef. Mix the cornstarch and sesame seeds in a shallow dish. Dredge each beef slice until thoroughly coated. Shake off excess.

4. Sear the beef. Heat the oil in a wok over medium-high heat. When a piece of beef sizzles on contact, the oil is ready. Lay the beef slices in an even layer. Shallow-fry for 1 minute per side until the coating is crispy. Transfer to a plate in a single layer to maintain crispness.

5. Clean the wok. Let the particles settle, drain the oil, and wipe the wok clean with a paper towel. Add 1 tablespoon of oil back to the wok over medium heat.

6. Build the sauce. Add the garlic and cook for 15 seconds. Pour in the sauce and bring to a simmer. Add the scallions. Stir in the cornstarch slurry and cook until the sauce thickens enough to coat a spoon. Add a teaspoon of hot water if the sauce gets too thick.

7. Finish. Add the beef and toss until every piece is coated in the glossy sauce. Serve immediately with steamed rice.

---

## Why This Works

Mixing toasted sesame seeds into the cornstarch dredge embeds them into the coating so they stay attached during searing and sauce-tossing. The seeds toast further in the hot oil, intensifying their flavor. Sesame oil in both the marinade and the sauce reinforces the sesame theme from the inside out.

The rice vinegar is a small addition with a large effect. It provides acidity that balances the brown sugar and prevents the sauce from tasting one-dimensionally sweet. The white pepper adds a gentle, aromatic heat that registers in the nose rather than on the tongue.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Sesame seeds:** Rich in calcium, iron, and magnesium. They contain lignans (sesamin and sesamol) studied for antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Toasting releases their aromatic oils and deepens their nutty flavor.

**Rice vinegar:** Milder and less acidic than Western vinegars, rice vinegar provides brightness without sharpness. It contains amino acids from the rice fermentation process.

---

## Substitutions and Variations

**Skirt steak:** Works well as a substitute for flank steak. Slice thin against the grain.

**Chicken:** Boneless thighs cut into strips work with the same marinade and technique.

**More sesame:** Garnish with additional toasted sesame seeds after saucing for extra crunch.

---

## Serving Suggestions

Serve with steamed white rice and steamed broccoli. Also works well over [Lo Mein](/recipes/lo-mein) noodles.

---

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store for up to 2 days. The coating softens.

**Reheating:** Oven at 400°F for 5 to 8 minutes on a wire rack. Avoid the microwave.

**Freezing:** Not recommended.
