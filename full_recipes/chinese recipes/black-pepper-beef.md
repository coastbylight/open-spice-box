# Black Pepper Beef (黑椒牛柳)

*Tender beef strips stir-fried with bell pepper and red onion in a peppery sauce of soy, oyster sauce, and a splash of red wine*

---

## Headnote

The Cantonese name for this dish is haak jiu ngauh lauh, and the word lauh is worth knowing. It refers to the willow-style cut, long thin strips of meat that cook fast in a hot wok and pick up sauce on every surface. This is the Chinese approach to steak: rather than serving a whole slab of meat and letting the diner cut it, the cook does the knife work first and the wok work second. The result is beef that is tender, thoroughly seasoned, and ready to eat with chopsticks.

Black pepper is the star, which is unusual in Chinese cooking where white pepper is more common. Here the coarser, more aromatic black pepper goes into the marinade and drives the flavor of the entire dish. It is not a background note. You should taste it in every bite, warm and sharp and slightly floral, backed by the savory depth of oyster sauce and soy.

The technique has a step that surprises most Western cooks: rinsing the raw beef in cold water before marinating. This is common in Cantonese cooking and removes the myoglobin, the protein that gives raw beef its bloody color and raw-meat smell. The result is cleaner-tasting beef that takes on the marinade more readily. After rinsing, the beef is drained thoroughly and pressed dry, then massaged with the marinade to help it absorb.

A splash of red wine goes into both the marinade and the sauce. This is a Cantonese restaurant touch that adds a fruity acidity you cannot get from Shaoxing wine alone. It is not traditional in the strictest sense, but it has been standard in Hong Kong-style black pepper beef for decades. The bell pepper and red onion cook for just long enough to soften without losing their crunch, and the sauce thickens into a glossy coating that pulls everything together. Fast, punchy, and deeply satisfying over steamed rice.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the beef marinade
- 340 g New York strip or beef tenderloin (12 oz), cut into thin strips against the grain
- 1 tsp coarsely ground black pepper
- 1 tbsp cornstarch
- 1 tbsp oyster sauce
- 1 tsp light soy sauce
- 1 tbsp Shaoxing wine
- 1 tbsp water
- 1 tbsp red wine
- 1 tsp sesame oil
- 2 tbsp corn or vegetable oil (added last, to seal)

### For the sauce
- 1 tbsp cornstarch
- 1 tbsp water
- 1 tsp sugar
- 1 tsp light soy sauce
- 1 tsp dark soy sauce
- 1 tbsp Shaoxing wine
- 1 tbsp red wine
- 1/2 tsp salt
- 1 tsp oyster sauce

### For the stir-fry
- 1 bell pepper (any color), cut into strips
- 1/2 red onion, sliced into thin strips
- 2 cloves garlic, minced
- 2 tbsp corn or vegetable oil

---

## Method

1. Rinse the beef. Place the cut beef strips in a bowl of cold water. Massage gently for 1 to 2 minutes. This removes the myoglobin and cleans the surface. Drain in a colander and press firmly to remove as much water as possible.

2. Marinate. Combine the black pepper, cornstarch, oyster sauce, soy sauce, Shaoxing wine, water, red wine, and sesame oil in a bowl. Add the beef and massage for 30 to 60 seconds until the marinade is absorbed. Set aside. Just before cooking, add the 2 tablespoons of oil to the marinated beef and mix for 20 to 30 seconds. This coats the strips in oil, which seals in moisture during the high-heat sear.

3. Mix the sauce. In a separate bowl, combine the cornstarch, water, sugar, soy sauces, Shaoxing wine, red wine, salt, and oyster sauce. Stir until the cornstarch dissolves. Set near the stove.

4. Heat the wok. Set the stove to high heat. Let the wok heat for 2 to 3 minutes until smoking. Add the 2 tablespoons of cooking oil and swirl.

5. Cook the beef. Add the oil-coated beef to the wok. Spread into an even layer and let it sear undisturbed for about 30 seconds. Stir-fry until just cooked through, about 2 minutes total. Do not overcook. Remove the beef and set aside.

6. Cook the aromatics and vegetables. In the same wok, add the minced garlic and stir for 10 seconds until fragrant. Add the bell pepper and red onion. Stir-fry for 1 to 2 minutes until slightly softened but still crunchy.

7. Combine and sauce. Return the beef to the wok. Give the sauce a stir and pour it in. Stir constantly until the sauce thickens and coats everything in a glossy, peppery glaze, about 30 to 45 seconds.

8. Serve immediately with steamed white rice.

## Why This Works

Rinsing the beef in cold water removes surface myoglobin and blood proteins that can give the meat a gamey or metallic taste when stir-fried at high heat. This is a standard Cantonese prep step for beef stir-fries and produces a cleaner, more neutral-tasting meat that lets the black pepper and sauce flavors come through clearly.

Adding oil to the marinated beef right before cooking creates a thin oil barrier around each strip. In the screaming-hot wok, this oil layer heats instantly and seals the surface, preventing the moisture inside from escaping. The result is beef that stays juicy and tender rather than steaming in its own liquid.

The red wine in both the marinade and the sauce is a Hong Kong restaurant technique. The fruity acidity of the wine adds a dimension that rice wine cannot replicate. It bridges the savory elements (oyster sauce, soy) and the aromatic elements (black pepper, garlic) in a way that rounds out the entire dish.

Black pepper rather than white pepper gives this dish a more robust, complex heat. The volatile compounds in black pepper (piperine, plus the aromatic terpenes in the outer husk that white pepper lacks) stand up to the strong flavors of the soy and oyster sauce without disappearing.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Black pepper:** The outer husk of the peppercorn contains aromatic terpenes that white pepper lacks, giving it a more complex, floral heat. Piperine, the active compound, has been extensively studied for enhancing nutrient absorption, particularly of curcumin from turmeric. In traditional Chinese medicine, black pepper is classified as warming, used to stimulate digestion and warm the stomach.

**Beef (New York strip):** A moderately lean cut with good marbling that stays tender when sliced thin and cooked fast. High in protein, iron, zinc, and B12.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Beef cut:** Tenderloin is the most tender but expensive. Flank steak works if sliced very thin against the grain and marinated with 1/4 tsp baking soda for extra tenderness.

**Without red wine:** Add an extra tablespoon of Shaoxing wine. The dish will be slightly less complex but still good.

**More vegetables:** Add asparagus, snap peas, or mushrooms with the bell pepper.

**Chicken or shrimp:** Both work with the same marinade and sauce. Reduce cooking time for shrimp.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store for up to 2 days. The pepper flavor intensifies overnight.

**Reheating:** Toss in a hot wok for 1 to 2 minutes. Add a splash of water if the sauce has thickened.

**Freezing:** Not recommended. The vegetables and sauce texture degrade.
