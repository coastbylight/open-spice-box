# Chicken Feet (鳳爪)

*Dim sum chicken feet braised in fermented black bean sauce until the skin is puffy and meltingly tender*

---

## Headnote

Chicken feet are one of those dishes that divides people before they have even tried them. The idea of eating feet puts some people off entirely. But in Cantonese dim sum culture, fung zao (literally "phoenix claws") is one of the most popular items on the cart, and for good reason. When prepared properly, the skin puffs up into a wrinkled, almost gelatinous pillow, the cartilage softens until it yields with the slightest pressure, and the black bean sauce clings to every crevice and fold. There is almost no "meat" in the Western sense. The pleasure is textural: the soft chew of collagen-rich skin and the satisfying crunch of tiny bones giving way between your teeth.

The texture comes from a multi-stage cooking process that is more involved than most home cooks expect. First, the feet are parboiled, which cleans them and makes it easy to trim the nails. Then they are deep-fried. This step is dramatic and a bit messy, as the feet spit and pop in the hot oil, but it is essential. The frying causes the skin to blister and puff. An immediate plunge into ice water then shocks the puffed skin, causing it to contract and wrinkle into that characteristic crinkled texture.

After the fry and ice bath, the feet are braised low and slow in a sauce built from fermented black beans, garlic, ginger, star anise, soy sauce, and sugar. The long braise breaks down the collagen and softens the cartilage, while the sauce penetrates deep into all the crevices created by the frying and shocking process. Some versions finish with a final steam, which further tenderizes the feet and concentrates the sauce.

The result is pure dim sum luxury. Each foot is picked up by the toes, and the bones and cartilage are nibbled off, leaving the sauce-coated skin as the real prize. It is not a dish you eat in a hurry.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### Chicken Feet
- 500 g chicken feet (about 12 to 14 feet)
- Vegetable oil for deep frying (about 750 ml)
- 15 ml white vinegar (for parboiling water)
- Ice water for shocking

### Braising Sauce
- 25 g fermented black beans (dou chi), rinsed and roughly chopped
- 5 cloves garlic, minced
- 10 g fresh ginger, minced
- 2 whole star anise
- 1 small dried red chili, broken in half (or more to taste)
- 20 ml light soy sauce
- 10 ml dark soy sauce
- 15 ml oyster sauce
- 10 g sugar
- 10 ml Shaoxing wine
- 5 ml sesame oil
- 5 g cornstarch mixed with 15 ml water
- 250 ml water or chicken broth

### Garnish
- 30 g roasted peanuts
- Sliced scallion
- Sliced fresh red chili

---

## Method

1. Rinse the chicken feet thoroughly under cold running water. Place them in a pot, cover with cold water, and add the white vinegar. Bring to a boil and cook for 5 minutes. The vinegar helps the skin dry out and crisp better during frying. Drain and rinse under cold water.

2. Using kitchen shears, trim the nails from each toe. This is much easier to do after parboiling, when the nails are softened. Cut off any rough or discolored spots.

3. Pat the chicken feet completely dry with paper towels. This is critical for safety. Any residual moisture will cause violent splattering when the feet hit the hot oil. Take your time and blot every crevice between the toes.

4. Heat the vegetable oil in a deep pot or wok to 190 C. The oil should be deep enough to submerge the feet. Carefully lower the feet into the oil in small batches (3 to 4 at a time). Stand back, as they will pop and splatter. Fry for 3 to 4 minutes until the skin blisters, puffs, and turns deep golden.

5. Remove the fried feet immediately and plunge them into a large bowl of ice water. Let them soak for at least 15 minutes, or up to 1 hour. The longer the soak, the more wrinkled and puffy the skin becomes as the rapid cooling contracts the blistered surface.

6. Drain the soaked feet and cut each one in half through the center for easier eating and better sauce penetration.

7. In a wok or braising pot, heat 15 ml of vegetable oil over medium heat. Add the chopped black beans, garlic, ginger, star anise, and dried chili. Stir-fry for 30 seconds until very fragrant.

8. Add the soy sauces, oyster sauce, sugar, Shaoxing wine, and water. Bring to a simmer.

9. Add the chicken feet to the braising liquid. The liquid should mostly cover them. Bring to a gentle simmer, then reduce heat to low. Cover and braise for 45 to 60 minutes, stirring every 15 minutes, until the feet are very tender and the skin is soft and slightly gelatinous.

10. In the last 5 minutes of braising, stir in the cornstarch slurry to thicken the remaining sauce. Add the sesame oil.

11. For an optional final step that many dim sum restaurants use, transfer the braised feet and sauce to a heatproof bowl, scatter the peanuts over the top, and steam for an additional 15 to 20 minutes. This further softens the feet and allows the peanuts to absorb some of the sauce.

12. Transfer to a serving plate or keep in the steaming bowl. Garnish with sliced scallion and fresh chili. Serve warm.

## Why This Works

Deep frying the parboiled feet causes the moisture inside the skin to flash into steam, creating thousands of tiny bubbles that puff and blister the surface. When the puffed feet hit the ice bath, the expanded skin contracts rapidly but cannot return to its original shape, creating the wrinkled, honeycomb-like texture that defines good dim sum chicken feet. This textured surface also dramatically increases the surface area for sauce absorption.

The long braise breaks down collagen in the skin, tendons, and cartilage. Chicken feet are almost entirely collagen, and prolonged gentle heat converts that collagen into gelatin, which gives the finished dish its soft, almost melting mouthfeel. The braising liquid itself becomes rich and slightly viscous from the dissolved gelatin.

Fermented black beans thrive in long-cooked dishes because their flavor deepens and mellows over time. The initial sauté blooms their volatile aromatics, and the extended braising integrates their umami into the sauce.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Chicken feet** are one of the richest dietary sources of collagen. They contain very little muscle meat but are packed with skin, cartilage, and connective tissue. Some studies suggest that dietary collagen peptides may support joint health and skin elasticity, though more research is needed.

**Fermented black beans** provide concentrated umami through naturally occurring glutamate. As a fermented soy product, they contain isoflavones and beneficial compounds produced during the fermentation process.

**Star anise** contains anethole, the compound responsible for its licorice-like flavor. It also contains shikimic acid, which is the starting material for the antiviral drug oseltamivir (Tamiflu), though eating star anise does not provide therapeutic antiviral effects.

## Substitutions and Variations

- **Skipping the frying:** Some home versions skip the deep-fry and go straight to braising after parboiling. The feet will be tender but will not have the signature puffy, wrinkled texture. They will be noticeably different from the dim sum restaurant version.
- **Oven shortcut for drying:** Instead of frying, brush the parboiled feet with oil and bake at 220 C for 20 minutes until blistered. Then proceed with the ice bath soak. The result is not identical but avoids deep frying.
- **Spice level:** Increase the dried chilies to 3 or 4, or add a tablespoon of chili bean paste to the braising sauce for a spicier version.
- **Peanuts:** Roasted peanuts are traditional and add a nice crunch. Some versions use raw peanuts added early in the braise so they soften completely.
- **Without star anise:** The dish works without it, but you lose the warm, aromatic backbone. Five-spice powder (a pinch) can substitute.

## Serving Suggestions

Serve as part of a dim sum spread alongside siu mai, har gow, steamed spare ribs, and turnip cake. At home, chicken feet work well as a shared appetizer or side dish before a larger meal. Set them out with toothpicks and a stack of napkins. Steamed rice alongside is welcome for soaking up the rich black bean sauce. Beer or hot tea both pair well.

---

## Storage and Reheating

Refrigerate in an airtight container for up to 3 days. The braising sauce will gel when cold due to the high gelatin content. Reheat by steaming for 8 to 10 minutes, which is the best method for maintaining the soft, puffy texture. Microwaving works but can make the skin slightly rubbery in spots. The flavor actually improves after a day in the refrigerator as the sauce penetrates further. Freezes well for up to 2 months. Thaw overnight in the refrigerator and reheat by steaming.
