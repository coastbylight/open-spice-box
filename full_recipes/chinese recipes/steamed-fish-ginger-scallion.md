# Steamed Fish with Ginger and Scallion (薑蔥蒸魚)

*Whole steamed fish finished with a sizzle of smoking hot oil over fresh ginger and scallion, the crown jewel of Cantonese home cooking*

---

## Headnote

In Cantonese cooking, the ability to steam a fish well is considered a mark of real skill. It sounds paradoxical, since steaming is the most hands-off cooking method there is. But that simplicity is exactly the point. There is nothing to hide behind. No heavy sauce, no aggressive seasoning, no caramelized crust. The fish arrives at the table looking almost exactly as it did when it was alive, and if you have done your job, the flesh will be silky and just barely set, pulling away from the bone in clean, moist flakes. If you have not, it will be dry, chalky, and overcooked, and everyone at the table will know it.

The technique itself is straightforward. Ginger slices and scallion greens go under and on top of the fish to neutralize any fishiness and perfume the steam. The fish cooks over high, rolling steam for about 12 minutes, depending on thickness. But the defining moment of this dish comes after the steaming, not during it. You pile julienned ginger and fresh scallion strips on the cooked fish, then pour smoking hot oil directly over them. The oil hits the aromatics and they sizzle violently, blooming their fragrance in a way that raw garnishes never could. The kitchen fills with a smell that is impossible to describe and impossible to forget. That sizzle is the sound of Cantonese home cooking at its best.

After the oil comes a light sauce of soy, a touch of cooking wine, and sesame oil, poured around and over the fish. The sauce mixes with the juices that the fish released during steaming, creating a thin, intensely savory liquid that you spoon over rice. The fish itself needs almost nothing else. Freshness is the only ingredient that matters, and no amount of technique can compensate for its absence.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the fish
- 1 whole tilapia (about 1.5 lb / 680 g), cleaned and scaled
- 1 oz (28 g) fresh ginger, divided: half sliced into thin coins, half cut into fine julienne
- 5 stalks scallions, divided: 2 stalks cut into 3-inch lengths, 3 stalks cut into thin strips
- a few sprigs of fresh cilantro, for garnish

### For the sauce
- 2 tbsp light soy sauce
- 1/2 tsp dark soy sauce
- 1/2 tbsp Shaoxing cooking wine
- 1/4 tsp ground white pepper
- 1 tbsp water
- 1/2 tsp sugar
- 1/2 tsp salt
- 1 tbsp sesame oil

### For the hot oil finish
- 2 tbsp vegetable or peanut oil

---

## Method

1. Pat the fish completely dry inside and out with paper towels. Make two or three diagonal slashes on each side of the fish, cutting about halfway to the bone. This allows steam to penetrate evenly and helps the fish cook at the same rate from the thickest part to the thinnest.

2. Scatter the ginger slices and the 3-inch scallion lengths on a heatproof plate large enough to hold the fish. Lay the fish on top. Tuck a few ginger slices and scallion pieces inside the cavity as well. These aromatics serve as a bed that lifts the fish slightly off the plate and perfumes the steam from below.

3. Fill a wok or large pot with enough water to come about 2 inches below the steamer rack. Bring the water to a vigorous, rolling boil over high heat. You want aggressive steam from the start.

4. Place the plate with the fish on the steamer rack. Cover with a tight-fitting lid. Steam over high heat for 12 minutes. Do not reduce the heat and do not lift the lid during cooking. The fish is done when the flesh at the thickest part near the backbone is opaque and flakes easily when pressed gently with a chopstick.

5. While the fish steams, prepare the sauce. In a small bowl, combine the light soy sauce, dark soy sauce, Shaoxing wine, white pepper, water, sugar, salt, and sesame oil. Stir until the sugar dissolves. Set aside.

6. When the fish is done, carefully remove the plate from the steamer. The plate will be very hot and there will be liquid pooled around the fish. Carefully pour off and discard this steaming liquid, as it can taste fishy and will dilute the sauce.

7. Remove and discard the cooked ginger slices and scallion pieces from under and around the fish. They have done their job and will look limp and spent.

8. Scatter the julienned ginger and the thin scallion strips over the top of the fish in an even layer. Pile them generously along the center.

9. Heat the 2 tablespoons of oil in a small saucepan or ladle held over a burner until the oil is smoking hot and shimmering. You need the oil at its highest temperature for this step to work.

10. Pour the smoking hot oil directly over the julienned ginger and scallion strips on the fish. The aromatics will sizzle and crackle on contact, releasing an intense fragrance. This is the defining moment of the dish. The oil flash-cooks the raw aromatics and blooms their essential oils.

11. Pour the prepared sauce evenly over and around the fish. Garnish with fresh cilantro sprigs. Serve immediately, spooning the sauce from the plate over rice.

## Why This Works

Steaming over high, constant heat cooks the fish quickly and evenly, preserving its moisture and delicate texture. Unlike poaching or braising, where the fish sits in liquid and can easily overcook, steaming surrounds the fish with moist heat without waterlogging the flesh.

Pouring off the steaming liquid before saucing is a critical and often overlooked step. The liquid released during steaming contains dissolved proteins and can have a fishy taste. Leaving it on the plate would dilute the carefully balanced sauce and muddy the clean flavors.

The smoking hot oil finish works because of the Maillard reaction and the rapid volatilization of aromatic compounds in the ginger and scallion. At room temperature, these aromatics release their fragrance slowly. When hit with oil at over 200 degrees Celsius, their volatile oils are released all at once, creating an intensity of flavor and aroma that raw garnishes cannot achieve.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Tilapia** is a mild, affordable white fish that steams well due to its lean flesh. Other good options for steaming include sea bass, snapper, or grouper. The key is freshness. A truly fresh whole fish should have clear, bright eyes, red gills, and firm flesh that springs back when pressed.

**Ginger** contains gingerol, a bioactive compound with documented anti-inflammatory properties. In Chinese culinary tradition, ginger is always paired with fish and seafood for both flavor and digestive reasons.

---

## Substitutions and Variations

- **Fish:** Sea bass, red snapper, striped bass, or any fresh whole white fish works well. Fillets can be substituted, though they will cook in 6 to 8 minutes rather than 12. Adjust timing based on thickness.
- **Soy sauce:** If you cannot find dark soy sauce, use all light soy sauce and add a tiny pinch of sugar to compensate for the missing caramel sweetness.
- **Without Shaoxing wine:** Dry sherry is the closest substitute. In a pinch, omit it entirely rather than using mirin or rice vinegar, which would change the flavor profile.
- **Spicier version:** Add a sliced red chili pepper to the julienned ginger and scallion topping before pouring the hot oil.

---

## Serving Suggestions

Steamed fish is the centerpiece of a Cantonese home meal. Serve it alongside steamed jasmine rice, a stir-fried green vegetable like gai lan or bok choy, and a light soup such as [Egg Drop Soup](/recipes/egg-drop-soup) or [Winter Melon Soup](/recipes/dong-gua-tang). The sauce from the fish plate is meant to be spooned over rice, so make sure everyone at the table has a bowl ready.

---

## Storage and Reheating

Steamed fish is best eaten immediately. The delicate texture deteriorates quickly once it cools, and reheating tends to dry out the flesh. If you must store leftovers, refrigerate in an airtight container for up to 1 day. Reheat gently by steaming for 3 to 4 minutes or flake the cold fish into congee or fried rice. Not recommended for freezing.
