# Clams in Black Bean Sauce (豉汁炒蜆)

*Parboiled clams stir-fried with fermented black beans, garlic, ginger, and chili in a glossy Cantonese sauce*

---

## Headnote

Seafood and fermented black beans are two pillars of Cantonese cooking, and when they meet in a hot wok the result is one of those dishes that smells so good it draws people into the kitchen before you have even finished cooking. The fermented black beans (douchi) bring a deep, salty, funky umami that amplifies the briny sweetness of the clams rather than covering it.

The technique uses a two-stage cooking method that is worth understanding because it applies to all shellfish stir-fries. The clams are parboiled first in plain water just until they pop open, which cooks them to the perfect point of doneness. Then they go into the wok with the sauce for only 30 seconds, just long enough to coat them. If you try to cook clams entirely in the wok, some will overcook while others remain raw, and the liquid they release will thin the sauce into a watery mess. Parboiling first solves both problems.

The aromatics are chopped small so they distribute evenly: garlic, ginger, chili, scallion whites, red onion, and bell pepper. They cook fast in hot oil before the fermented black bean paste goes in on low heat for just a few seconds to bloom its flavor. Then the parboiled clams go back in, the sauce follows, and a cornstarch slurry thickens everything into a glossy coating. A drizzle of sesame oil at the end adds the final layer of aroma. The whole wok stage takes about two minutes. The eating takes longer.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the clams
- 900 g clams or cockles (about 2 lb), soaked in cold water for 30 minutes
- 7 g fresh ginger (about 1/4 oz), minced
- 3 cloves garlic, minced
- 2 whole chili peppers, finely chopped
- 3 stalks scallion, white parts finely chopped, greens reserved for garnish
- 3 thin layers red onion, finely chopped
- 30 g red bell pepper (about 1 oz), finely chopped
- 2 tbsp cooking oil
- 1 tsp cooking oil (for finishing)

### For the sauce
- 2 tsp light soy sauce
- 1 tsp dark soy sauce
- 1 tbsp oyster sauce
- 60 ml water (1/4 cup)
- 1/4 tsp white pepper
- 1 tsp sugar

### For seasoning and thickening
- 1 1/4 tbsp fermented black bean sauce (douchi paste)
- 1 tsp sesame oil
- 1 tbsp cornstarch mixed with 3 tbsp water (slurry)

---

## Method

1. Soak and clean the clams. Place clams in cold water for 30 minutes to purge sand. Drain, discarding the sandy water.

2. Mix the sauce. Combine the soy sauces, oyster sauce, water, white pepper, and sugar. Set aside. Mix the cornstarch slurry separately.

3. Parboil the clams. Boil a large amount of water in the wok on high heat. Add the clams and cook until they open, about 4 minutes. Remove opened clams. For any that remain closed, pry open with a small knife and check the smell. Discard any that smell off. Drain and clean the wok.

4. Stir-fry the aromatics. Heat the wok on high heat. Add 2 tablespoons of oil. Add the ginger, garlic, scallion whites, and chili pepper. Lower to medium heat and cook until fragrant, about 15 to 20 seconds. Turn heat to low, add the fermented black bean sauce, and stir for 5 to 10 seconds. Add the onion and bell pepper and cook for 30 to 35 seconds.

5. Combine and sauce. Turn heat to medium-high. Add the clams and stir-fry for 25 to 30 seconds. Add the sauce mixture and turn to the highest heat. Stir for about 50 seconds. Give the cornstarch slurry a stir and add gradually, stirring until the sauce thickens to your preference. You may not need all of it.

6. Finish. Turn off heat. Drizzle sesame oil and finishing oil over the top. Stir to distribute, garnish with scallion greens, and serve immediately.

---

## Why This Works

The two-stage method (parboil then wok-toss) prevents the clams from overcooking and from releasing excess liquid into the stir-fry. Clams that go directly into a wok dump water as they open, turning the sauce watery and steaming the meat rather than searing it. Pre-cooking them separately means they enter the wok already done, and the brief 30-second toss is just for coating.

Fermented black beans are added on low heat for just a few seconds. High heat would burn them and turn the flavor bitter. The brief bloom releases their aroma without scorching.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Clams:** High in iron (one of the richest food sources), B12, and zinc. Low in fat, high in protein. The briny liquor they release during cooking is itself a seasoning.

**Fermented black beans (douchi):** One of the oldest fermented foods in Chinese cuisine, predating soy sauce. Rich in umami from natural glutamate produced during fermentation.

---

## Substitutions and Variations

**Mussels:** Work with the same technique. They open faster, about 2 to 3 minutes.

**Shrimp or squid:** Both can replace clams. Skip the parboiling and stir-fry directly in the wok.

**Jarred black bean sauce:** Works in a pinch but lacks the depth of real fermented beans.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store for up to 1 day. Clams toughen when reheated.

**Reheating:** Warm gently in a covered pan. Do not boil.

**Freezing:** Not recommended.
