# Beef Brisket Soup (清湯牛腩)

*A clear, fragrant Cantonese broth simmered low and slow with tender beef brisket, daikon radish, and a warm spice packet*

---

## Headnote

This is the soup that Cantonese families make when the weather turns cold, when someone is feeling run down, or when the occasion simply calls for something deeply nourishing and unhurried. The finished bowl is deceptively simple in appearance: a clear, golden broth, chunks of fork-tender brisket, and translucent wedges of daikon radish. But that simplicity is the product of time and technique.

The clarity of the broth is the first thing that sets this soup apart. Cantonese clear soups are meant to be exactly that: clear, not cloudy. You achieve this by parboiling the brisket first, discarding that initial murky water, and starting the real simmer with fresh water and clean meat. The parboil draws out blood, impurities, and loose proteins that would otherwise cloud the broth. What remains is a liquid that cooks down into a clean, deeply beefy broth with a golden hue.

The spice packet is traditional and restrained. Star anise provides a warm, licorice-like fragrance. A few Sichuan peppercorns add a subtle tingle without making the soup spicy. Dried mandarin peel (chen pi) contributes a citrus note that lightens the richness of the beef. And dried scallops, if you can find them, dissolve slowly into the broth, adding an oceanic depth of flavor that is hard to pinpoint but impossible to miss.

Daikon radish goes in during the last 30 to 45 minutes. Cooked too long, it disintegrates. Cooked just right, it becomes translucent and tender, sweet from the broth it has absorbed and silky on the tongue. The brisket itself needs 1.5 to 2 hours of gentle simmering, during which the collagen in the connective tissue melts into gelatin, turning tough meat into something yielding and rich.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### Main
- 700 g beef brisket, cut into 4 cm chunks
- 400 g daikon radish, peeled and cut into 3 cm chunks
- 2 L water (for simmering)
- 3 slices fresh ginger, about 3 mm thick
- 2 scallions, cut into 5 cm lengths
- 10 ml Shaoxing wine
- Salt to taste

### Spice Packet (tied in cheesecloth or placed in a spice bag)
- 2 whole star anise
- 5 g Sichuan peppercorns
- 1 piece dried mandarin peel (chen pi), about 3 cm square, soaked and softened
- 3 dried scallops (optional but recommended)
- 1 small cinnamon stick (about 5 cm)
- 2 bay leaves

### Dipping Sauce
- 30 ml light soy sauce
- 10 ml chili oil
- 5 g fresh ginger, finely minced
- 5 g scallion, finely minced

### Garnish
- Fresh cilantro leaves
- Sliced scallion

---

## Method

1. Place the brisket chunks in a large pot and cover with cold water. Bring to a boil over high heat. Boil for 3 to 4 minutes, during which a large amount of scum and foam will rise to the surface. Drain the brisket, discard the water, and rinse each piece under running water to remove any clinging impurities. Rinse out the pot as well.

2. Return the cleaned brisket to the pot. Add 2 L of fresh water, the ginger slices, scallion lengths, and Shaoxing wine.

3. Place the star anise, Sichuan peppercorns, dried mandarin peel, dried scallops, cinnamon stick, and bay leaves in a cheesecloth pouch or spice bag. Tie it closed and add it to the pot. The spice bag makes removal easy and prevents loose spices from muddying the broth.

4. Bring to a boil over high heat. As soon as it boils, reduce the heat to a gentle simmer. You want small, lazy bubbles breaking the surface, not a rolling boil. A hard boil will emulsify the fat into the broth and make it cloudy.

5. Simmer partially covered for 1.5 to 2 hours. Check occasionally and skim any fat or foam that rises to the surface. The brisket is ready when it is tender enough to pierce easily with a chopstick but still holds its shape.

6. About 30 to 45 minutes before the brisket is done, add the daikon chunks. They should cook until translucent and easily pierced but not falling apart.

7. Remove the spice bag. Taste the broth and season with salt. The broth should be clean, beefy, and fragrant with a subtle warmth from the spices.

8. While the soup finishes, make the dipping sauce by combining the soy sauce, chili oil, minced ginger, and minced scallion in a small dish.

9. Ladle the soup into individual bowls, distributing brisket and daikon evenly. Garnish with cilantro and sliced scallion.

10. Serve with the chili-soy dipping sauce on the side. Diners can dip the brisket pieces into the sauce or stir some directly into their broth.

## Why This Works

Parboiling the brisket before simmering is the single most important step for achieving a clear broth. The initial boil draws out blood proteins and impurities that coagulate in the hot water. Discarding this water and starting fresh means the final broth is built from clean meat and clean water, producing a liquid that is golden and translucent rather than murky and gray.

Maintaining a gentle simmer rather than a hard boil is equally important. A rolling boil creates turbulence that emulsifies rendered fat into the broth in tiny droplets, turning it opaque and greasy. A gentle simmer keeps the fat in larger globules that float to the surface where they can be skimmed off.

Beef brisket is ideal for this preparation because it is rich in connective tissue (collagen) that breaks down into gelatin during the long simmer. This gelatin gives the broth a silky, full-bodied mouthfeel that water-thin broths lack. The meat itself becomes tender as the collagen dissolves.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Beef brisket** is a collagen-rich cut that provides protein, iron, zinc, and B vitamins. The long cooking time breaks down the collagen into gelatin, which may support joint health and gut lining integrity. Bone broth advocates cite these collagen-derived benefits, though the evidence is still developing.

**Daikon radish** is low in calories and high in vitamin C, potassium, and digestive enzymes. In traditional Chinese medicine, daikon is considered cooling and is used to aid digestion and reduce phlegm.

**Dried mandarin peel (chen pi)** contains limonene and other terpenes that have been studied for their anti-inflammatory and digestive properties. Aged chen pi (over 10 years old) is prized both culinarily and medicinally.

**Dried scallops** are a concentrated source of umami and provide taurine, an amino acid that supports cardiovascular function. Even a few small scallops dissolving into the broth add noticeable depth.

## Substitutions and Variations

- **Beef cut:** Beef shank or beef tendon can be used alongside or in place of brisket. Tendon requires 2 to 3 hours of simmering but becomes wonderfully gelatinous.
- **Daikon:** White turnip can substitute, though it has a stronger, more peppery flavor. Carrots work but change the character of the soup considerably.
- **Dried scallops:** If unavailable, the soup is still excellent without them. Do not substitute fresh scallops; they serve a different purpose.
- **Curry version:** Cantonese curry beef brisket (咖喱牛腩) uses the same base technique but adds curry powder, coconut milk, and potatoes. It is an entirely different dish but equally beloved.
- **Noodle soup:** Serve the brisket and broth over cooked rice noodles or thick wheat noodles for a complete one-bowl meal.

---

## Serving Suggestions

This is a centerpiece soup meant to anchor a meal. Serve it with steamed rice and two or three side dishes, such as a stir-fried green vegetable, steamed fish, or braised tofu. The chili-soy dipping sauce is essential and should be set out for each person. For a simpler meal, ladle the soup over rice noodles in the bowl and serve as a noodle soup with pickled mustard greens on the side.

---

## Storage and Reheating

This soup improves with time and is an excellent make-ahead dish. Refrigerate in an airtight container for up to 4 days. The fat will solidify on the surface when cold, making it easy to lift off and discard if desired. Reheat gently on the stovetop over medium heat. Do not boil vigorously, as this can make the daikon fall apart and cloud the broth. Freezes well for up to 3 months. The daikon may become slightly softer after freezing but the broth and brisket hold up beautifully.
