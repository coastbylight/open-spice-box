# Chinese Stuffed Peppers (釀辣椒)

*Bell pepper wedges packed with a bouncy shrimp and fish paste, pan-fried crisp and topped with savory black bean sauce*

---

## Headnote

If you have made the stuffed eggplant from this collection, you already know the filling. The Cantonese "Three Treasures" use the same bouncy shrimp and fish paste across three different vehicles: eggplant, peppers, and tofu. What changes is the wrapper and the sauce. For peppers, the traditional pairing is black bean sauce, and the combination is one of those pairings that just makes sense. The sweet crunch of the bell pepper, the springy, savory filling, and the salty, fermented depth of the black beans create a bite that hits several registers at once.

Stuffed peppers show up on dim sum carts across Hong Kong and Guangdong, but they are just as common as a home-cooked side dish. The technique is forgiving and fast. You cut bell peppers into wide wedges, dust the interiors with cornstarch so the filling sticks, then pack in the paste and pan-fry filling-side down until golden. The whole process from cutting board to plate takes about ten minutes of actual cooking.

The one thing that separates a good version from a mediocre one is the filling. You need to work it properly with salt until it develops that characteristic bounce. Cantonese cooks call this texture "tang," and it comes from extracting myosin proteins through vigorous stirring. Shortcut the mixing and the filling will be mushy rather than springy.

Green bell peppers are traditional and their slight bitterness works beautifully with the fermented black beans, but red or yellow peppers are sweeter and more visually striking if you prefer.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### Filling
- 150 g shrimp, peeled and deveined
- 100 g white fish fillet (cod, sole, or basa), boneless and skinless
- 10 g cornstarch
- 1 egg white
- 3 g salt
- 2 g white pepper
- 5 ml sesame oil
- 15 g scallion, finely minced
- 5 g fresh ginger, finely minced

### Peppers
- 3 large bell peppers (green, red, or a mix), about 450 g total
- 15 g cornstarch (for dusting)
- 30 ml vegetable oil (for pan-frying)

### Black Bean Sauce
- 20 g fermented black beans (dou chi), rinsed and roughly chopped
- 3 cloves garlic, minced
- 5 g fresh ginger, minced
- 15 ml light soy sauce
- 5 ml dark soy sauce
- 5 g sugar
- 5 ml Shaoxing wine
- 5 g cornstarch mixed with 30 ml water
- 80 ml chicken broth or water

---

## Method

1. Pat the shrimp and fish dry. Mince finely with a cleaver, or pulse in a food processor until you have a coarse paste. Transfer to a bowl.

2. Add the salt and stir vigorously in one direction for about 2 minutes until the paste becomes sticky and pulls together. Add the cornstarch, egg white, white pepper, sesame oil, scallion, and ginger. Continue stirring in the same direction until springy. Refrigerate for 15 minutes.

3. Cut each bell pepper in half lengthwise. Remove the seeds and white ribs. Cut each half into 3 wide wedges, giving you about 18 pieces total. Each wedge should be large enough to hold a generous mound of filling.

4. Dust the inside of each pepper wedge with a thin, even layer of cornstarch. Tap off any excess. This step is essential for keeping the filling attached to the pepper.

5. Mound about 15 g of filling onto the interior of each pepper wedge, pressing it firmly into the cavity and shaping it into a smooth dome that rises slightly above the rim.

6. Heat the vegetable oil in a large skillet or wok over medium-high heat. Place the stuffed peppers filling-side down in the pan. Cook without moving for 2 to 3 minutes until the filling turns deep golden and forms a firm crust.

7. Flip the peppers carefully and cook the skin side for 1 to 2 minutes. Add 30 ml of water, cover, and reduce heat to medium. Steam for 2 minutes to ensure the filling is cooked through and the pepper softens slightly while retaining some crunch. Remove to a serving plate.

8. In the same pan, add a splash of oil if needed. Sauté the chopped black beans, garlic, and ginger over medium heat until fragrant, about 30 seconds.

9. Add the soy sauces, sugar, Shaoxing wine, and broth. Bring to a simmer. Stir in the cornstarch slurry and cook until the sauce thickens to a glossy consistency, about 30 seconds.

10. Pour the black bean sauce over the stuffed peppers. Serve immediately.

---

## Why This Works

Cornstarch dusted on the interior of the pepper creates a dry, porous layer that absorbs moisture from the filling and forms a starchy bond between the two surfaces. Without it, the smooth, waxy interior of the pepper gives the filling nothing to grip, and it separates during cooking.

Frying the filling side first is critical. The direct contact with hot oil firms the exposed surface of the paste quickly, creating a crust that holds the filling in place before the pepper softens and releases steam. If you start pepper-side down, the filling stays raw and exposed, and the moisture from the cooking pepper loosens the bond.

Fermented black beans work so well with bell peppers because the salt and umami of the beans offset the pepper's sweetness. The brief sauté in oil before adding liquid blooms the beans' flavor and removes their raw, overly pungent edge.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Bell peppers** are exceptionally high in vitamin C. A single green bell pepper contains more vitamin C than an orange. Red and yellow peppers have even higher levels, as the vitamin C content increases as the pepper ripens.

**Fermented black beans** are one of the oldest fermented soy products in China, predating soy sauce. They contain naturally occurring glutamate, giving them a concentrated umami flavor. As a fermented food, they may support gut microbiome diversity.

**White fish** used in the paste provides a neutral-flavored base that lets the shrimp flavor come through while contributing proteins that help bind the filling.

## Substitutions and Variations

- **Peppers:** Green peppers are traditional and their mild bitterness pairs best with black bean sauce. Red, yellow, or orange peppers will be sweeter. Long hot peppers or mild Italian frying peppers can be halved lengthwise and stuffed for a different presentation.
- **Filling swap:** Ground pork (250 g) seasoned with soy sauce, sesame oil, and minced water chestnuts makes a classic alternative filling.
- **Black bean sauce shortcut:** Use 30 ml of prepared black bean sauce (like Lee Kum Kee) mixed with 60 ml broth. It will not have the same depth, but it saves time.
- **Sauce variation:** Swap the black bean sauce for the yuxiang garlic sauce from the stuffed eggplant recipe for a different flavor profile.
- **Baked version:** Arrange on an oiled baking sheet, filling-side up, and bake at 200 C for 15 minutes. The filling will not develop the same golden crust.

## Serving Suggestions

Serve as a side dish alongside steamed rice and one or two other dishes. For the full "Three Treasures" experience, make stuffed eggplant and stuffed tofu alongside and arrange all three on a large oval platter, with black bean sauce pooled underneath. This trio makes a complete meal with rice and is a traditional Cantonese home-style spread. The stuffed peppers also work well as part of a dim sum spread alongside steamed spare ribs, turnip cakes, and har gow.

---

## Storage and Reheating

Refrigerate leftover stuffed peppers in an airtight container for up to 2 days. Reheat in a covered skillet over medium heat with a splash of water for 3 to 4 minutes, or microwave on medium power for 1 to 2 minutes. The peppers retain their texture better than the eggplant version. The black bean sauce can be stored separately and rewarmed. Uncooked stuffed peppers can be prepared ahead, covered, and refrigerated for up to 6 hours before frying. Not recommended for freezing.
