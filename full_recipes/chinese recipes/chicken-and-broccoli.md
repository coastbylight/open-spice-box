# Chicken and Broccoli in White Sauce (白汁鸡花椰)

*Velveted chicken breast and blanched broccoli in a light, glossy white sauce of chicken stock, garlic, ginger, and sesame oil*

---

## Headnote

There are two versions of chicken and broccoli at every Chinese takeout spot: the brown sauce version with soy sauce, and the white sauce version with chicken stock. The brown sauce gets all the attention, but the white sauce is the more interesting dish. It is lighter, cleaner, and lets the chicken and broccoli actually taste like themselves rather than disappearing into a soy-based gravy.

The technique is pure restaurant Chinese cooking, applied at home. Both the chicken and the broccoli are blanched in boiling water before they go near the wok. The chicken gets a cornstarch-and-oyster-sauce marinade first, which creates the "velvet" that makes Chinese restaurant chicken impossibly silky. Thirty seconds in boiling water sets the coating and cooks the chicken about 80% through. The broccoli blanches for one to two minutes depending on how crunchy you like it. Both come out, the wok gets cleaned, and the sauce builds from scratch: garlic and ginger in oil, rice wine around the wok edge, chicken stock with sesame oil and white pepper, thickened with a cornstarch slurry until it clings to a spoon. The chicken and broccoli go back in for a final 30-second toss.

The whole thing is done in fifteen minutes of cooking time, and the result tastes like something that costs twelve dollars in a white paper container. The white sauce is subtle but not bland. The garlic and ginger are there. The sesame oil rounds out every bite. The white pepper adds a gentle heat that you feel in the back of your throat. It is the lighter, more refined sibling of the brown sauce version, and it deserves its place on the table.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the chicken marinade
- 340 g boneless skinless chicken breast (12 oz), thinly sliced
- 2 tbsp water
- 1 tbsp oyster sauce
- 2 tsp cornstarch
- 1 tsp vegetable oil

### For the sauce
- 240 ml low-sodium chicken stock (1 cup), warmed
- 1/4 tsp sugar
- 1 tsp salt, or to taste
- 1/2 tsp sesame oil
- 1/8 tsp ground white pepper

### For the stir-fry
- 4 cups broccoli florets (about 280 g)
- 2 tsp vegetable oil
- 1/4 tsp fresh ginger, minced
- 2 tsp garlic, minced
- 1 tbsp rice wine or Shaoxing wine
- 2 tbsp cornstarch mixed with 2 tbsp water (slurry)

---

## Method

1. Marinate the chicken. Combine the sliced chicken with the water, oyster sauce, cornstarch, and oil. Massage until absorbed. Set aside for 30 minutes.

2. Mix the sauce. Combine the chicken stock, sugar, salt, sesame oil, and white pepper. Set aside.

3. Blanch the broccoli. Bring 6 cups of water to a boil in the wok. Blanch the broccoli for 1 to 2 minutes depending on your crunch preference. Remove and drain.

4. Blanch the chicken. Let the water return to a boil. Add the marinated chicken and stir to separate the pieces. Cook just until the chicken turns opaque, about 30 to 45 seconds. Remove with a strainer. Discard the water and wash the wok.

5. Build the sauce. Heat the wok over medium-high heat until dry. Add the vegetable oil, ginger, and garlic. Cook for 20 seconds. Pour the rice wine around the edge of the wok. Add the chicken stock mixture. Stir and bring to a simmer.

6. Thicken. Pour about three-quarters of the cornstarch slurry into the sauce, stirring constantly. Check the thickness. Add the rest if needed, or add more stock if too thick. The sauce should coat the back of a spoon.

7. Finish. Add the chicken and broccoli back to the wok. Toss for 30 seconds until coated. Serve immediately with steamed rice.

---

## Why This Works

Blanching the chicken in water rather than stir-frying it in oil is the Chinese restaurant technique called water-velveting. The cornstarch marinade creates a protective coating that sets in the boiling water, sealing in moisture and producing a silky texture. The chicken finishes cooking in the sauce, which means it never gets tough or rubbery.

The white sauce is deliberately simple: chicken stock, sesame oil, white pepper, and a little sugar. There is no soy sauce, which is what keeps it pale and lets the natural flavors come through. The cornstarch slurry transforms the thin stock into a glossy sauce that clings to the chicken and broccoli rather than pooling on the plate.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Broccoli:** High in vitamin C, vitamin K, and sulforaphane. Brief blanching preserves more nutrients than long stir-frying.

**White pepper:** Sharper and more aromatic than black pepper, with a heat that registers in the nose. Traditional in Cantonese white sauces because it dissolves invisibly.

---

## Substitutions and Variations

**Brown sauce version:** Replace the chicken stock with 1 cup water, add 1 1/2 tbsp soy sauce, 1/2 tsp dark soy sauce, and 1 tbsp oyster sauce to the sauce mix. Skip the salt.

**Chicken thighs:** More flavorful and harder to overcook than breast. Slice thin and marinate the same way.

**Snow peas or green beans:** Can replace or supplement the broccoli.

---

## Serving Suggestions

Serve over steamed white rice. Pairs well with [Lo Mein](/recipes/lo-mein) or [Egg Drop Soup](/recipes/egg-drop-soup) for a complete Chinese takeout spread at home.

---

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store for up to 2 days. Reheat gently in a covered pan. The sauce may need a splash of stock to thin.

**Freezing:** Not recommended. The blanched broccoli becomes waterlogged.
