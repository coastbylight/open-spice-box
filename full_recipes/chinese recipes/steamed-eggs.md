# Chinese Steamed Eggs (蒸蛋)

*Silky, jiggly egg custard steamed to a glass-smooth finish, the ultimate Chinese comfort food*

---

## Headnote

If you grew up in a Chinese household, you almost certainly ate this before you could hold chopsticks properly. Steamed eggs, zheng dan, is the dish parents make when children are too young, too sick, or too tired for anything else. It is soft enough to eat with a spoon, gentle enough for the most delicate stomach, and satisfying in a way that is difficult to explain to anyone who has not had a really good version.

The texture you are aiming for sits somewhere between a savory panna cotta and a barely set flan. When you tap the edge of the bowl, the custard should jiggle like gelatin, trembling all the way to the center. The surface should be smooth as glass, without a single pockmark or bubble crater. Achieving this requires understanding one ratio and one principle. The ratio is one part egg to two parts warm water by volume, roughly half a cup of water per egg. The principle is that gentle heat produces smooth custard while aggressive heat produces a cratered, honeycomb texture full of air pockets.

The water must be warm, not hot and not cold. Water between 35 and 45 degrees Celsius dissolves the salt and bouillon evenly and helps the eggs set into a uniform texture. Cold water takes longer to come to temperature in the steamer, creating uneven cooking. Boiling water scrambles the eggs on contact. Straining the beaten egg mixture through a fine sieve removes any chalazae and stray bubbles, and covering the dish with plastic wrap or a plate prevents condensation from dripping onto the surface and pitting the custard. These small steps are the difference between a rough, porous result and the impossibly smooth version you remember from childhood.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the custard
- 4 large eggs
- 2 cups warm water (35 to 45°C)
- 1/2 tsp salt
- 1/2 tsp chicken bouillon powder
- pinch of ground white pepper
- 1 tbsp neutral oil (vegetable or canola)

### For finishing
- 1 tsp light soy sauce
- 1/2 tsp sesame oil
- 1 scallion, thinly sliced

---

## Method

1. Crack the eggs into a mixing bowl. Beat gently with chopsticks or a fork until the yolks and whites are just combined. You want a uniform mixture without creating foam. Vigorous whisking introduces air bubbles that will mar the surface of the custard.

2. Measure 2 cups of warm water, between 35 and 45 degrees Celsius. It should feel comfortably warm to the touch but not hot. Stir the salt, chicken bouillon powder, and white pepper into the warm water until dissolved.

3. Pour the seasoned water into the beaten eggs in a slow, steady stream while stirring gently in one direction. Add the tablespoon of oil and stir to combine.

4. Set a fine-mesh sieve over a shallow, heatproof dish or wide bowl. Pour the egg mixture through the sieve, pressing gently on any solids. Discard what remains in the sieve. This step removes the chalazae and any bubbles, and is essential for a smooth finish.

5. Use a spoon to skim off any remaining small bubbles from the surface of the strained mixture. Take your time with this. Every bubble you remove now is one less crater in the finished custard.

6. Cover the dish tightly with plastic wrap or a flat plate. This prevents condensation from dripping onto the custard surface during steaming.

7. Fill a wok or pot with enough water to come about 2 inches up the sides. Set a steamer rack inside and bring the water to a rolling boil over high heat.

8. Once the water is boiling vigorously, place the covered dish on the steamer rack. Immediately reduce the heat to maintain a gentle simmer. The water should be barely bubbling, not churning. Close the lid.

9. Steam for 8 minutes without lifting the lid. The custard is done when it jiggles like gelatin when you gently shake the dish. The center should wobble but not slosh. If the center is still liquid, steam for another 1 to 2 minutes.

10. Carefully remove the dish from the steamer. Remove the plastic wrap or plate cover. Drizzle the light soy sauce and sesame oil over the surface. Scatter the sliced scallions on top. Serve immediately with rice.

## Why This Works

The 1:2 ratio of egg to warm water creates a custard that is tender enough to eat with a spoon but firm enough to hold its shape. Too little water produces something dense and rubbery. Too much water and the custard will not set properly, remaining liquid in the center.

Straining removes the chalazae, those ropy white strands attached to the yolk, as well as any air bubbles introduced during mixing. Both would create imperfections in the finished texture.

The shift from high heat to a simmer is critical. High heat throughout would cause the proteins in the egg to contract rapidly, squeezing out water and creating a honeycomb of air pockets. Gentle heat allows the proteins to set slowly and evenly, trapping the water within the gel matrix and producing the smooth, silky texture that defines a properly made steamed egg.

Covering the dish serves a dual purpose. It prevents condensation droplets from falling onto the custard surface, which would leave pockmarks, and it creates a sealed environment that promotes even cooking from all sides.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Eggs** provide complete protein and are among the few natural food sources of vitamin D. The gentle steaming method preserves nutrients that might be degraded by high-heat cooking methods like frying.

**Chicken bouillon** adds umami depth without requiring homemade stock. If you prefer a cleaner ingredient list, substitute with a pinch of mushroom powder or simply increase the salt slightly.

## Substitutions and Variations

- **Dashi version:** Replace the warm water with dashi stock and omit the chicken bouillon. This produces chawanmushi, the Japanese cousin of this dish, which is traditionally steamed in individual cups.
- **Shrimp or clam:** Place a few raw shrimp or fresh clams in the bottom of the dish before pouring in the egg mixture. They will cook through during steaming and add a seafood sweetness.
- **Richer custard:** Replace half the water with unsweetened soy milk for a creamier, slightly denser custard.
- **Meat floss topping:** Instead of scallions, top the finished custard with a generous pile of pork floss (rou song) and a drizzle of soy sauce. This is a common Taiwanese variation.

## Serving Suggestions

Steamed eggs are a side dish, meant to accompany rice and other dishes rather than stand alone. Serve alongside [Tomato Egg Stir-Fry](/recipes/tomato-egg-stir-fry) and a simple vegetable for a light home-style meal, or as the gentle counterpoint to something bold like [Mapo Tofu](/recipes/mapo-tofu) or [Kung Pao Chicken](/recipes/gong-bao-ji-ding). For young children, it is often served on its own over a small bowl of congee.

## Storage and Reheating

Steamed eggs are best eaten immediately. The custard will continue to firm up as it cools and will weep liquid if stored. Leftovers can be refrigerated in an airtight container for up to 1 day. Reheat gently by steaming for 2 to 3 minutes, though the texture will not be as smooth as when freshly made. Not recommended for freezing.
