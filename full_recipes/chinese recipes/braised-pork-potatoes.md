# Braised Pork with Potatoes (薯仔炆豬肉)

*Tender pork belly and golden potatoes braised in a savory sauce of ground bean paste, oyster sauce, and soy*

---

## Headnote

This is the kind of dish that Cantonese families eat on a Tuesday night and never think to put in a cookbook. Pork belly. Potatoes. A handful of pantry sauces. Twenty minutes of simmering. It is not fancy, it is not photogenic in the way a roast duck is, and nobody is going to order it at a banquet. But it is deeply, unreasonably satisfying, the kind of food that makes you eat three bowls of rice without realizing it.

The sauce comes together from three ingredients that do most of the heavy lifting: ground bean sauce (mian chi jiang), which is a thick paste of fermented soybeans with a salty, savory depth; oyster sauce, which adds sweetness and body; and light soy sauce for seasoning. Together, they create a rich, glossy coating that clings to the pork and potatoes as the braising liquid reduces.

The technique is Cantonese home cooking at its most straightforward. You brown the pork belly to render some fat and develop flavor on the surface. You brown the potatoes in that rendered fat, giving them golden edges that hold up during braising. Then everything goes in together with the sauce and a bit of water, and you walk away for twenty minutes. The potatoes absorb the braising liquid and become creamy inside while their browned edges keep them from falling apart entirely.

What makes this dish quietly brilliant is that it actually improves with time. The day after cooking, the flavors have melded and the sauce has thickened around the pork and potatoes. Leftovers heated up in a pan with a splash of water are arguably better than the fresh version.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### Main
- 400 g pork belly, skin on or off, cut into 2 cm cubes
- 350 g potatoes (Yukon Gold or similar waxy variety), peeled and cut into 3 cm chunks
- 25 g ground bean sauce (mian chi jiang / 麵豉醬)
- 15 ml oyster sauce
- 15 ml light soy sauce
- 5 ml dark soy sauce
- 10 g sugar
- 5 ml Shaoxing wine
- 3 cloves garlic, smashed
- 3 slices fresh ginger
- 250 ml water
- 15 ml vegetable oil
- 2 scallions, sliced (for garnish)
- White pepper to taste

---

## Method

1. Cut the pork belly into roughly 2 cm cubes. If the skin is on, keep it. The skin will become tender and slightly gelatinous during braising. Pat the pieces dry.

2. Peel the potatoes and cut them into chunks slightly larger than the pork, about 3 cm. They will shrink a bit during cooking. Keep them in water until ready to use, then drain and pat dry.

3. Heat the vegetable oil in a wok or heavy-bottomed pot over medium-high heat. Add the pork belly pieces in a single layer. Let them sear without stirring for 2 minutes until golden on the bottom, then turn and brown on a second side. You want color and rendered fat, not a full cook. Remove the pork and set aside.

4. In the rendered pork fat, add the potato chunks. Sear them on one or two sides until golden, about 3 minutes total. The browning gives them structure to survive the braise. Remove and set aside with the pork.

5. Pour off all but a thin film of fat. Add the garlic and ginger to the pan and stir for 20 seconds until fragrant.

6. Add the ground bean sauce to the pan and stir-fry it in the oil for 30 seconds. This blooms the flavor of the fermented beans and removes any raw taste.

7. Return the pork and potatoes to the pan. Add the oyster sauce, light soy sauce, dark soy sauce, sugar, Shaoxing wine, and white pepper. Stir to coat everything evenly.

8. Add the water. It should come about two-thirds of the way up the ingredients. Bring to a boil, then reduce heat to a steady simmer. Cover and cook for 18 to 20 minutes, stirring once halfway through, until the potatoes are tender and the pork is soft.

9. Remove the lid and check the sauce consistency. If it is too thin, raise the heat and simmer uncovered for 2 to 3 minutes to reduce. The sauce should be glossy and thick enough to coat the back of a spoon.

10. Transfer to a serving bowl. Garnish with sliced scallions. Serve immediately with steamed rice.

## Why This Works

Browning the pork belly first renders some of its fat and creates Maillard reaction products on the surface that add savory depth to the braising liquid. Using that rendered fat to brown the potatoes gives them flavor and creates a starchy crust that slows their breakdown during the braise.

Ground bean sauce is a paste of whole fermented soybeans, and it provides glutamate (umami) along with a rich, almost meaty savoriness. Combined with oyster sauce, which adds its own layer of glutamate from oyster extracts, the sauce achieves a complex, layered savoriness from just a few spoonfuls of condiment.

The relatively short braising time of 20 minutes is enough because the pork belly is cut small. The high surface-area-to-volume ratio means each piece absorbs sauce and tenderizes quickly, unlike a whole slab that needs hours.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Pork belly** is higher in fat than most cuts, but the braising process renders some of that fat into the cooking liquid. The collagen in pork belly breaks down during cooking, giving the sauce body and the meat a tender, yielding texture. Pork is a good source of thiamine (vitamin B1), which supports energy metabolism.

**Potatoes** provide potassium, vitamin C, and resistant starch (especially when cooled and reheated). The waxy varieties recommended here hold their shape better during braising than starchy russets.

**Ground bean sauce** is a traditionally fermented condiment. Like other fermented soy products, it contains isoflavones and bioactive peptides produced during fermentation.

## Substitutions and Variations

- **Pork cut:** Boneless pork shoulder or country-style ribs work well. They are leaner than belly but have enough fat and connective tissue to braise successfully. Increase cooking time by 5 to 10 minutes.
- **Ground bean sauce:** If you cannot find it, substitute a combination of 15 g yellow miso paste and 10 ml hoisin sauce. The flavor will not be identical but follows the same sweet-salty-fermented profile.
- **Potatoes:** Taro is a traditional Cantonese swap and becomes wonderfully creamy when braised. Peel and cut it the same way. Daikon radish is another option.
- **Spicy version:** Add 1 to 2 dried red chilies when you fry the garlic and ginger, or stir in 5 ml of chili bean paste (doubanjiang) with the ground bean sauce.
- **Clay pot version:** For a more traditional presentation, transfer everything to a heated clay pot (砂鍋) after combining, and braise in the clay pot. Serve it directly at the table, still bubbling.

---

## Serving Suggestions

This is a one-dish dinner with rice. The rich, savory sauce is meant to flavor plain steamed jasmine rice, so you want plenty of it. Add a simple stir-fried green vegetable or a bowl of blanched gai lan with oyster sauce to round out the meal. For a heartier spread, serve alongside steamed spare ribs or a light soup like egg drop or wintermelon.

---

## Storage and Reheating

This dish is a strong candidate for meal prep. Refrigerate in an airtight container for up to 4 days. The flavors develop and improve overnight as the sauce continues to absorb into the pork and potatoes. Reheat in a covered pan over medium heat with a splash of water, stirring occasionally, for about 5 minutes. The potatoes may break down slightly upon reheating, thickening the sauce further. Freezes well for up to 2 months. Thaw overnight in the refrigerator before reheating.
