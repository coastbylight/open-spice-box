# Singapore Noodles (星洲炒米)

*Curry-spiced rice vermicelli stir-fried with shrimp, char siu, egg ribbons, and bell pepper, the Cantonese takeout classic done right*

---

## Headnote

Singapore noodles have nothing to do with Singapore. You will not find them on menus there. The dish is a creation of Cantonese and Hong Kong restaurant kitchens, named for a country rather than born in one, the way General Tso's chicken has no connection to the actual General Tso. What it does have is curry powder, which is the ingredient that makes it unlike anything else on a Chinese takeout menu.

The curry powder goes into a sauce with soy sauce, Shaoxing wine, and white pepper, and that sauce gets tossed with thin rice vermicelli noodles, shrimp, sliced char siu pork, and egg that has been cooked into a thin omelet, rolled up, and sliced into ribbons. The result is a tangle of golden noodles that are savory and aromatic and faintly spicy, with enough going on in every bite that you never get bored.

The technique requires a hot wok and some confidence. Rice vermicelli is delicate and can clump or stick if you hesitate, so everything needs to be prepped and ready before you start cooking. The proteins cook first and come out. The aromatics go in. The noodles and sauce follow. Then everything gets tossed back together in the final minute. The whole stir-fry takes about ten minutes, which means the prep takes longer than the cooking. That is normal for wok cooking. The work is in the chopping, not the flame.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the sauce
- 2 tbsp soy sauce (light or all-purpose, not dark)
- 2 tbsp Shaoxing wine
- 2 1/2 tsp curry powder
- 1/2 tsp sugar
- 1/2 tsp ground white pepper

### For the stir-fry
- 100 g dried rice vermicelli noodles
- 2 tbsp peanut oil, divided
- 8 to 10 medium raw shrimp, shelled and deveined
- 2 large eggs, beaten
- 1/2 medium onion, thinly sliced
- 4 cloves garlic, minced
- 1 tsp fresh ginger, grated
- 225 g Chinese barbecue pork (char siu), thinly sliced
- 1 red bell pepper, cut into thin strips
- 2 tsp thinly sliced hot green pepper (optional)

---

## Method

1. Prepare the sauce. Combine the soy sauce, Shaoxing wine, curry powder, sugar, and white pepper in a small bowl. Stir well and set aside.

2. Soak the noodles. Place the rice vermicelli in a large bowl and cover with boiling water. Soak according to package directions until softened but still slightly firm, usually 3 to 5 minutes. Drain well and set aside. Wet noodles will steam rather than fry, so shake off as much water as possible.

3. Cook the shrimp. Heat 1 tablespoon of oil in a wok over medium-high heat. Add the shrimp and cook until just pink and curled into a C shape, about 2 to 3 minutes. Remove and set aside.

4. Make the egg ribbons. In the same wok, pour in the beaten eggs and swirl to form a thin omelet. Once set on top, use a spatula to roll it up, slide it out, and slice the roll into thin ribbons. Set aside with the shrimp.

5. Stir-fry the aromatics. Return the wok to medium-high heat with the remaining 1 tablespoon of oil. Add the garlic, ginger, and onion. Cook for 2 minutes until the onion softens and the garlic is fragrant. Add the bell pepper and cook for 1 more minute.

6. Toss everything together. Add the drained noodles and the sauce to the wok. Toss vigorously to coat the noodles evenly with the curry sauce. Add the shrimp, egg ribbons, char siu, and green pepper if using. Toss for 1 to 2 minutes until everything is heated through and the sauce is evenly distributed. The noodles should be golden and fragrant.

7. Serve immediately. Pile onto plates and eat while hot.

## Why This Works

Curry powder is the signature of this dish and the ingredient that makes it work. The warm spices in curry powder (turmeric, coriander, cumin, fenugreek) marry surprisingly well with soy sauce and Shaoxing wine, creating a flavor profile that is neither purely Chinese nor purely South Asian but something distinctly its own. The turmeric in the curry powder gives the noodles their characteristic golden color.

Rice vermicelli behaves differently from wheat noodles in a wok. It absorbs sauce quickly and clings to other ingredients, which is why the sauce is concentrated rather than voluminous. Too much liquid and the noodles become soggy. The right amount coats each strand without pooling at the bottom.

Cooking the components separately and combining them at the end is the standard wok technique for dishes with multiple proteins and vegetables. Each ingredient gets the right amount of heat for its needs. Shrimp overcook in seconds. Eggs need a flat, undisturbed surface. Aromatics need oil and time. Combining everything too early means some things are overcooked and others are undercooked.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Curry powder:** The turmeric in curry powder provides curcumin, one of the most studied natural anti-inflammatory compounds. The combination of cumin, coriander, and fenugreek in most curry blends reflects spice combinations that Ayurvedic tradition considers digestive and warming. The fat in the peanut oil helps with curcumin absorption.

**Shrimp:** A lean protein source high in selenium, B12, and iodine. Shrimp also contains astaxanthin, the carotenoid that gives them their pink color when cooked, which has been studied for antioxidant properties.

**Rice vermicelli:** Made from rice flour and water, these noodles are naturally gluten-free. They are lighter and lower in calories than wheat noodles, which is part of why this dish feels satisfying without being heavy.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Char siu:** Substitute with diced cooked chicken, bacon, ham, or sliced Chinese sausage (lap cheong). For a vegetarian version, use extra vegetables and firm tofu.

**Shrimp:** Chicken breast sliced thin or extra-firm tofu cubed work well. Cook the chicken fully before removing from the wok.

**Shaoxing wine:** Dry sherry is the closest substitute. Cooking sake or mirin also work. For alcohol-free, use chicken broth.

**Noodles:** Wai Wai brand rice vermicelli is recommended for its texture and durability during tossing. Any thin rice noodle will work.

**Heat level:** Use hot curry powder for more spice, or add chili flakes. The green pepper is optional and can be adjusted to taste.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store in an airtight container for up to 2 days. The noodles will absorb sauce as they sit and may dry out slightly.

**Reheating:** Toss in a hot wok with a splash of water or soy sauce for 1 to 2 minutes. Microwave works but the noodles lose their texture. A hot pan is always better for stir-fried noodles.

**Freezing:** Not recommended. Rice vermicelli becomes brittle and breaks apart when frozen and thawed.
