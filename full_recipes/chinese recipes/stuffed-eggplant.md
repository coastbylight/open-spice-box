# Chinese Stuffed Eggplant (釀茄子)

*Tender Chinese eggplant filled with a bouncy shrimp and fish paste, pan-fried golden and finished with yuxiang garlic sauce*

---

## Headnote

Stuffed eggplant is one third of the Cantonese "Three Treasures" (san bao), a trio of stuffed vegetables that shows up on dim sum carts and takeaway menus across southern China. The other two are stuffed peppers and stuffed tofu, and all three share the same basic seafood paste filling. The idea is simple enough that you wonder why it works so well: a hinge-cut piece of eggplant, its halves spread open like a book, packed with a bouncy mixture of minced shrimp and white fish, then pan-fried until the filling sets and the eggplant turns soft and silky.

The filling is the heart of all three dishes. Getting it right means understanding how to develop the "bounce" (what the Cantonese call "tang") in a fish paste. You achieve this by mincing the shrimp and fish finely, then working the mixture with salt and a bit of cornstarch, mixing in one direction until the proteins bind and the paste becomes sticky and springy. When cooked, a properly made paste has a satisfying snap to it, not mushy but not rubbery either.

The eggplant itself matters. You want Chinese or Japanese long eggplant, which has thinner skin, fewer seeds, and a sweeter, less bitter flavor than the globe variety. The hinge cut keeps the two halves connected, creating a pocket for the filling while allowing even cooking on both sides.

The yuxiang (garlic) sauce that dresses the finished dish brings together soy sauce, vinegar, sugar, and plenty of garlic into a balanced, savory-sweet glaze. It is the same flavor profile found in many Sichuan and Cantonese preparations, and it complements the mild seafood filling beautifully.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### Filling
- 150 g shrimp, peeled and deveined
- 100 g white fish fillet (cod, sole, or basa), boneless and skinless
- 10 g cornstarch
- 1 egg white
- 3 g salt
- 2 g white pepper
- 5 ml sesame oil
- 15 g scallion, finely minced
- 5 g fresh ginger, finely minced

### Eggplant
- 4 Chinese or Japanese eggplants (about 500 g total)
- 15 g cornstarch (for dusting)
- 30 ml vegetable oil (for pan-frying)

### Yuxiang (Garlic) Sauce
- 4 cloves garlic, minced
- 15 ml light soy sauce
- 10 ml dark soy sauce
- 10 ml Chinkiang black vinegar
- 10 g sugar
- 5 ml Shaoxing wine
- 5 g cornstarch mixed with 30 ml water
- 60 ml chicken broth or water
- 5 ml chili bean paste (doubanjiang), optional

---

## Method

1. Pat the shrimp and fish dry. Roughly chop both, then mince finely with a cleaver, or pulse in a food processor until you have a coarse paste with some small pieces remaining. Transfer to a bowl.

2. Add the salt to the paste and stir vigorously in one direction for about 2 minutes. You will feel the mixture become sticky and cohesive. Add the cornstarch, egg white, white pepper, sesame oil, minced scallion, and ginger. Continue stirring in the same direction until the paste pulls away from the sides of the bowl and feels springy. Refrigerate for 15 minutes to firm up.

3. Cut each eggplant crosswise into 4 cm segments. Make a deep cut down the center of each segment, slicing about three-quarters of the way through but leaving the bottom connected, creating a hinge.

4. Dust the inside surfaces of each eggplant pocket with a thin layer of cornstarch. This helps the filling adhere to the eggplant during cooking.

5. Pack about 15 g of filling into each eggplant pocket, pressing firmly so it fills the cavity and mounds slightly above the surface. Smooth the exposed filling with a wet finger.

6. Heat the vegetable oil in a large skillet or wok over medium-high heat. Place the stuffed eggplant pieces filling-side down first. Cook for 2 to 3 minutes until the filling turns golden and sets. Flip carefully and cook the eggplant side for another 2 to 3 minutes.

7. Add 60 ml of water to the pan, reduce heat to medium, cover, and let the eggplant steam for 3 to 4 minutes until tender when pierced with a chopstick. Remove the eggplant to a serving plate.

8. In the same pan, add a splash of oil if needed and sauté the minced garlic and chili bean paste (if using) over medium heat until fragrant, about 30 seconds.

9. Add the soy sauces, vinegar, sugar, Shaoxing wine, and broth. Bring to a simmer. Stir in the cornstarch slurry and cook until the sauce thickens and turns glossy, about 30 seconds.

10. Pour the sauce over the stuffed eggplant on the plate. Garnish with sliced scallion and serve immediately.

## Why This Works

Stirring the fish and shrimp paste in one direction aligns the myosin proteins, creating a network that gives the cooked filling its characteristic bouncy texture. Adding salt early extracts these proteins from the muscle fibers, making the binding process more effective. The egg white adds moisture and helps set the paste during cooking.

Dusting the inside of the eggplant with cornstarch creates a dry, starchy surface that the wet filling can grip. Without it, the filling tends to slide out of the eggplant during cooking as moisture is released.

Pan-frying the filling side first sets and seals it before the eggplant has a chance to soften and release moisture. The brief covered steaming step that follows cooks the eggplant through without requiring so much oil that the dish becomes greasy.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Chinese eggplant** contains nasunin, an anthocyanin found in the skin that has antioxidant properties. The long, slender shape of Chinese eggplant means a higher skin-to-flesh ratio compared to globe varieties, and the thinner skin is more pleasant to eat.

**Shrimp** provide a concentrated source of the mineral selenium, which supports thyroid function and acts as an antioxidant. Combined with white fish, the filling is a high-protein, low-fat preparation.

**Chinkiang black vinegar** is a fermented rice vinegar with a malty, complex flavor. Fermented vinegars contain organic acids and polyphenols that may support digestive health.

## Substitutions and Variations

- **Eggplant variety:** If Chinese eggplant is unavailable, use Japanese eggplant. Globe eggplant can work if cut into thick rounds and scooped out to create a hollow, but the flavor and texture are not as good.
- **Filling variation:** For a pork-based filling, substitute 250 g ground pork for the fish and shrimp. Add 5 ml soy sauce and 5 g minced water chestnuts for crunch.
- **Vegetarian:** Fill with a mixture of firm tofu, minced shiitake mushrooms, and water chestnuts. Season with soy sauce and sesame oil.
- **Sauce swap:** Black bean sauce (see the stuffed peppers recipe) is the traditional alternative, especially when serving as part of the Three Treasures trio.
- **Baked version:** Arrange stuffed eggplant on an oiled baking sheet and bake at 200 C for 20 minutes, flipping halfway. Less hands-on, though you lose the golden crust on the filling.

---

## Serving Suggestions

Serve as a main course with steamed rice and a simple stir-fried leafy green. For a full Cantonese "Three Treasures" presentation, make the stuffed eggplant alongside stuffed peppers and stuffed tofu, arranging all three on one large platter with sauce spooned over the top. This combination is a classic dim sum spread and makes a visually striking centerpiece for a family-style meal.

---

## Storage and Reheating

Stuffed eggplant keeps in the refrigerator for up to 2 days in an airtight container. Reheat in a covered skillet with a splash of water over medium heat until warmed through, about 5 minutes. Alternatively, microwave on medium power for 2 minutes. The filling retains its texture well upon reheating. The sauce can be stored separately and rewarmed on the stove. Not recommended for freezing, as the eggplant becomes watery when thawed.
