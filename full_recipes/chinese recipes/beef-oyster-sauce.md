# Beef with Oyster Sauce (蠔油牛肉)

*Velveted beef stir-fried with snow peas, celery, and mushrooms in a glossy oyster sauce*

---

## Headnote

This is one of those Cantonese dishes that shows up on every takeaway menu in the world, and for good reason: it is fast, it is satisfying, and when done well, the beef is impossibly tender. The secret, as with so many Chinese stir-fries, is velveting. You toss the sliced beef with cornstarch, a pinch of baking soda, and a splash of soy sauce, then let it sit for 15 minutes. The cornstarch forms a thin coating that shields the meat from direct heat, while the baking soda raises the surface pH and prevents the proteins from seizing up and squeezing out moisture. The result is beef that stays juicy and almost silky even after hitting a screaming hot wok.

The cooking itself moves fast. You sear the beef hard in a very hot wok, cooking it only about 80 percent of the way through before pulling it out. The vegetables go in next, each one chosen for its crunch and ability to stand up to high heat: snow peas that stay snappy, celery that adds a clean bite, mushrooms that soak up sauce, and carrot coins for sweetness and color. Everything gets tossed together with the sauce for the final minute, during which the beef finishes cooking in the residual heat and the sauce reduces into a glossy glaze.

The sauce is built on oyster sauce, which provides the backbone of sweetness and umami. Light soy sauce adds salt, and a small amount of dark soy sauce at the very end gives the dish its characteristic deep brown color without making it too salty. It is a simple formula that works every time, and once you have the technique down, you can swap in whatever vegetables look good at the market.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### Beef
- 400 g beef (tri-tip, flank steak, or sirloin), sliced against the grain into 3 mm strips
- 10 g cornstarch
- 2 g baking soda
- 10 ml light soy sauce
- 5 ml Shaoxing wine
- 5 ml vegetable oil
- 2 g white pepper

### Vegetables
- 100 g snow peas, trimmed and strings removed
- 80 g celery, sliced on the diagonal into 5 mm pieces
- 80 g shiitake or button mushrooms, sliced
- 60 g carrot, sliced into thin coins or half-moons
- 3 cloves garlic, sliced
- 3 slices fresh ginger

### Sauce
- 30 ml oyster sauce
- 10 ml light soy sauce
- 5 ml dark soy sauce
- 5 g sugar
- 5 ml sesame oil
- 5 g cornstarch mixed with 30 ml water
- 30 ml water or chicken broth

### For Cooking
- 30 ml vegetable oil, divided

---

## Method

1. Slice the beef against the grain into thin strips, about 3 mm thick. Place in a bowl and add the cornstarch, baking soda, soy sauce, Shaoxing wine, vegetable oil, and white pepper. Mix well and let sit for at least 15 minutes at room temperature. The beef will darken slightly as the baking soda works.

2. Prepare all vegetables and have them arranged near the stove. Mix the sauce ingredients together in a small bowl, keeping the cornstarch slurry separate until ready to use. Stir-frying moves fast and there is no time to prep mid-cook.

3. Heat 15 ml of vegetable oil in a wok over the highest heat until it shimmers and just begins to smoke. Spread the beef strips in a single layer across the wok. Let them sear undisturbed for 45 seconds to develop a brown crust on the bottom.

4. Flip and stir-fry for another 30 seconds. The beef should be about 80 percent cooked, still slightly pink in the thickest parts. Remove immediately and set aside on a plate.

5. Add the remaining 15 ml of oil to the wok. When smoking, add the garlic and ginger. Stir for 10 seconds until fragrant.

6. Add the carrots and celery first, as they take the longest. Stir-fry for 1 minute over high heat.

7. Add the mushrooms and snow peas. Stir-fry for another 1 minute. The vegetables should be crisp-tender, with bright colors and some charred edges.

8. Return the beef and any accumulated juices to the wok. Pour in the sauce mixture. Toss everything together for 30 seconds.

9. Give the cornstarch slurry a stir (it settles quickly) and pour it into the wok. Stir continuously for 20 to 30 seconds as the sauce thickens and becomes glossy, coating everything evenly.

10. Add the dark soy sauce last, tossing quickly just to distribute the color. Transfer immediately to a serving plate. Serve with steamed rice.

## Why This Works

Velveting with cornstarch creates a thin barrier around each piece of beef that insulates the meat from the extreme heat of the wok. This coating slows moisture loss, keeping the interior juicy. The cornstarch also absorbs and holds onto the sauce, giving each piece a glossy, flavorful coating.

Baking soda raises the pH on the meat's surface, which inhibits the proteins from bonding tightly together. When proteins contract less, they squeeze out less moisture, resulting in a noticeably more tender texture. A small amount (about 2 g for 400 g of beef) is enough. Too much leaves an unpleasant soapy taste.

Cooking the beef to only 80 percent doneness before removing it from the wok accounts for carryover cooking. When the beef returns to the wok for the final toss with sauce and vegetables, it finishes cooking in about 30 seconds. If you cooked it fully the first time, it would be overcooked and tough by the time the dish is done.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Beef** is a concentrated source of heme iron, the most bioavailable form of dietary iron. It also provides zinc, selenium, and B12. Slicing against the grain shortens the muscle fibers, making each bite more tender regardless of the cooking method.

**Snow peas** are eaten pod and all, providing fiber, vitamin C, and vitamin K. They contribute a clean, sweet crunch that contrasts with the rich sauce.

**Oyster sauce** originated in Guangdong province in the 1880s, reportedly discovered by accident when a cook left oysters simmering too long. Modern versions are made from oyster extracts, sugar, and soy sauce, and they provide a concentrated source of umami.

## Substitutions and Variations

- **Beef cut:** Flank steak and tri-tip are ideal for their balance of flavor and tenderness when sliced thin. Sirloin or eye of round work too. Avoid very lean cuts without some marbling, as they dry out more easily.
- **Vegetables:** Broccoli, bell peppers, water chestnuts, baby corn, and bok choy are all common swaps. The dish is flexible as long as you keep a mix of textures.
- **Chicken or pork:** Slice chicken breast or pork loin the same way and velvet identically. Reduce the initial sear time slightly.
- **Without baking soda:** If you prefer to skip the baking soda, increase the cornstarch to 15 g and add an egg white. The beef will still be tender, just slightly less silky.
- **Spicy version:** Add 10 g of chili bean paste (doubanjiang) when you fry the garlic and ginger, or drizzle with chili oil at the end.

## Serving Suggestions

Serve over steamed jasmine rice, letting the sauce pool into the rice. This is a classic weeknight dinner on its own, or serve as one of two or three dishes in a Chinese family-style meal. It pairs particularly well with a light soup and a green vegetable dish. For a faster weeknight version, serve over instant ramen noodles instead of rice.

---

## Storage and Reheating

Refrigerate leftovers in an airtight container for up to 2 days. Reheat in a hot wok or skillet over high heat for 2 minutes, adding a splash of water to loosen the sauce. The beef will be slightly less tender upon reheating but still good. Microwaving works in a pinch at medium power for 1 to 2 minutes. The vegetables will lose some crunch. Not recommended for freezing, as the vegetables become watery and the sauce breaks down.
