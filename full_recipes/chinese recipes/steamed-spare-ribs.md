# Steamed Spare Ribs with Black Bean Sauce (豉汁蒸排骨)

*Bite-sized pork spare ribs steamed until tender with fermented black beans, garlic, ginger, and dried mandarin peel*

---

## Headnote

If you have ever been to dim sum in a Cantonese restaurant, you have probably seen these arrive on a cart in a small steamer basket. They come out glistening and fragrant, each little rib piece coated in a dark, savory glaze of fermented black beans and garlic. The pork is tender but still has some chew to it, the black beans are salty and deep, and the occasional piece of red chili adds a flash of heat. It is one of the simplest dishes in the entire dim sum canon, and one of the best.

The secret to good steamed ribs starts before any cooking happens. You need to ask your butcher to cut the spare ribs crosswise through the bone into strips about 3 cm wide, then you chop those strips into individual pieces between the bones. Most Chinese grocery stores sell ribs pre-cut this way, sometimes labeled "pork ribs for steaming." If you have a heavy cleaver, you can do this yourself at home.

After cutting, the ribs get rinsed several times with cold water and a bit of cornstarch. This step looks odd, but it has a purpose: the cornstarch draws out myoglobin and blood from the cut surfaces, resulting in ribs that look cleaner and lighter after steaming instead of turning gray and murky. Once rinsed until the water runs clear, the ribs are marinated briefly with black beans, garlic, ginger, a piece of dried mandarin peel, and a bit of soy sauce and sugar.

The steaming itself is straightforward, but one detail matters: arrange the ribs in a single layer with no pieces stacked on top of each other. Overlapping ribs steam unevenly, and the pieces on top will be tough while the ones on the bottom overcook.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### Main
- 500 g pork spare ribs, cut crosswise into 3 cm pieces between the bones
- 20 g fermented black beans (dou chi), rinsed and roughly chopped
- 4 cloves garlic, minced
- 10 g fresh ginger, minced
- 1 piece dried mandarin peel (chen pi), about 3 cm square, soaked in warm water for 10 minutes and finely diced
- 10 g cornstarch (for washing)
- 10 g cornstarch (for marinating)
- 15 ml light soy sauce
- 5 ml Shaoxing wine
- 5 g sugar
- 5 ml sesame oil
- 2 g white pepper
- 1 small red chili, sliced into rings (optional)
- Sliced scallion for garnish

---

## Method

1. Place the rib pieces in a large bowl and cover with cold water. Add 10 g of cornstarch and massage the ribs gently, squeezing and rubbing the pieces. The water will turn pink and cloudy. Drain, and repeat this process 2 to 3 more times until the water runs mostly clear. This removes surface blood and myoglobin for a cleaner-looking finished dish.

2. Drain the ribs thoroughly and pat dry with paper towels.

3. Rinse the fermented black beans briefly under cold water to remove excess salt. Chop them roughly. Some pieces can stay whole for texture.

4. Soak the dried mandarin peel in warm water for 10 minutes until pliable. Scrape off any white pith on the inside with a spoon, then dice finely.

5. In a large bowl, combine the ribs with the chopped black beans, garlic, ginger, diced mandarin peel, light soy sauce, Shaoxing wine, sugar, sesame oil, white pepper, and the remaining 10 g of cornstarch. Mix well, making sure each rib piece is evenly coated. Let marinate for 10 minutes at room temperature, or up to overnight in the refrigerator for deeper flavor.

6. Arrange the marinated rib pieces in a single layer on a heatproof plate or shallow bowl that fits inside your steamer. Do not overlap or stack the pieces. If you have too many for one plate, use two plates and steam in batches or stack the steamers.

7. Scatter the chili slices over the top if using.

8. Bring water in your steamer or wok to a vigorous, rolling boil before placing the plate inside.

9. Steam over high heat for 18 to 20 minutes. The ribs are done when the meat has pulled back slightly from the bone and the pork is tender when pierced with a chopstick. Thicker pieces may need an extra 2 to 3 minutes.

10. Remove the plate carefully from the steamer. Garnish with sliced scallion. Serve immediately in the steaming plate.

## Why This Works

Washing the ribs with cornstarch draws out myoglobin, the protein responsible for the red color in raw meat. Cornstarch acts as a mild abrasive and absorbent, pulling blood and dissolved proteins out of the cut surfaces more effectively than water alone. The result is ribs that steam to a clean, pale color rather than a murky gray.

Steaming cooks the ribs gently and evenly, allowing the connective tissue in the small pieces to break down without drying out the meat. The enclosed, moist environment keeps the ribs juicy. High heat and vigorous steam are important because weak steam extends the cooking time and results in tough, overcooked meat.

The dried mandarin peel (chen pi) adds a subtle citrus fragrance that cuts through the richness of the pork. In Cantonese cooking, chen pi is used specifically with steamed and braised meats for this balancing effect. Its slight bitterness works alongside the salty black beans and sweet sugar to create a sauce that is complex despite its simplicity.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Pork spare ribs** are a source of protein, zinc, and B vitamins. The small, bone-in pieces used in this dish mean more surface area for the marinade to flavor and more collagen to break down during steaming, contributing to the tender texture.

**Fermented black beans** have been used in Chinese cooking for over two thousand years. They are salted and fermented whole black soybeans, rich in naturally occurring glutamate. Studies suggest fermented soy foods may contribute to cardiovascular health.

**Dried mandarin peel (chen pi)** is both a culinary and traditional Chinese medicine ingredient. In TCM, aged chen pi is believed to support digestion and reduce phlegm. The peel contains limonene and other aromatic compounds that provide its distinctive fragrance.

## Substitutions and Variations

- **Ribs:** If you cannot find pre-cut spare ribs, buy a full slab and ask your butcher to cut it crosswise into 3 cm strips. Baby back ribs can be used but will be leaner and cook slightly faster.
- **Mandarin peel:** If dried mandarin peel is unavailable, substitute a 1 cm strip of fresh orange zest. The flavor will be brighter and less complex, but still pleasant.
- **With taro:** Layer sliced taro (about 1 cm thick) on the bottom of the plate and arrange ribs on top. The taro absorbs the juices and becomes silky and deeply flavored.
- **With pumpkin:** Place the ribs on top of pumpkin slices for a sweeter variation common in Cantonese home cooking.
- **Spicier version:** Add 5 ml of chili bean paste (doubanjiang) to the marinade, or increase the amount of fresh chili.

## Serving Suggestions

Serve as part of a dim sum spread alongside har gow, siu mai, stuffed peppers, and turnip cake. At home, these ribs work well as a side dish in a multi-course Chinese meal, particularly alongside lighter dishes like steamed fish or a stir-fried green vegetable. Steamed rice is essential to soak up the savory juices that collect on the plate during steaming.

---

## Storage and Reheating

Refrigerate leftover ribs in an airtight container for up to 3 days. Reheat by steaming for 5 to 6 minutes until heated through, which preserves the texture better than microwaving. If microwaving, cover with a damp paper towel and heat on medium power for 2 minutes. The ribs maintain their flavor well but will be slightly more tender upon reheating. Freezes adequately for up to 1 month. Thaw overnight in the refrigerator and reheat by steaming.
