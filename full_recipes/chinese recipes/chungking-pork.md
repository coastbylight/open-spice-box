# Chungking Pork (重庆猪肉)

*Thin-sliced pork tenderloin stir-fried with fermented black beans, crushed red pepper, cabbage, and bell pepper in a spicy hoisin sauce*

---

## Headnote

Chungking is the old romanization of Chongqing, a massive city in Sichuan province known for food so spicy it makes the rest of Sichuan look mild. The cooking there is built on chili peppers and fermented black beans (douchi), used together in proportions that would alarm anyone not raised on the cuisine. This stir-fry captures that spirit in a dish that takes less than thirty minutes from cutting board to table.

The pork tenderloin is sliced thin, tossed with rice wine and cornstarch, and stir-fried fast in a hot wok. Cabbage and bell pepper go in next, cooking just long enough to soften at the edges while staying crunchy in the center. The sauce is built on fermented black beans and crushed red pepper, cooked briefly in oil with ginger and garlic, then finished with hoisin sauce and dark soy sauce. The hoisin adds a sweet-savory depth that balances the heat. The dark soy adds color and a faint bitterness that keeps the sweetness in check.

The result is a dish that is spicy, savory, slightly sweet, and deeply satisfying over steamed rice. The cabbage is an underrated partner for pork in Sichuan cooking. Its mild sweetness and water content temper the heat without diluting the flavor, and its crunch provides textural contrast to the silky pork. The whole thing comes together in twelve minutes of wok time. The hardest part is slicing the tenderloin thin enough, and a ten-minute stint in the freezer solves that.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the pork
- 340 g pork tenderloin (3/4 lb), sliced crosswise 1/8 inch thick
- 2 tsp Chinese rice wine or dry sherry
- 4 tsp cornstarch, divided

### For the sauce
- 2 tbsp fermented black beans, coarsely chopped
- 1 tsp crushed red pepper (or to taste)
- 3 tbsp hoisin sauce
- 2 tsp dark soy sauce

### For the stir-fry
- 4 tbsp canola or vegetable oil, divided
- 225 g green cabbage (1/2 lb), cut into 1 1/2-inch chunks (about 3 cups)
- 1 medium red bell pepper, cut into 1 1/2-inch chunks
- 3 slices fresh ginger, unpeeled
- 2 cloves garlic, smashed and peeled

---

## Method

1. Marinate the pork. Whisk the rice wine and 2 teaspoons of cornstarch together in a bowl. Add the pork and mix well. In a separate small bowl, whisk the remaining 2 teaspoons of cornstarch with 60 ml (1/4 cup) water until dissolved.

2. Prepare the seasonings. In one bowl, combine the chopped black beans and crushed red pepper. In another, stir together the hoisin sauce and dark soy sauce.

3. Stir-fry the vegetables. Heat 2 tablespoons of oil in a wok over high heat until hot but not smoking. Add the cabbage. It should sizzle immediately. Cook, stirring, for about 3 minutes. Add the bell pepper and cook for 2 more minutes. The cabbage may brown slightly. Transfer to a plate.

4. Stir-fry the pork. Add the remaining 2 tablespoons of oil to the wok over high heat. Add the ginger and garlic and stir until they sizzle. Do not let them brown. Stir the pork mixture, add it to the wok, and cook, stirring briskly, until the pork is no longer pink, 2 to 3 minutes.

5. Combine and sauce. Add the black bean mixture and stir to combine. Add the hoisin-soy mixture and mix well. Return the vegetables and toss to coat. Pour in the cornstarch-water mixture and stir for 30 seconds until the sauce thickens and coats everything. Remove the ginger and garlic if desired.

6. Serve immediately with steamed rice.

## Why This Works

Slicing the pork tenderloin very thin (1/8 inch) ensures it cooks through in 2 to 3 minutes without toughening. Partially freezing the tenderloin for 10 minutes firms it enough to cut this thin.

The fermented black beans and crushed red pepper are combined and added together because they need the same amount of time in the hot oil to release their flavors. The hoisin and soy go in separately because they cook faster and would burn if added too early.

Cabbage holds up to high-heat stir-frying better than most vegetables. Its thick leaves can brown slightly without going limp, and its natural sweetness intensifies with heat, providing a counterpoint to the spicy, salty black bean sauce.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Fermented black beans (douchi):** The Sichuan pantry staple that provides deep, funky umami. Coarsely chopping them before adding releases more of their concentrated flavor.

**Pork tenderloin:** The leanest cut of pork, with only 3 grams of fat per 100g. Rich in thiamine (vitamin B1) and selenium.

**Cabbage:** A cruciferous vegetable high in vitamin C and fiber. The brief stir-fry preserves most of its nutrients.

---

## Substitutions and Variations

**Chicken or shrimp:** Both work with the same sauce. Adjust cooking time accordingly.

**Brussels sprouts:** Deconstructed sprout leaves can replace the cabbage for a nuttier variation.

**More heat:** Increase the crushed red pepper to 2 teaspoons, or add dried Sichuan chilies with the ginger.

**Coleslaw mix:** A bag of pre-shredded coleslaw can replace the chopped cabbage in a pinch.

---

## Serving Suggestions

Serve over steamed white rice, brown rice, or a wild rice blend. Pair with [Egg Drop Soup](/recipes/egg-drop-soup) and a simple green vegetable for a complete Sichuan-inspired dinner.

---

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store for up to 3 days. Reheat in a hot wok for 1 to 2 minutes.

**Freezing:** Not recommended. The vegetables soften and the sauce thins.
