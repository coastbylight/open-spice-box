# Jing Jiang Rou Si (京酱肉丝)

*Beijing-style shredded pork tenderloin stir-fried in sweet bean sauce, wrapped in tofu skin with raw scallion strips, the home cook's Peking duck*

---

## Headnote

The name breaks down simply: jing for Beijing, jiang for sauce, rou for meat, si for shreds. It is one of the defining dishes of Beijing home cooking, and the reason it works so well is that it borrows the entire eating experience of Peking duck, the sweet-savory sauce, the raw scallion strips, the thin wrapper, and replaces the duck with something a home cook can actually make on a Tuesday night.

The origin story, whether true or embellished, places the dish in a 1930s Beijing courtyard where an old man named Lao Chen could not afford roast duck for his grandson on New Year's Eve. He shredded pork, stir-fried it in sweet bean sauce, and wrapped it in the tofu skins he made for a living. His grandson grew up to become a chef at a duck restaurant, and the improvised dish eventually became one of Beijing's most iconic.

The sauce that defines this dish is tian mian jiang, sweet fermented wheat paste. It is not hoisin, not doubanjiang, not regular soybean paste. Tian mian jiang is thick, dark, salty, and faintly sweet, with a deep fermented umami that nothing else can replicate. It gets fried briefly in hot oil before the pork goes back in, which transforms it from a raw, sharp paste into something rounded and aromatic. The pork is tenderloin cut into thin matchsticks, marinated with cornstarch and Shaoxing wine for a basic velvet that keeps the shreds silky rather than tough. The whole stir-fry takes about three minutes. The eating is the slow part: laying a strip of tofu skin on your palm, spooning on the sauced pork, adding a few strips of raw scallion, rolling it up, and biting through the cool wrapper into the warm, savory filling.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the pork marinade
- 350 g pork tenderloin (about 12 oz), cut into thin matchstick strips
- 1/4 tsp salt
- 1/8 tsp ground white pepper
- 1 tsp Shaoxing wine
- 2 tsp cornstarch
- 1 tsp vegetable oil

### For the sauce
- 2 1/2 tbsp tian mian jiang (sweet bean sauce / sweet wheat paste)
- 1 tbsp light soy sauce
- 1 tsp sugar
- 60 ml water (about 1/4 cup)
- 1/2 tsp dark soy sauce (optional, for color)

### For stir-frying
- 3 tbsp vegetable oil

### For serving
- 4 to 5 large scallion whites (or 8 to 10 regular scallions), cut into 3-inch strips and soaked in ice water for 10 minutes
- Tofu skin sheets (doupi), blanched in hot water for 5 seconds and cut into 3-inch squares
- Or: Mandarin pancakes (the same thin wrappers used for Peking duck)

---

## Method

1. Marinate the pork. Place the pork strips in a bowl. Add the salt, white pepper, and Shaoxing wine. Mix well and let the pork absorb the liquid for a minute. Add the cornstarch and toss to coat evenly. Add the teaspoon of oil last and mix again. This seals the marinade coating. Set aside for 15 minutes.

2. Mix the sauce. In a small bowl, combine the tian mian jiang, light soy sauce, sugar, water, and dark soy sauce if using. Stir well and set near the stove.

3. Stir-fry the pork. Heat the wok over high heat until smoking. Add the oil and swirl to coat. Add the marinated pork and immediately spread the strips out in the wok. Stir-fry for about 30 to 45 seconds, using chopsticks to separate the strips, until the surface has turned white but the inside is still slightly pink. The pork will finish cooking in the sauce. Remove the pork from the wok and set aside. Leave about 1 tablespoon of oil in the wok.

4. Cook the sauce. Return the wok to medium heat. Add the tian mian jiang mixture to the remaining oil. Stir-fry vigorously for about 30 seconds until the paste is fragrant and slightly darkened. This step cooks off the raw flavor of the bean sauce and deepens it. Add the rest of the sauce liquid and stir until the sauce thickens and becomes glossy, about 1 minute.

5. Combine and finish. Return the pork to the wok. Toss quickly to coat every strip in the sauce, about 30 seconds. The sauce should cling to the pork in a concentrated, glossy coating, not pool as a liquid. Turn off the heat.

6. Plate and serve. Drain the scallion strips and arrange them as a bed on a serving plate. Pile the sauced pork on top. Serve with tofu skin squares or Mandarin pancakes on the side. To eat: place a wrapper flat, spoon some pork and a few scallion strips onto it, roll it up, and eat.

## Why This Works

Cutting pork tenderloin into thin matchsticks and marinating with cornstarch creates a basic velvet that protects the meat during high-heat stir-frying. The starch coating forms a thin barrier between the pork and the hot oil, keeping the interior moist while the surface sets quickly. Removing the pork while still slightly pink ensures it finishes cooking gently in the sauce rather than toughening.

Frying the tian mian jiang in oil before adding liquid is the step that separates a good version from a flat one. Raw tian mian jiang has a sharp, one-dimensional saltiness. Thirty seconds in hot oil triggers Maillard reactions in the fermented wheat proteins, rounding the flavor and adding a toasted depth. This is the same principle as blooming spices in oil, applied to a paste.

The scallion strips soaked in ice water curl slightly and develop a crisp, refreshing bite that contrasts the warm, rich, salty pork. In traditional Beijing cooking, the scallions are not a garnish. They are a structural part of the dish, providing the fresh, sharp element that makes the wrap experience balanced rather than one-note.

The tofu skin wrapper ties the dish back to its origin story and to the parallel with Peking duck. The cool, slightly chewy wrapper against the warm, sauced pork and the crisp, raw scallion creates a three-texture bite that is more satisfying than eating the pork over rice.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Tian mian jiang (sweet bean sauce):** A fermented paste made primarily from wheat flour with some soybeans, salt, and water. The fermentation process produces glutamate (umami), amino acids, and B vitamins. It is the same sauce family as the dipping sauce served with Peking duck. In Beijing, the most famous producer is Liu Bi Ju, a soy sauce shop that has been operating since the Ming Dynasty. Not interchangeable with hoisin sauce (which is sweeter and thinner) or doubanjiang (which is chili-based and Sichuanese).

**Pork tenderloin:** The leanest cut of pork, with about 22 grams of protein and only 3 grams of fat per 100-gram serving. It is also one of the best meat sources of thiamine (vitamin B1), a nutrient important for energy metabolism. The lean, fine-grained texture makes it ideal for julienning into the thin strips this dish requires.

**Scallions:** Raw scallions provide allicin (the same sulfur compound found in garlic) with documented antimicrobial properties, as well as vitamin K and folate. In traditional Chinese medicine, scallions are classified as warming and pungent, used to promote sweating and dispel surface cold. The large northern Chinese scallion (da cong) used traditionally in this dish is milder and sweeter than regular scallions.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Pork loin:** Works well if tenderloin is unavailable. Slightly less tender but more widely available. Partially freeze for 30 minutes before slicing to make thin cuts easier.

**Chicken breast:** A common substitution. Cut into thin strips and marinate the same way. Cooks even faster than pork.

**Tian mian jiang substitutes:** There is no perfect substitute. Hoisin sauce thinned with a little soy sauce is the closest approximation but is sweeter and less complex. Korean ssamjang is another option. Do not use doubanjiang, which is a completely different flavor profile.

**Mandarin pancakes instead of tofu skin:** The same thin wheat pancakes used for Peking duck. Steam them briefly to soften before serving. This is the more common restaurant presentation.

**Lettuce wraps:** Butter lettuce cups work as a lighter, lower-carb wrapper alternative, though this is not traditional.

**With egg white in marinade:** Some recipes add 1 tablespoon of egg white to the marinade for an even silkier texture on the pork strips. This is a valid variation.

## Serving Suggestions

This is a wrap dish, meant to be assembled and eaten by hand. Set the platter of pork and scallions in the center of the table with a stack of tofu skin squares or warm Mandarin pancakes, and let everyone build their own wraps. For a complete Beijing-style meal, serve alongside [Egg Drop Soup](/recipes/egg-drop-soup), a plate of [Sauteed Broccoli with Garlic](/recipes/sauteed-broccoli-with-garlic) or [Bok Choy in Ginger Sauce](/recipes/bok-choy-in-ginger-sauce), and steamed rice. The pork also works over plain noodles if you prefer not to wrap.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store the pork in sauce for up to 2 days. Keep scallions and wrappers separate. The pork reheats well; the scallions should be fresh.

**Reheating:** Warm the pork in a wok or skillet over medium heat for 1 to 2 minutes. Add a splash of water if the sauce has thickened too much. Prepare fresh scallion strips when serving.

**Freezing:** The sauced pork freezes well for up to 2 months. Thaw overnight and reheat in a wok. The sauce may need a little water to loosen.
