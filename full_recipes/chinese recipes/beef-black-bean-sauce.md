# Beef in Black Bean Sauce (豉汁牛肉)

*Velveted beef seared in a hot wok with fermented black beans, garlic, bell pepper, and onion in a glossy cornstarch sauce*

---

## Headnote

Fermented black beans are one of the oldest seasoning ingredients in Chinese cooking, older than soy sauce, and they taste like nothing else. They are small, wrinkled, intensely salty, and carry a deep, funky umami that makes everything they touch taste more savory. In Cantonese kitchens, they are the foundation of black bean sauce, paired most often with garlic and applied to everything from spare ribs to clams to, in this case, beef and bell peppers.

This is the dish that appears on every Chinese takeout menu in the West, but the restaurant version is usually built on jarred black bean sauce. The from-scratch version uses actual fermented black beans, soaked to soften their saltiness, then fried in oil with garlic until they become fragrant and slightly sticky. The difference is the same difference between fresh garlic and garlic powder. Both work. One is alive.

The beef is marinated with soy sauce, oyster sauce, cornstarch, and a pinch of baking soda, then seared fast in hot oil. The baking soda is a velveting shortcut that tenderizes the surface proteins, giving the beef that silky, almost slippery texture that defines restaurant-quality Chinese stir-fries. The bell pepper and onion cook for just a minute so they stay crisp. The sauce is cornstarch-thickened water with a little soy and sugar, and it turns glossy and clingy in the wok, coating everything in a shiny, savory glaze. Shaoxing wine poured around the edge of the wok at the last moment adds a burst of fragrance that pulls the whole dish together.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the beef marinade
- 400 g beef sirloin or flank steak (about 14 oz), thinly sliced against the grain
- 1 tbsp light soy sauce
- 1 tsp dark soy sauce
- 1 tbsp oyster sauce
- 2 tsp cornstarch
- 1/4 tsp baking soda
- 1 tbsp toasted sesame oil

### For the stir-fry
- 75 g fermented black beans (about 1/2 cup), soaked in water for 30 minutes and drained
- 1 medium onion, cut into 1-inch squares
- 1 green bell pepper, cut into 1-inch squares
- 1 tbsp garlic, finely minced (about 4 cloves)
- 120 ml peanut or vegetable oil (1/2 cup, for velveting)
- 1 tbsp Shaoxing wine

### For the sauce
- 1 tbsp light soy sauce
- 2 tsp sugar
- 2 tbsp cornstarch
- 240 ml water (1 cup)

---

## Method

1. Marinate the beef. Mix the soy sauces, oyster sauce, cornstarch, and baking soda in a bowl. Add the beef and toss to coat. Add the sesame oil last and mix again. Refrigerate for 1 hour.

2. Soak the beans. Place the fermented black beans in a bowl, cover with water, and soak for 30 minutes to 1 hour. This removes excess salt. Drain well.

3. Mix the sauce. Stir the cornstarch, soy sauce, and sugar together until smooth, then stir in the water. Set aside.

4. Velvet the beef. Heat the oil in a wok over high heat. Add the beef and stir-fry for 30 seconds until it just changes color from red to brown. Remove immediately with a slotted spoon. Drain the wok, keeping about 3 tablespoons of oil.

5. Stir-fry the aromatics. Return the wok to high heat. Add the drained black beans and stir for 20 seconds. Add the garlic and stir for 10 seconds. Add the bell pepper and onion and cook for 1 minute until barely softened.

6. Finish. Add the beef and any juices back to the wok. Toss for 1 minute. Pour the Shaoxing wine around the sides of the wok so it sizzles down into the ingredients. Toss for 30 seconds. Give the sauce a stir and pour it in. Let it bubble for about 1 minute until it thickens and turns glossy, coating everything evenly.

7. Serve immediately with steamed white rice.

## Why This Works

Fermented black beans (douchi) are soybeans that have been salted, dried, and fermented. The fermentation produces glutamate and other umami compounds that give the sauce a savory depth that soy sauce alone cannot achieve. Soaking them first is essential because they are intensely salty straight from the package, and the soaking mellows them to the right level.

The baking soda in the marinade raises the pH on the surface of the beef, which prevents the proteins from contracting and squeezing out moisture during high-heat cooking. This is the Chinese restaurant technique called velveting, and it is why takeout beef has that silky, almost impossibly tender texture.

Pouring the Shaoxing wine around the edge of the wok rather than directly onto the ingredients is a traditional technique. The wine hits the screaming-hot metal surface first, instantly vaporizing and creating a burst of fragrant steam that infuses the food from above. Pouring it directly onto the ingredients would cool them and dilute the sauce.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Fermented black beans (douchi):** One of the oldest fermented foods in Chinese cuisine, predating soy sauce. The fermentation produces beneficial bacteria and enzymes, along with the glutamate that gives them their intense umami. They are high in sodium, which is why the recipe soaks them and uses moderate soy sauce elsewhere. Do not confuse with Western dried black beans or canned black beans, which are entirely different.

**Baking soda:** Used here as a meat tenderizer, not a leavener. It works by raising the pH on the meat's surface, which inhibits protein bonding and keeps the texture soft. A quarter teaspoon is enough for 400g of beef.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Beef cut:** Flank steak, scotch fillet (boneless rib eye), or porterhouse all work. For a budget option, blade steak marinated for 24 hours instead of 1 hour.

**Less oil:** Use 3 tablespoons of oil and saute the beef instead of velveting. The texture will be slightly less silky but still good.

**Shaoxing wine:** Dry sherry is the closest substitute. For alcohol-free, replace half the water in the sauce with low-sodium chicken stock.

**With other proteins:** Chicken thigh, pork, shrimp, or firm tofu all work with the same black bean sauce base.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store for up to 3 days. The sauce may thin slightly when reheated.

**Reheating:** Warm in a hot wok or skillet for 1 to 2 minutes. Add a splash of water if the sauce has thickened.

**Freezing:** Not recommended. The cornstarch sauce breaks down when frozen and thawed.
