# Gobi Manchurian

*Crispy battered cauliflower tossed in a tangy, sweet, and spicy Indo-Chinese sauce of soy, chili, and vinegar*

---

## Headnote

Indo-Chinese food is one of those cuisines that exists because two cultures collided in a kitchen and decided to keep going. It was born in the Chinese communities of Kolkata and Mumbai, where Chinese cooking techniques met Indian ingredients and Indian appetites. The result is a category of food that sounds Chinese and tastes Indian, or maybe the other way around. Gobi Manchurian is its most famous creation, and it appears on street carts and restaurant menus across India in quantities that dwarf most actual Chinese dishes in popularity.

The dish is cauliflower florets dipped in a batter of flour and cornstarch, deep-fried until shatteringly crisp, and then tossed in a sauce built from soy sauce, chili sauce, vinegar, and garlic. The sauce is the key. It hits sweet, sour, salty, and spicy simultaneously, and it clings to the cratered surface of the fried cauliflower like it was designed for exactly that purpose. The contrast between the crunchy batter and the sticky, punchy sauce is what makes the dish addictive.

There are two versions: dry and gravy. The dry version, which is what this recipe produces, is an appetizer or snack, meant to be eaten with toothpicks or fingers alongside drinks or as a starter before fried rice and hakka noodles. The gravy version adds more liquid to the sauce and is served over rice. Both are good. The dry version is more popular because the crunch lasts longer. Make it, eat it fast, and do not apologize for the pile of napkins.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the cauliflower batter
- 1 small head cauliflower (about 500 g), cut into medium florets (250 g cleaned)
- 65 g all-purpose flour (about 1/2 cup)
- 32 g cornstarch (about 1/4 cup)
- 1/2 to 1 tsp Kashmiri red chili powder (optional, for color)
- 1/4 tsp ground black pepper
- 1/4 tsp salt
- 120 ml water, or as needed
- Oil for deep frying

### For the Manchurian sauce
- 1 1/2 tbsp vegetable oil
- 1 tbsp garlic, finely chopped
- 1/2 tbsp fresh ginger, finely chopped
- 1 green chili, chopped (optional)
- 1/3 cup onion or scallion whites, finely chopped
- 1/4 cup bell pepper, finely chopped (optional)
- 1 tbsp soy sauce
- 2 to 3 tbsp red chili sauce (or 2 tbsp hot sweet sauce, adjust to taste)
- 1 to 2 tbsp tomato ketchup (optional)
- 1/2 tbsp rice vinegar
- 1 tsp sugar
- 1/2 to 3/4 tsp ground black pepper
- 3 to 4 tbsp water
- Salt to taste

### For garnish
- 2 tbsp scallion greens, chopped

---

## Method

1. Prepare the cauliflower. Cut the cauliflower into medium-sized florets. Soak them in slightly hot water for 5 minutes to clean, then drain and rinse with cold water. Pat dry thoroughly with a clean cloth or paper towels. Any moisture on the florets will prevent them from crisping properly.

2. Make the batter. In a mixing bowl, combine the flour, cornstarch, chili powder, black pepper, and salt. Add water gradually, stirring as you go, until you have a smooth, free-flowing batter with no lumps. The consistency should coat a floret evenly without being too thick or too runny. Taste and adjust salt.

3. Fry the cauliflower. Heat oil for deep frying over medium heat. Test by dropping a small amount of batter into the oil. It should sizzle and rise to the surface without browning immediately. Add the florets to the batter in batches and coat them well. Drop them one by one into the hot oil. Do not stir for the first 1 to 2 minutes, as the coating can break apart. Then stir occasionally and fry until golden and crisp. Remove with a spider or slotted spoon and drain on paper towels. Repeat with remaining florets.

4. Make the sauce. Heat 1 1/2 tablespoons of oil in a wide pan or wok over high heat. Add the garlic, ginger, and green chili. Saute for 1 to 2 minutes until fragrant. Add the scallion whites and bell pepper and cook on high heat for 2 minutes, tossing frequently.

5. Add the sauce liquids. Stir together the soy sauce, chili sauce, ketchup, vinegar, and sugar in a small bowl. Pour this mixture and the water into the pan. Cook, stirring, until the sauce thickens and becomes glossy. Add the black pepper. Taste and adjust. The sauce should be tangy, slightly sweet, and hot.

6. Toss and serve. Let the sauce cool for 1 to 2 minutes so it does not immediately soften the batter. Add the fried cauliflower and toss gently to coat every piece with the sauce. Garnish with scallion greens. Serve immediately while the cauliflower is still crisp.

## Why This Works

The batter is a combination of all-purpose flour and cornstarch, and the ratio matters. The flour provides structure and cling. The cornstarch provides crunch. Together they create a coating that is crispy on the outside and barely there on the inside, letting the cauliflower's texture come through. Too much flour makes the coating bready. Too much cornstarch makes it glassy and fragile.

Drying the cauliflower thoroughly before battering is the step that separates crispy gobi from soggy gobi. Water on the surface creates steam during frying, which softens the batter from within. A few minutes with a towel prevents this entirely.

The Manchurian sauce achieves its distinctive flavor through the balance of four elements: soy sauce for salt and umami, chili sauce for heat and sweetness, vinegar for acidity, and sugar to round everything out. The sauce is intentionally thick and concentrated. It needs to coat the cauliflower without making it soggy, which is why it is kept to a small volume rather than a pool.

Letting the sauce cool briefly before adding the cauliflower is a detail that makes a real difference. Blazing hot sauce immediately softens the batter. A slightly cooled sauce clings without breaking the crust.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Cauliflower:** A cruciferous vegetable rich in vitamin C, vitamin K, and fiber. It contains sulforaphane and indole-3-carbinol, compounds that have been extensively studied for their role in supporting the body's detoxification pathways and potential anti-cancer properties. In Ayurveda, cauliflower is classified as a vata-aggravating food that benefits from warming spices and oil to balance its qualities, which this preparation provides generously.

**Ginger and garlic:** The combination appears in virtually every Indo-Chinese dish and serves both culinary and functional purposes. Ginger's gingerols and garlic's allicin both have documented anti-inflammatory properties. Together they form the aromatic base that bridges Indian and Chinese cooking traditions.

**Soy sauce:** The fermented soybean condiment that anchors the dish in its Chinese roots. Naturally fermented soy sauce provides glutamate (umami), amino acids, and antioxidants from the fermentation process.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Gravy version:** Triple the amounts of soy sauce, chili sauce, vinegar, ketchup, and sugar. Mix 3/4 tablespoon cornstarch with 1 cup water and stir into the bubbling sauce to create a gravy. Serve over steamed rice.

**Semi-dry version:** Double the sauce quantities and add 1 teaspoon cornstarch mixed with 1/2 cup water to thicken.

**Baked version:** For less oil, toss the battered florets on a lined baking sheet and bake at 425°F (220°C) for 25 minutes, turning once. They will not be as crispy as fried but still hold the sauce well.

**Paneer Manchurian:** Replace the cauliflower with cubes of paneer. Skip the batter and simply coat in cornstarch before frying. The technique is otherwise identical.

**Baby corn or mushroom Manchurian:** Both work with the same batter and sauce. Adjust frying time accordingly.

## Serving Suggestions

Gobi Manchurian is an appetizer first. Serve it on its own with toothpicks alongside drinks, or as a starter before a meal of hakka noodles or vegetable fried rice. In Indo-Chinese restaurants, it appears alongside chili paneer, spring rolls, and manchow soup as part of the starter spread. For a complete Indo-Chinese dinner at home, pair with [vegetable fried rice](/recipes/vegetable-fried-rice) or [hakka noodles](/recipes/hakka-noodles) and a hot bowl of sweet corn soup.

---

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store the fried cauliflower and sauce separately for up to 2 days. The cauliflower will lose its crunch in the fridge regardless, but keeping the sauce separate gives you the option to re-crisp.

**Reheating:** Reheat the cauliflower in an oven at 400°F (200°C) for 8 to 10 minutes to restore some crunch, then toss with warmed sauce. Microwaving makes the coating completely soft.

**Freezing:** Not recommended. The batter coating does not survive freezing and thawing.
