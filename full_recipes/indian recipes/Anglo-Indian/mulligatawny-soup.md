# Mulligatawny Soup

*The Anglo-Indian pepper water soup, a colonial adaptation of Tamil rasam thickened with butter, rice, apple, and cream, with chicken or any protein you like*

---

## Headnote

The name comes from the Tamil "milagu thanni," which means pepper water. The original was rasam: a thin, fiery, tamarind-spiked broth served alongside rice at the end of a South Indian meal. It was never a soup in the European sense. It was a digestive, a course, a daily ritual. When British officers stationed in the Madras Presidency encountered it in the 18th century, they wanted it as a first course. Their Indian cooks, the khansamas who ran colonial kitchens, obliged by thickening it, enriching it, adding meat, and transforming it into something that fit the structure of a British dinner.

What arrived back in England was a different dish entirely: a thick, curry-scented soup with chicken, vegetables, cream, and rice. The original Tamil cooks would barely recognize it. But it became one of the most popular soups in Victorian England and remains a staple of Anglo-Indian cooking, a genuine hybrid cuisine that emerged from three centuries of cultural collision in kitchens across the subcontinent.

This version is the Anglo-Indian classic, not the Tamil original. It uses butter and flour as a base, curry powder as the spice shortcut the colonial kitchen standardized, and a tart apple that adds a sweetness and body that no South Indian cook would have considered but that somehow works. The chicken goes in raw and simmers in the broth until tender. The rice cooks in the soup itself, thickening it slightly and giving each spoonful some heft. Cream at the end is optional but traditional in the British style, turning the broth from golden to a pale, rich, warming thing that tastes like a cold evening made comfortable. The recipe is flexible with protein: chicken thighs, chicken breast, pre-stewed beef chuck, or sauteed mushrooms for vegetarian all work well.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the soup
- 56 g butter (half a stick)
- 1/2 cup yellow onion, chopped
- 2 celery stalks, chopped
- 2 small carrots, diced
- 1 leek, white part only, thinly sliced
- 2 tbsp all-purpose flour
- 2 tsp curry powder (mild, medium, or hot to taste)
- 5 cups chicken broth
- 6 boneless, skinless chicken thighs, cut into 1/2-inch cubes (see substitutions)
- 1/2 apple, cored, peeled, and finely chopped (a tart variety like Granny Smith)
- 1/4 cup white rice
- 1/8 tsp ground nutmeg
- 1/8 tsp dried thyme
- Salt to taste
- Freshly ground black pepper to taste

### For finishing
- 1 cup heavy cream, heated (optional)
- Chopped fresh parsley, to garnish

---

## Method

1. Cook the vegetables. Melt the butter in a large pot over medium heat. Add the onion, celery, leek, and carrots. Saute until they start to soften, about 8 to 10 minutes. Do not let them brown. You want them translucent and tender, not caramelized.

2. Add the flour and curry. Sprinkle the flour and curry powder over the softened vegetables. Stir well and cook over medium heat for 5 minutes. The flour will coat the vegetables and the curry powder will bloom in the butter, turning the mixture fragrant and golden. This is a roux, the base that thickens the soup.

3. Add the broth. Pour in the chicken stock and stir well, scraping up anything stuck to the bottom of the pot. Bring to a boil, then reduce to a gentle simmer. Cook for 30 minutes, stirring occasionally, until the vegetables are very tender and the broth has thickened slightly.

4. Add the remaining ingredients. Add the apple, rice, chicken, salt, pepper, nutmeg, and thyme. Stir well. Return to a gentle simmer and cook for 15 minutes, or until the rice is tender and the chicken is cooked through.

5. Finish and serve. Taste and adjust the seasoning. If using cream, heat it separately (do not boil) and stir it into the soup just before serving, or swirl it into each bowl individually. Garnish with chopped fresh parsley.

## Why This Works

The butter-flour roux gives the soup body without making it stodgy. Two tablespoons of flour in five cups of broth produces a thickness that is somewhere between a broth and a puree, substantial enough to feel like a meal but not so heavy that it sits in your stomach. Cooking the curry powder in the butter for five minutes blooms the spices and removes their raw, dusty taste.

The apple is the ingredient that surprises people. It softens completely during the 15-minute simmer and effectively disappears into the soup, adding a faint sweetness and a pectin-based body that rounds out the curry flavor. Tart apples work best because the acidity echoes the tamarind that would have been in the original rasam. Sweet apples make the soup cloying.

Rice cooked directly in the broth thickens the soup further as it releases starch, and each grain absorbs the curry-scented liquid as it cooks. This is more traditional than serving rice on the side, though both approaches have historical precedent.

The cream finish is the British contribution. It tempers the heat of the curry and adds a richness that transforms the soup from a light starter into something that could anchor a meal. Leaving it out produces a leaner, more intensely spiced version that sits closer to the original rasam spirit.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Curry powder:** The standardized spice blend that British colonial kitchens adopted as a convenience. Most commercial curry powders contain turmeric, coriander, cumin, fenugreek, and chili in varying proportions. The turmeric provides curcumin, one of the most studied natural anti-inflammatory compounds. In Ayurveda, the combination of warming spices found in curry powder is considered digestive and circulatory.

**Apple:** The pectin in apples acts as a natural thickener when cooked down. Apples also provide quercetin, an antioxidant flavonoid that has been studied for anti-inflammatory and antihistamine properties. The acidity of a tart apple contributes a brightness that balances the richness of the butter and cream.

**Nutmeg:** Used in very small quantities here, nutmeg contains myristicin and elemicin, compounds with mild sedative and digestive properties in traditional medicine. It adds a warm, slightly sweet complexity that supports the curry powder without competing with it.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Protein options:** This recipe is flexible:
- **Chicken breast:** Use 2 breasts, cubed. Cooks in the same time as thighs but is leaner.
- **Beef chuck or stewing beef:** About 1 lb, stewed to full tenderness separately before adding to the soup. Beef takes much longer to cook than chicken and must be pre-cooked.
- **Mushrooms:** About 3 cups sauteed mushrooms for a vegetarian version. Add them with the apple and rice. Use vegetable broth instead of chicken broth.

**With lentils:** For a more substantial, lentil-thickened version closer to many modern recipes, add 1/2 cup rinsed red lentils with the broth. They will break down completely and thicken the soup naturally. Reduce or eliminate the flour.

**With coconut milk:** Replace the cream with one can of coconut milk for a dairy-free version that leans more toward the South Asian side of the dish's heritage.

**Pureed version:** For a smoother soup, blend half or all of the soup after step 3 (before adding the chicken, rice, and apple). This produces a velvety base with a more refined texture.

**Without cream:** Simply omit it. The soup is still rich from the butter and completely satisfying without cream. This produces a lighter, more spice-forward bowl.

## Serving Suggestions

Mulligatawny is a meal in a bowl. Serve it with crusty bread, warm naan, or a simple green salad. For a more formal presentation, ladle the soup into bowls and swirl cream into each one individually. In the Victorian tradition, it was served as a first course before a roast. At home, it works perfectly as a one-pot dinner on a cold evening, perhaps with a plate of [Scallion Pancakes](/recipes/scallion-pancakes) or warm bread on the side.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store in an airtight container for up to 4 days. The flavors deepen significantly overnight. The soup will thicken as the rice continues to absorb liquid.

**Reheating:** Warm gently over medium-low heat, adding a splash of broth or water to thin the soup back to its original consistency. If you added cream originally, add a little more when reheating to refresh the richness.

**Freezing:** Freezes well for up to 3 months. The rice will soften further when thawed but the overall texture remains good. For best results, freeze before adding cream and add fresh cream when reheating.
