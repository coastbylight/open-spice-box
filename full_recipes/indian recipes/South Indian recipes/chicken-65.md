# Chicken 65

*Chennai's famous deep-fried chicken tossed in a crackling tempering of curry leaves, mustard seeds, green chilies, and garlic*

---

## Headnote

The story goes that Chicken 65 was invented in 1965 at Buhari Hotel on Mount Road in Chennai, and that the number was simply the item's position on the menu. The restaurant later added Chicken 78 and Chicken 82, following the same logic. Whether the origin story is precisely true matters less than the fact that the dish became one of the most popular appetizers in India, traveling from Chennai to Hyderabad to every Indian restaurant with a tandoor section and a drinks menu.

What makes Chicken 65 different from other fried chicken is what happens after the frying. The chicken comes out of the oil golden and crispy, looking like any good fritter. Then it goes into a wok with a small amount of oil where mustard seeds are crackling, curry leaves are crisping, green chilies are sizzling, and garlic is turning golden. This tempering, this tadka, is what transforms fried chicken into Chicken 65. The curry leaves shatter between your teeth with a nutty, citrusy intensity. The mustard seeds pop with sharp, pungent heat. The green chilies add a fresh, grassy fire that is completely different from the dried chili heat already in the marinade.

The marinade itself is yogurt-based, which is the South Asian solution to tender fried chicken. The lactic acid in the yogurt breaks down the surface proteins gently, making the chicken tender without the mushiness that vinegar or citrus can cause. A whole egg goes in to bind the coating, along with cornstarch for crunch and enough chili powder to turn everything a deep, warning red. The chicken marinates for at least a couple of hours, though overnight is better. Then it fries fast in small batches and finishes in the tadka. The whole second phase takes about two minutes. Serve it with sambar and rice and do not expect leftovers.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the marinade
- 500 g boneless chicken (thighs preferred), cut into big chunks or strips
- 1 tbsp plain yogurt
- 1 tbsp red chili powder (Kashmiri for color, regular for heat, or a mix)
- Salt to taste
- 2 tsp ginger-garlic paste
- 1 tsp turmeric
- 1 tbsp cornstarch
- 1 tsp garam masala
- 1 large egg
- Oil for deep frying

### For the tempering (tadka)
- 1 tbsp oil (from the frying oil, or fresh)
- 1/2 tsp mustard seeds
- 20 to 25 fresh curry leaves, torn from the stem
- 5 to 6 green chilies, slit lengthwise and cut into 2-inch pieces
- 1 tbsp fresh yogurt (curd)
- Red chili powder to taste (optional, for color)

---

## Method

1. Marinate the chicken. In a large bowl, combine the chicken with the yogurt, red chili powder, salt, ginger-garlic paste, turmeric, cornstarch, garam masala, and egg. Mix thoroughly with your hands until every piece is evenly coated. The mixture should be thick and clingy, not runny. Cover and refrigerate for at least 2 hours, ideally overnight.

2. Deep fry the chicken. Heat oil for deep frying in a deep pan or wok over medium-high heat. Test the temperature by dropping a small piece of batter into the oil. It should sizzle and rise to the surface slowly without browning instantly. Fry the chicken in small batches, 5 to 6 pieces at a time. Do not crowd the pan. Fry for 4 to 5 minutes per batch until the pieces are golden and crispy, turning once. They should look like golden fritters. Remove with a slotted spoon and drain on paper towels. Repeat until all the chicken is fried.

3. Make the tempering. Pour out all but about 1 tablespoon of oil from the pan (or use a clean kadai with 1 tablespoon of oil). Heat over high heat. Add the mustard seeds. Wait for them to crackle and pop, about 10 seconds. Add the torn curry leaves. They will sputter aggressively. Stand back. Add the slit green chilies and toss for 15 to 20 seconds until the curry leaves are crisp and the chilies are blistered.

4. Toss the chicken. Add all the fried chicken back into the pan. Toss vigorously to coat every piece with the tempering. Add the tablespoon of fresh yogurt and toss again. The yogurt adds a slight tang and helps the tempering cling. If the color is not red enough, dust in a little more chili powder and toss. The entire tempering and tossing step takes about 1 to 2 minutes.

5. Serve immediately. Pile onto a plate and serve hot with sambar and steamed rice, or on its own as a snack with onion rings and lemon wedges.

## Why This Works

The yogurt marinade does two things simultaneously. The lactic acid tenderizes the surface of the chicken by gently denaturing the proteins, which is why yogurt-marinated chicken stays tender even after deep frying. The yogurt also helps the spice paste adhere to the meat, creating a thick coating that sets into a crispy shell during frying. The egg in the marinade binds everything together and adds richness. The cornstarch provides the crunch.

Frying in small batches is not a suggestion. Crowding the pan drops the oil temperature, which means the chicken steams rather than fries, producing a soggy coating instead of a crispy one. Five to six pieces at a time is the maximum for most home woks.

The tempering at the end is what makes this Chicken 65 rather than generic fried chicken. The mustard seeds, curry leaves, and green chilies are the holy trinity of South Indian tempering. They provide three different kinds of heat: the sharp pungency of the mustard seeds, the nutty warmth of the fried curry leaves, and the fresh, grassy burn of the green chilies. Layered on top of the dried chili heat already in the marinade, the result is a dish with a complex, multi-layered spiciness that hits different parts of your mouth at different times.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Curry leaves:** Fresh curry leaves are one of the most nutritionally dense herbs in South Indian cooking. They are rich in vitamins A, B, C, and E, and contain carbazole alkaloids, which have been studied for antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. In Ayurveda, curry leaves are classified as a digestive stimulant, used to strengthen agni (digestive fire) and relieve nausea and indigestion. They are also traditionally used in hair oils to prevent premature graying, one of Ayurveda's best-known folk remedies. The brief frying in this recipe preserves their aromatic compounds while making them crisp enough to eat whole.

**Mustard seeds:** When crackled in hot oil, mustard seeds release isothiocyanates, the same class of compounds found in wasabi and horseradish. These compounds have documented antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory properties. In Ayurveda, mustard seeds are classified as warming, used to stimulate digestion and circulation. In the tadka here, they add a subtle crunch and a sharp, pungent flavor that is the signature of South Indian cooking.

**Yogurt:** The lactic acid bacteria in yogurt (Lactobacillus) produce acid that gently denatures surface proteins on the chicken, tenderizing it without the harshness of chemical marinades. Yogurt has been used as a meat marinade across South Asia and Central Asia for thousands of years, a technique that appears in tandoori chicken, kebabs, and countless other preparations.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Bone-in version:** The original Buhari Hotel version used bone-in chicken. Use small drumsticks or wings, extend the frying time to 7 to 8 minutes, and ensure the oil temperature stays steady. Bone-in pieces are more forgiving and stay juicier.

**Without egg:** Replace the egg with 2 tablespoons of rice flour or additional cornstarch. The coating will be slightly less rich but still crispy.

**Paneer 65:** Replace the chicken with cubes of paneer. Skip the marinade and instead coat the paneer in cornstarch mixed with the spice powders. Fry until golden and toss in the same tadka.

**Gobi 65:** Use cauliflower florets instead of chicken. Blanch briefly, coat in the same marinade (without the egg), and fry. Toss in the tempering the same way.

**Restaurant-style red color:** Restaurants achieve the deep red color with Kashmiri chili powder (which is more about color than heat) or a pinch of red food coloring. At home, a heavy hand with Kashmiri chili powder in both the marinade and the tadka will get you close.

## Serving Suggestions

Chicken 65 is an appetizer or a snack, not typically a main course, though nobody would fault you for making it one. In Chennai restaurants, it appears alongside other starters before a meal of biryani or a thali. At home, serve it with sambar and steamed rice for a South Indian meal, or on its own with sliced raw onion, lemon wedges, and green chutney as a drinking snack. It is also excellent stuffed into a paratha or rolled into a kathi roll with onions and mint chutney. For a complete spread, serve alongside [rasam](/recipes/rasam) and a vegetable poriyal for contrast.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store in an airtight container for up to 2 days. The tempering coating softens as it sits, but the flavor remains good.

**Reheating:** Spread on a baking sheet and reheat in an oven at 400°F (200°C) for 8 to 10 minutes to re-crisp. Alternatively, re-fry briefly in a hot wok with a teaspoon of oil and a few fresh curry leaves. Microwaving makes the coating soggy and is not recommended.

**Freezing:** Freeze the fried chicken (before the tempering step) in a single layer, then transfer to a freezer bag. Reheat from frozen in the oven and do the tadka fresh when serving. Freezes well for up to 2 months.
