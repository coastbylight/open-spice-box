# Ghurma Aloo

*Cumin-scented potatoes simmered with tomatoes, turmeric, and cayenne, a 30-minute one-pot curry of Iranian origin adapted through North Indian kitchens*

---

## Headnote

Ghurmas are thick-sauced stews of Iranian origin, spiked with dry herbs and thickened with vegetables. This one traveled through North Indian boarding school kitchens and became a weeknight staple, the kind of dish that feeds a table with almost nothing in the pantry. Potatoes, an onion, a tomato, cumin seeds, turmeric, and cayenne. That is the entire ingredient list, and it produces a curry that is deeply satisfying in a way that defies its simplicity.

The technique is straightforward: bloom cumin seeds in hot oil until they sizzle and turn reddish-brown, which takes about five seconds and fills the kitchen with a warm, earthy aroma. Potatoes and onion go in next and stir-fry until the edges start to brown. Salt, turmeric, and cayenne get stirred through. Then a cup of water goes in, the lid goes on, and the potatoes simmer for about twenty minutes until they are almost falling apart. The tomato and cilantro go in at the very end and cook for just two minutes, long enough to warm through but not so long that the tomato loses its freshness.

This is not a fancy dish. It is a dish that a cook of Iranian ancestry made for boarding school students who were hungry and needed something good. It works as a side alongside dal and rice, or as a main with roti and a squeeze of lemon. It is vegan, gluten-free, and costs almost nothing to make. The cumin does all the heavy lifting, and it does it well.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

- 680 g russet or Yukon Gold potatoes (1.5 lb), peeled and cut into 1/2-inch cubes
- 2 tbsp canola or vegetable oil
- 1 tbsp cumin seeds
- 1 small red onion, cut into 1/2-inch cubes
- 1 tsp ground turmeric
- 2 tsp coarse salt
- 1 tsp cayenne pepper
- 240 ml water (1 cup)
- 1 medium tomato, cored and cut into 1-inch cubes
- 2 tbsp fresh cilantro, finely chopped

---

## Method

1. Drain and dry the potatoes. If you submerged them in water to prevent browning, drain and pat dry thoroughly.

2. Bloom the cumin. Heat the oil in a medium saucepan over medium-high heat. Add the cumin seeds and cook until they sizzle, turn reddish-brown, and are fragrant, about 5 to 10 seconds.

3. Stir-fry. Add the potatoes, onion, and turmeric. Stir-fry until the potatoes and onion are lightly browned around the edges, 4 to 6 minutes.

4. Simmer. Add the salt and cayenne, stir once or twice. Pour in the water and bring to a boil. Reduce heat to medium-low, cover, and cook, stirring occasionally, until the potatoes are almost fall-apart tender, 18 to 20 minutes.

5. Finish. Stir in the tomato and cilantro. Cover and simmer for about 2 minutes until the tomato is warmed through. Serve.

---

## Why This Works

Blooming cumin seeds in hot oil is the foundational technique of North Indian cooking. The heat releases the volatile compounds in the seeds (thymol, cuminaldehyde) and infuses the oil, which then distributes that flavor through the entire dish. Cumin added later, as a ground powder, tastes flat by comparison.

The potatoes cook covered in a small amount of water, which means they partially steam and partially simmer. This produces potatoes that are tender all the way through with slightly broken edges that thicken the sauce naturally, without any added starch or cream.

Adding the tomato at the very end preserves its freshness and acidity. A tomato cooked for twenty minutes with the potatoes would dissolve into the sauce and lose its identity. Two minutes of warming is enough to soften it while keeping its bright, slightly tart contrast to the earthy cumin and potatoes.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Cumin seeds:** One of the most studied digestive spices. Cumin stimulates the secretion of pancreatic enzymes, aiding digestion and nutrient absorption. In Ayurveda, cumin is classified as a deepana (digestive stimulant) and carminative.

**Turmeric:** Provides curcumin, an anti-inflammatory compound. The oil in this recipe aids absorption of the fat-soluble curcumin.

**Potatoes:** High in potassium, vitamin C, and resistant starch (especially when cooked and cooled). The turmeric and cumin turn a simple starch into something nutritionally more interesting.

---

## Substitutions and Variations

**Sweet potatoes:** Work well with the same technique. Reduce cooking time to 12 to 15 minutes.

**More heat:** Increase cayenne to 1 1/2 teaspoons, or add a slit green chili with the onion.

**With peas:** Add 1/2 cup of frozen peas with the tomato for color and sweetness.

**With paneer:** Cube and pan-fry 200g of paneer and add with the tomato for a more substantial dish.

---

## Serving Suggestions

Serve as a side with dal, rice, and a cooling raita. Or serve as a main with hot roti or paratha and a squeeze of lemon. It also works alongside [Palak Paneer](/recipes/palak-paneer) or [Chicken Achar](/recipes/chicken-achar) as part of a larger spread.

---

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store for up to 4 days. The flavors deepen overnight.

**Reheating:** Warm in a covered pan over medium-low heat with a splash of water.

**Freezing:** Freezes well for up to 2 months. Potatoes may soften slightly when thawed.
