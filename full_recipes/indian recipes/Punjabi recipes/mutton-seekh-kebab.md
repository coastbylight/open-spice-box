# Mutton Seekh Kebab

*Spiced minced mutton pressed onto skewers with raw onion, green chili, and fresh herbs, grilled over charcoal or cooked in a hot pan until charred and juicy*

---

## Headnote

Seekh kebabs are the reason people stand around charcoal grills in the cold outside kebab shops across Pakistan and northern India. The word seekh means skewer, and the technique is ancient: seasoned minced meat pressed by hand onto flat metal skewers, held over live coals, and turned until the outside chars and the inside stays pink and juicy. The fat in the meat bastes the kebab from within as it cooks, dripping into the coals and sending up smoke that flavors everything it touches.

The key to a good seekh kebab is the mince. It needs to be fatty, ideally from the shoulder or leg with about 20 percent fat, and it needs to be kneaded aggressively until it becomes sticky and cohesive enough to hold onto the skewer without falling off. This kneading develops the myosin proteins in the meat, which act as a natural binder. No egg, no breadcrumb, no flour. The meat holds itself together through technique alone.

The spice mix is restrained compared to a curry. Cumin, coriander, garam masala, and chili powder provide warmth and heat. Raw onion goes in finely chopped, squeezed dry, because wet onion will steam the kebab from inside and make it fall apart. Green chilies, fresh coriander, and fresh mint go in at the end and stay in rough pieces, providing bursts of freshness and heat in every bite. The mixture chills for at least an hour before shaping, which firms the fat and makes the kebabs easier to mold.

On a charcoal grill, seekh kebabs take about eight to ten minutes, turned every two minutes. In a pan or under a broiler, they take the same time but need oil and high heat to develop the char that makes them worth eating. Serve them straight off the skewer onto a piece of naan with raw onion rings, green chutney, and a squeeze of lemon. They do not need a plate. They barely need a table.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

- 500 g mutton or lamb mince (1.1 lb), from shoulder or leg, at least 20% fat
- 1 medium onion, very finely chopped and squeezed dry in a towel
- 2 green chilies, finely chopped (seeds in for heat, deseeded for mild)
- 3 tbsp fresh coriander (cilantro), finely chopped
- 2 tbsp fresh mint leaves, finely chopped
- 2 tsp ginger paste
- 2 tsp garlic paste
- 1 tsp cumin powder
- 1 tsp coriander powder
- 1 tsp Kashmiri red chili powder (or paprika for color with less heat)
- 1/2 tsp garam masala
- 1/4 tsp ground black pepper
- 1 1/4 tsp salt
- 1 tbsp vegetable oil or ghee (for pan-cooking)

---

## Method

1. Squeeze the onion. Place the finely chopped onion in a clean kitchen towel and wring out as much water as possible. This is not optional. Wet onion will release steam inside the kebab and cause it to break apart on the skewer.

2. Mix the mince. In a large bowl, combine the mince with the squeezed onion, green chilies, fresh coriander, mint, ginger paste, garlic paste, cumin, coriander powder, chili powder, garam masala, black pepper, and salt.

3. Knead aggressively. Work the mixture with your hands for 4 to 5 minutes, pressing, folding, and slapping the meat against the bowl. The mixture should become sticky, cohesive, and slightly tacky. This kneading develops myosin proteins that bind the meat naturally. No egg or breadcrumb needed.

4. Chill. Cover and refrigerate for at least 1 hour. This firms the fat and makes the kebabs much easier to shape.

5. Shape the kebabs. Wet your hands with water or oil. Take a portion of the mixture (about 60 to 70 grams), press it around a flat metal skewer, and shape into a long cylinder about 1 inch thick. If you do not have skewers, shape into logs or long patties. They will cook the same way in a pan.

6. Cook. For charcoal grill: cook over hot coals, turning every 2 minutes, for 8 to 10 minutes total until charred on the outside and just cooked through. For pan: heat 1 tablespoon of oil in a heavy skillet over high heat. Cook the kebabs, turning every 2 minutes, for about 8 to 10 minutes until deeply browned on all sides.

7. Serve immediately on naan or paratha with sliced raw onion, green chutney, lemon wedges, and a sprinkle of chaat masala.

## Why This Works

Kneading the mince develops myosin, a sticky protein that acts as a natural glue. This is the same principle behind sausage-making: mechanical working of salted meat creates a protein matrix that holds everything together without added binders. Five minutes of aggressive kneading is the minimum.

Squeezing the onion dry is the step most home cooks skip, and it is the number one reason seekh kebabs fall apart. Raw onion releases water as it heats, and that water turns to steam inside the kebab, creating pockets that weaken the structure. Dry onion provides flavor and texture without the structural sabotage.

The fat content of the mince matters. Lean mince produces dry, crumbly kebabs. The fat melts during cooking, basting the meat from inside, and the rendered fat hitting the hot coals or pan creates the smoky char that defines the dish.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Mutton/Lamb:** Rich in iron, zinc, B12, and complete protein. The fat in shoulder and leg cuts contains conjugated linoleic acid (CLA), which has been studied for potential metabolic benefits. In Ayurveda, mutton is considered warming and nourishing, traditionally recommended during winter and for building strength.

**Cumin:** A foundational digestive spice in both Ayurveda and Unani medicine. Cumin seeds stimulate the secretion of digestive enzymes and have been studied for effects on bloating and gas relief.

**Green chilies:** Contain capsaicin with documented anti-inflammatory and metabolic properties. In Unani medicine, green chilies are considered heating and are used to stimulate appetite and circulation.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Beef seekh kebabs:** Use beef mince with at least 20% fat. The kneading and technique are identical. Common in Pakistani street food.

**Chicken seekh kebabs:** Use chicken thigh mince (not breast, which is too lean). Add 1 tablespoon of cream cheese or malai to help bind the leaner mixture.

**Oven method:** Shape kebabs on a wire rack over a baking sheet. Broil on high, about 4 inches from the element, for 4 to 5 minutes per side.

**Without skewers:** Shape into long oval patties or sausage shapes. Pan-fry in a cast iron skillet. The result is essentially the same, just a different shape.

**Chaat masala finish:** A sprinkle of chaat masala over the finished kebabs adds a tangy, sulfurous complexity that is traditional street food style.

## Serving Suggestions

The classic serving is on a piece of hot naan or inside a paratha roll with sliced raw onion, green chutney (coriander-mint), lemon wedges, and a dusting of chaat masala. For a full kebab spread, serve alongside [Chicken Achar](/recipes/chicken-achar), raita, and a simple salad of sliced cucumber, tomato, and onion with lemon juice and salt. Seekh kebabs also work as part of a mixed grill platter with tandoori chicken and chapli kebabs.

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store cooked kebabs for up to 3 days. The uncooked mixture can be shaped and refrigerated for up to 24 hours before cooking.

**Reheating:** Reheat under a hot broiler for 2 to 3 minutes per side, or in a dry hot skillet. Avoid the microwave, which makes them rubbery.

**Freezing:** The raw mixture freezes well for up to 2 months. Shape into kebabs before freezing. Thaw overnight and cook from refrigerator temperature. Cooked kebabs can also be frozen and reheated under a broiler.
