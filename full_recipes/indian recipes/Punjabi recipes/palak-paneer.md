# Palak Paneer

*A generous amount of spinach pressure-cooked with ginger, pureed, and simmered with a cumin-coriander base, loaded with ghee-fried paneer and finished with kasuri methi*

---

## Headnote

There are two kinds of palak paneer. There is the restaurant kind, which is a mild, creamy, pale green sauce with a few cubes of paneer floating in it, and there is the home kind, which is a thick, dark, intensely spinach-forward dish where the paneer has been fried in ghee until golden and the gravy tastes like spinach that has actually been cooked by someone who wanted to eat spinach.

This is the home kind. It uses a generous amount of spinach, four to five large bundles, which is roughly twice what most recipes call for. The spinach is pressure-cooked with salt and ginger paste, which is the older North Indian method that predates the blanching technique popularized by food blogs. Pressure cooking produces a deeper, darker green and a more concentrated spinach flavor. It does not produce the bright, vivid green of restaurant versions, and it does not try to. The color is honest. The flavor is the point.

The base is simple: cumin seeds crackled in ghee, chopped onions and tomatoes cooked down and mashed together on low heat until they become a paste, with a little ginger and coriander powder. The blended spinach goes in and the two simmer together until they become one thing rather than two. The paneer is cut into big chunks, salted and dusted with chili powder, and shallow-fried in ghee until brown on the edges. It goes into the spinach at the end. Kasuri methi, the dried fenugreek leaves that are the quiet signature of North Indian cooking, gets crushed between your palms and stirred in after the heat is off. No cream. Some people add cream. This recipe does not, because the ghee provides all the richness the dish needs, and the spinach tastes more like itself without dairy diluting it.

## Ingredients {#ingredients}

### For the spinach
- 600 to 750 g fresh spinach (4 to 5 large bundles, about 1.5 lb), washed thoroughly and roughly chopped
- 1 tbsp ginger paste
- 1/2 tsp salt
- 60 ml water (about a quarter cup)

### For the base
- 2 tbsp ghee
- 1/2 tsp cumin seeds
- 1 large onion, finely chopped (about 150 g)
- 2 medium tomatoes, chopped (about 200 g)
- 1 tbsp ginger paste
- 1/2 tsp coriander powder
- 1/4 tsp turmeric
- Salt to taste

### For the paneer
- 300 g paneer (about 10 oz), cut into big chunks
- 1 to 2 tbsp ghee (for frying)
- Pinch of salt
- Pinch of red chili powder

### For finishing
- 1 tsp kasuri methi (dried fenugreek leaves), crushed between palms
- 1/2 tsp garam masala

---

## Method

1. Pressure cook the spinach. Place the washed spinach in a pressure cooker with the ginger paste, salt, and water. The spinach will wilt down dramatically. Close the lid and cook on medium heat until the pressure builds, then cook for 1 whistle. Turn off the heat and let the pressure release naturally. Do not rush this. Open the lid and let the spinach cool until it is comfortable to handle, about 15 minutes.

2. Blend the spinach. Transfer the cooked spinach along with its liquid to a blender. Puree until smooth. Set aside. The consistency should be thick, not watery. If it seems thin, it will reduce in the pan.

3. Cook the base. Heat 2 tablespoons of ghee in a wok or kadai over low heat. Add the cumin seeds and let them crackle for about 15 seconds. Add the chopped onions and cook on low heat until soft and translucent, about 5 minutes. Add the ginger paste and stir for a minute. Add the chopped tomatoes, coriander powder, turmeric, and salt. Cover and cook on low heat until the tomatoes break down completely and the mixture becomes a soft paste, about 8 to 10 minutes. Use the back of a spoon or a masher to press and mash everything together in the wok. The oil should start to separate from the paste.

4. Combine the spinach and base. Add the blended spinach puree to the wok. Stir well to mix the spinach into the onion-tomato base. Let it simmer on a low flame, stirring occasionally, for 8 to 10 minutes. The gravy will darken and thicken as the spinach and base meld together.

5. Fry the paneer. While the spinach simmers, heat 1 to 2 tablespoons of ghee in a separate pan over medium heat. Sprinkle the paneer chunks with salt and a pinch of chili powder. Place them in the hot ghee and cook, turning occasionally, until golden brown on the edges, about 3 to 4 minutes. Do not move them constantly. Let each side develop color before turning.

6. Finish the dish. Add the fried paneer to the spinach gravy and stir gently to coat without breaking the pieces. Turn off the heat. Crush the kasuri methi between your palms and sprinkle it over the dish. Add the garam masala and stir once. Cover the wok and let it sit for 5 minutes before serving. The kasuri methi will bloom in the residual heat.

## Why This Works

Pressure cooking the spinach with ginger paste infuses the ginger flavor directly into the spinach at a level that surface-level sauteing cannot match. The high-pressure environment also breaks down the spinach cell walls more completely than blanching, producing a smoother, more homogeneous puree with a deeper, more concentrated spinach flavor. The trade-off is color: pressure-cooked spinach is darker than blanched spinach. For this recipe, that is a feature, not a flaw. The dark green signals a dish that has been properly cooked, not one optimized for photographs.

Cooking the onion-tomato base on low heat and covered is the slow approach that builds the deepest flavor. High heat would brown the onions and give the base a sweet, caramelized character that does not suit palak paneer. Low heat softens everything gently and lets the tomatoes break down into the onions, creating a smooth, integrated paste rather than distinct chunks.

Frying the paneer in ghee rather than oil adds a richness that echoes the ghee in the base. The slight browning on the edges creates a thin crust that adds texture to the otherwise smooth dish and helps the paneer hold its shape in the gravy. Salting and dusting with chili powder before frying seasons the paneer independently, so each piece carries its own flavor rather than relying entirely on the gravy.

Kasuri methi added off-heat is the finishing move that elevates the dish. Dried fenugreek leaves have a sweet, hay-like, slightly bitter aroma that is the quiet signature of North Indian gravies. Crushing them between your palms releases the essential oils. Adding them off-heat preserves these volatile compounds. If you cook them for too long, the flavor dissipates.

## Ingredient & Health Notes

**Spinach:** An exceptionally nutrient-dense green, high in iron, vitamin K, vitamin A, folate, and magnesium. Spinach is also high in oxalates, which can bind to minerals and reduce their absorption. However, cooking (especially pressure cooking) significantly reduces oxalate content. Pairing spinach with dairy (paneer) provides calcium that binds to oxalates in the gut, potentially reducing their absorption into the body. In Ayurveda, spinach is classified as cooling and is used to balance pitta (heat). It is traditionally paired with warming spices like ginger and cumin to balance its cold qualities.

**Paneer:** Fresh, unaged cheese that is a primary protein source in vegetarian Indian cooking. Paneer provides about 18 grams of protein per 100 grams, along with calcium and B12. Unlike aged cheeses, paneer does not melt, which makes it ideal for frying and simmering in gravies. In Ayurveda, paneer is considered heavy and cooling, which is why it is almost always paired with warming spices.

**Ghee:** Clarified butter with the milk solids removed, giving it a high smoke point and a rich, nutty flavor. Ayurveda considers ghee one of the most sattvic (pure) foods, used to kindle digestive fire without aggravating pitta. Modern nutritional research has identified ghee as a source of butyric acid, a short-chain fatty acid that supports gut health and has anti-inflammatory properties. The fat in ghee also aids absorption of fat-soluble vitamins in the spinach.

**Kasuri methi (dried fenugreek leaves):** The finishing spice that professional Indian cooks consider essential for palak paneer, butter chicken, and many dal preparations. The leaves have a sweet, hay-like aroma that is entirely different from fenugreek seeds. They are always added at the end and crushed before adding to release their oils.

## Substitutions and Variations

**Blanching method (for brighter color):** If you prefer the vivid green of restaurant palak paneer, blanch the spinach in boiling water for 2 minutes, then immediately transfer to ice water. Drain and blend. Skip the pressure cooker. The color will be brighter but the spinach flavor will be milder.

**With cream:** For a richer, restaurant-style version, add 2 to 3 tablespoons of cream after adding the spinach puree to the base. Stir it in and simmer gently. This produces a lighter green color and a creamier texture.

**Vegan version:** Replace paneer with extra-firm tofu, pressed and cubed. Replace ghee with coconut oil. The technique stays the same.

**Mixed greens:** Replace half the spinach with mustard greens (sarson) for a palak-sarson hybrid. The mustard greens add a peppery bite that pairs beautifully with the paneer.

**Without pressure cooker:** Boil the spinach in a covered pot with the ginger paste, salt, and water for 10 to 15 minutes until completely soft. Drain, cool, and blend.

## Serving Suggestions

Palak paneer is a main dish that wants bread. Hot roti, naan, or paratha are the traditional companions, used to scoop up the thick spinach gravy and the chunks of paneer. For a complete North Indian meal, serve alongside steamed basmati rice, a dal (yellow moong dal is a good pairing), and a simple raita or kachumber salad of chopped onion, tomato, and cucumber with lemon juice. The richness of the ghee-fried paneer and the earthiness of the spinach need something plain and something fresh on the table to balance them.

---

## Storage and Reheating

**Refrigerator:** Store in an airtight container for up to 3 days. The spinach may darken further, which is normal. The flavor deepens overnight.

**Reheating:** Warm in a covered pan over medium-low heat, adding a splash of water if the gravy has thickened. Stir gently to avoid breaking the paneer. The spinach reheats beautifully and many prefer it the next day.

**Freezing:** The spinach gravy freezes well for up to 2 months. Freeze without the paneer if possible (paneer texture changes slightly when frozen). Add freshly fried paneer when reheating for the best result.
