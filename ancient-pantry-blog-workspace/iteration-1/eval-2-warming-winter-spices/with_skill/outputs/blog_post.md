There is a particular quality to cold-weather cooking — a gravitational pull toward dishes that feel substantial, fragrant, and deeply satisfying in a way that a summer salad never quite achieves. Part of this is instinct. Part of it is something both [Ayurveda](/traditions/ayurveda) and [traditional Chinese medicine](/traditions/traditional-chinese-medicine) have articulated for over two thousand years: that certain spices do not merely flavor food. They change how the body receives it.

The concept of warming foods is one of the most coherent threads running through two otherwise very different traditional systems. In cold months, when the body expends energy maintaining its temperature and digestion can slow, warming spices serve a specific function — one that these traditions mapped with remarkable precision long before modern science had the tools to describe the underlying mechanisms.

## What "Warming" Actually Means

Before getting into specific spices, it's worth being precise about the term. In both Ayurveda and TCM, "warming" is not metaphor — it refers to a measurable quality a food has on the body's internal environment.

In Ayurveda, this quality is called *ushna virya* (hot potency). Spices with hot potency are understood to stimulate *agni*, the digestive fire, increasing the capacity to break down and absorb nutrients. During winter, Ayurveda considers the digestive fire to be naturally stronger (the body draws inward to conserve heat), which is why heavier, more richly spiced foods are prescribed for cold months rather than avoided.

In TCM, warming foods are said to tonify *yang* energy and dispel cold from the body's interior. The clinical concept is *wenzhong sanhan* — warming the middle and dispersing cold — and it sits at the center of how TCM approaches winter health. Foods and spices are classified along a spectrum from cold to neutral to warm to hot, and this classification guides how they're used therapeutically.

The two systems arrive at similar lists through different frameworks. What follows are the spices both traditions agree on — the ones that have been put to work in cold-weather kitchens across Asia for millennia.

## The Core Four: Ginger, Cinnamon, Cardamom, Cloves

### Ginger

[Ginger](/ingredients/ginger) sits at the top of the warming spice hierarchy in both systems. In TCM, fresh ginger (*shengjiang*) is classified as acrid and warm, and is used to warm the stomach, dispel exterior cold, and support lung function — the organ associated with autumn and early winter. Dried ginger (*ganjiang*) is considered hotter still, with a deeper warming action that reaches the spleen and kidney systems.

Ayurveda calls ginger *vishwabheshaja*, or "universal medicine," and considers it the most important warming agent in the kitchen. Its ability to kindle *agni* is considered unmatched among common spices.

Modern research offers some support for the traditional picture: ginger contains gingerols and shogaols (the latter more concentrated in dried ginger) that have been studied for their effects on thermogenesis, circulation, and nausea. Ginger consumption has also been associated with reduced markers of inflammation in several clinical studies, though this remains an active area of research.

In the kitchen: use fresh ginger freely in stir-fries, soups, and braises. Use dried ginger more deliberately — in spice blends, warming teas, and slow-cooked dishes where you want depth rather than brightness. A mug of hot water with fresh ginger slices and a squeeze of lemon remains one of the most useful things you can drink on a cold morning.

### Cinnamon

[Cinnamon](/ingredients/cinnamon) is the warming spice with the longest reach — from Ayurvedic *trikatu* blends to Chinese five-spice, from Persian rice dishes to Mexican chocolate. In TCM, cinnamon bark (*rougui*) is one of the most tonifying warming herbs, classified as acrid, sweet, and hot. It is used to warm the kidneys and spleen, and is specifically indicated for cold-limb presentations and fatigue in winter.

In Ayurveda, cinnamon (called *tvak*) is considered tridoshic when used in small amounts — meaning it balances all three constitutional types — which is relatively rare for a warming spice. Its warming action is considered gentler and more nourishing than ginger, making it appropriate for daily use.

Research suggests that cinnamon may support blood sugar regulation, with several studies finding associations between cinnamon consumption and improved insulin sensitivity. This is consistent with Ayurveda's use of cinnamon in breakfast preparations designed to sustain energy through a cold day — exactly what [cinnamon cardamom oats](/recipes/cinnamon-cardamom-oats) does.

In the kitchen: cinnamon works in both sweet and savory contexts. It is essential in the braising liquids of Kashmiri cooking, the slow-cooked lamb dishes of Central Asia, and the spiced rice preparations of South India. Don't reserve it for dessert.

### Cardamom

[Cardamom](/ingredients/cardamom) occupies a specific niche in the warming spice world: it warms without overheating. In Ayurveda, cardamom is considered warming but not excessively *ushna*, which makes it useful for people who tend to run hot or who find ginger or black pepper too stimulating. It is particularly valued for its effect on the respiratory system, where it is used to clear congestion and warm the lungs.

In TCM, cardamom (*sharen*, though Chinese medicine typically uses *Amomum villosum* rather than the green cardamom of Ayurveda) is classified as warming to the middle energizer — the digestive system — and is used to move *qi* and resolve dampness. Winter, in TCM, is associated with the kidney system and with a tendency toward internal cold and fluid accumulation, making cardamom's drying, warming action particularly relevant.

Its flavor is complex — floral, camphoraceous, slightly eucalyptic — and it pairs beautifully with both milk-based preparations and meat. In Northern Indian cooking, black cardamom (*badi elaichi*, also available as [black cardamom](/ingredients/black-cardamom) on the site) provides a smokier warming quality in braises and rice dishes, while green cardamom is the default for teas, sweets, and spiced milks.

### Cloves

[Cloves](/ingredients/cloves) are among the most intensely warming spices available. In Ayurveda, cloves (*lavanga*) are classified as strongly heating and drying — reserved for situations where you want significant warmth, such as treating congestion, strengthening digestion in genuinely cold or depleted conditions, or in rich, winter-appropriate preparations.

In TCM, cloves (*dingxiang*) warm the kidney and stomach yang, and are specifically used for coldness in the middle and lower body — cold stomach, cold limbs, lack of appetite in cold weather. They are paired with warming herbs like cinnamon and dried ginger in classical formulas.

The key with cloves is restraint. Their eugenol concentration is very high, which is why a single clove can dominate a pot. In cooking, they are most effective when used whole in braising liquids (where they can be fished out) or in tiny quantities in ground spice blends. A whole garam masala blend — cardamom, cinnamon, cloves, [black pepper](/ingredients/black-pepper) — is essentially a winter-spice delivery system in classical North Indian cooking.

## How to Use Warming Spices Through the Cold Months

The traditional approach is not to take warming spices as supplements — it is to cook with them regularly, in quantities that accumulate over a week of meals rather than arriving in a single dose.

A few practical patterns:

**Morning drinks.** The practice of starting cold mornings with a warming drink is common to both traditions. [Golden milk](/recipes/golden-milk) — turmeric, ginger, cinnamon, and black pepper in warm milk — hits nearly every warming spice at once. It is one of the most coherent expressions of Ayurvedic winter cooking translated into a modern recipe.

**Broth and congee.** In TCM, slow-cooked broths and congees are the primary vehicle for winter nourishment. [Spiced bone broth](/recipes/spiced-bone-broth) incorporates warming spices into a preparation that builds over hours, extracting deep flavor and allowing the warming compounds to meld. A bowl before bed on a cold night is exactly the kind of use these traditions had in mind.

**Spiced cooking fats.** The Ayurvedic technique of tempering (*tadka*) — blooming spices in hot ghee before adding them to a dish — is partly practical (fat-soluble flavor compounds release more fully into fat) and partly about activating the warming quality of the spices before they enter the body. This is not incidental. It is how warming spices were designed to be used.

**Balance with cooling and neutral foods.** Both traditions caution against excess. Warming spices support the body in cold weather; they are not a treatment for illness, and overconsumption of the hottest ones — cloves, dried ginger, long pepper — can cause imbalance. The traditional approach is a base of grains, legumes, and vegetables, seasoned generously with warming spices in proportion.

### FAQ

### Are warming spices the same as "hot" spices like chili?

Not exactly. Chili is considered hot in both Ayurveda and TCM, but its action is more dispersing and surface-level — it raises body temperature quickly and can cause sweating. The warming spices discussed here (ginger, cinnamon, cardamom, cloves) are considered more deeply tonifying, with an action that penetrates to the digestive and circulatory systems rather than the surface.

### Can I use warming spices every day in winter?

Yes, in culinary quantities — this is how both traditions recommend them. Ginger in cooking, cinnamon in breakfast dishes, cardamom in tea. Concentrated supplemental doses are a different matter and outside what Ancient Pantry covers.

### Do warming spices actually raise body temperature?

Research suggests some warming spices, particularly ginger, may support thermogenesis and circulation. In the sense that traditional medicine uses the term "warming" — improved digestive function, better circulation, reduced sensation of cold — there is biological plausibility behind the classification, though direct clinical evidence varies by spice.

### What is the difference between Ayurvedic and TCM warming spices?

The overlap is substantial. Both systems strongly feature ginger, cinnamon, black pepper, cloves, and cardamom. The main divergence is in emphasis: Ayurveda places ginger at the center of its warming framework, while TCM gives a similarly central role to cinnamon bark (*rougui*) and dried ginger (*ganjiang*). The underlying logic — that cold months require spices that support the digestive fire and internal warmth — is shared.
